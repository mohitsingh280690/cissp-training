2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Organizational Ownership

Legal department leads criminal and civil investigations. HR leads employee misconduct investigations. Security/IT provides technical evidence and digital forensics. Internal audit investigates fraud and policy violations. Outside counsel should be engaged early for privilege protection.

Business Impact

  • Evidence preservation failures can make cases unprosecutable and expose the organization to spoliation sanctions
  • Privacy violations during investigations can result in lawsuits from employees (especially in EU jurisdictions under GDPR)
  • Improper investigations can taint evidence and lead to wrongful termination lawsuits
  • Failure to investigate can demonstrate lack of due care, increasing liability

Failure Modes

The Enron scandal showed how improper handling of investigations and evidence destruction (Arthur Andersen's document shredding) can result in criminal obstruction charges and firm dissolution. In digital terms, overwriting logs, reimaging systems prematurely, or failing to preserve email can constitute spoliation.

3. Investigation Types

Investigation TypePurposeBurden of ProofLed ByOutcome
AdministrativeDetermine policy violations; internal matters; employee misconductPreponderance of evidence (more likely than not; >50%)HR, internal audit, managementDisciplinary action, termination, policy changes
CriminalDetermine whether a crime was committed; prosecute the offenderBeyond a reasonable doubt (highest standard; ~99%)Law enforcement (FBI, police, Secret Service)Arrest, prosecution, imprisonment, fines
CivilResolve disputes between parties (breach of contract, negligence, IP theft)Preponderance of evidence (>50%)Plaintiff's legal team; may involve private investigatorsMonetary damages, injunctions, settlements
RegulatoryDetermine compliance with regulations (HIPAA, PCI, SOX, GDPR)Varies by regulation; typically substantial evidence or preponderanceRegulatory body (OCR, FTC, SEC, PCI Council)Fines, sanctions, consent decrees, loss of certification
Burden of Proof Hierarchy (Low to High):
1. Preponderance of evidence — civil/administrative ("more likely than not"; >50%)
2. Clear and convincing evidence — some civil cases (substantially more likely)
3. Beyond a reasonable doubt — criminal cases (near certainty; ~99%)
⚠️ Exam Trap — Administrative vs Criminal: Many incidents start as administrative investigations. If criminal activity is discovered, the investigation may transition to a criminal investigation — but the organization should NOT try to conduct a criminal investigation itself. Contact law enforcement. Do NOT alert the suspect, as this could constitute tipping off and impede the criminal case.

Investigation Transitions

4. The Digital Investigation Process

Digital Investigation: A systematic process of identifying, preserving, collecting, examining, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence from computers, networks, mobile devices, and cloud services. The goal is to reconstruct events while maintaining evidence integrity and chain of custody.

Investigation Process Steps

StepDescriptionKey Actions
1. IdentificationRecognize that an incident occurred; determine scope and natureReview alerts, logs, reports; identify affected systems; assess whether investigation is warranted
2. PreservationProtect evidence from alteration or destructionIssue litigation hold; preserve logs; isolate affected systems; create forensic images BEFORE any analysis
3. CollectionGather evidence using forensically sound methodsFollow order of volatility; use write-blockers; hash all collected evidence; document everything
4. ExaminationProcess collected data to extract relevant informationFile carving; keyword searches; timeline reconstruction; artifact extraction
5. AnalysisInterpret examined data to draw conclusionsCorrelate events; establish timelines; determine cause; identify responsible parties
6. ReportingDocument findings in a clear, defensible formatWrite formal report; present to stakeholders; maintain for legal proceedings
Exam Emphasis: The FIRST action when an incident is suspected is always to PRESERVE evidence. Do not begin analysis on the original evidence — create a forensic duplicate first. Analysis on original evidence risks modification and may render it inadmissible.

5. Due Care & Due Diligence in Investigations

ConceptDefinitionInvestigation Application
Due Care"Doing the right thing" — taking reasonable steps to act responsiblyConducting investigations when incidents occur; preserving evidence; following established procedures; acting on findings
Due Diligence"Doing things right" — verifying that reasonable care is actually being appliedHaving investigation procedures BEFORE incidents; training investigators; auditing the investigation process; maintaining readiness
Legal Liability: Organizations that fail to investigate security incidents may be found negligent. Due care requires that you investigate. Due diligence requires that you have the capability to investigate properly BEFORE the incident happens. Together they demonstrate the organization acted as a "reasonable and prudent" entity.

Preparedness Measures (Due Diligence)

  • Establish and maintain an investigation policy and procedures
  • Train staff on evidence handling and chain of custody
  • Deploy monitoring and logging systems capable of capturing relevant evidence
  • Establish relationships with law enforcement BEFORE incidents occur
  • Retain legal counsel familiar with digital investigations
  • Include investigation authority in employment agreements and acceptable use policies

6. Interview & Interrogation

AttributeInterviewInterrogation
PurposeGather information from witnesses, users, and non-suspectsObtain an admission or confession from a suspect
ToneConversational; non-accusatory; information-seekingConfrontational; focused; accusatory (used carefully)
SubjectWitnesses, reporting parties, system administratorsSuspects or individuals believed to be responsible
Who ConductsSecurity team, HR, investigatorsTrained investigators, law enforcement, HR specialists
Legal ConsiderationsVoluntary participation; privacy protections; union representative rights (Weingarten rights in US)Miranda rights (if in custody/criminal); employee rights; legal counsel; avoid coercion
⚠️ Critical Legal Boundaries:
  • Weingarten Rights (US): Unionized employees have the right to have a union representative present during investigative interviews that may lead to discipline
  • Miranda Rights: Only required when a suspect is in custody AND being questioned by law enforcement. Corporate investigators are NOT law enforcement — Miranda generally does not apply to internal investigations, but coerced confessions may be inadmissible
  • EU/GDPR: Employee monitoring and investigation data collection must comply with data protection regulations; employees may have rights to access data collected about them

7. Reporting Investigation Results

Investigation Report Components

SectionContent
Executive SummaryHigh-level overview of the incident, investigation scope, key findings, and recommendations
BackgroundHow the incident was discovered; initial reports; authorization for investigation
Scope & MethodologySystems examined; time period; tools used; forensic procedures followed
Findings of FactObjective chronological presentation of evidence discovered — no opinions, only facts
AnalysisInterpretation of findings; conclusions about what occurred, how, and by whom
RecommendationsActions to prevent recurrence; policy changes; referral to law enforcement or legal
AppendicesChain of custody records; hash values; tool output; evidence inventory
Report Objectivity: Investigation reports must present FACTS, not opinions or speculation. Conclusions must be supported by documented evidence. Use language like "the evidence indicates" rather than "the suspect definitely did X." Reports may be used in court — they must withstand scrutiny.

8. Cooperation with Law Enforcement

When to Contact Law Enforcement

  • When criminal activity is suspected (fraud, data theft, unauthorized access, ransomware)
  • When regulatory requirements mandate reporting (HIPAA breach notification, certain financial crimes)
  • When the organization cannot investigate effectively on its own (nation-state attacks, sophisticated intrusions)
  • When preservation of evidence requires legal authority (warrants for third-party data)

Key Considerations

IssueGuidance
Loss of controlOnce law enforcement is involved, they control the criminal investigation; the organization may lose access to systems while evidence is collected
Publicity riskCriminal investigations may become public through court filings, press releases, or leaks — consider reputational impact
Evidence standardsLaw enforcement requires evidence collected to Fourth Amendment standards (no unreasonable search/seizure); private entities can search their own property but must not act as government agents
Parallel investigationsThe organization may continue its internal (administrative) investigation simultaneously with the criminal investigation — but must coordinate with law enforcement to avoid interfering
Enticement vs EntrapmentEnticement (legal): making resources available and monitoring usage (honeypots). Entrapment (illegal): inducing someone to commit a crime they would not otherwise commit. Only government agents can legally entrap — private entities engaging in enticement is generally permissible
⚠️ Enticement vs Entrapment: A honeypot is enticement — it passively attracts attackers. Entrapment requires coercing/inducing someone to commit a crime they would NOT have committed otherwise. Entrapment is a defense available ONLY against government action. Private entities cannot "entrap" in the legal sense — but they should still avoid inducing employee misconduct.

9. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Investigation Types — "ACCR"
Administrative (internal policy — lowest burden)
Civil (disputes — preponderance)
Criminal (crimes — beyond reasonable doubt — HIGHEST burden)
Regulatory (compliance — varies)
"All Clues Carry Risk"
🧠 Burden of Proof — "PCB" (like a circuit board)
Preponderance (>50%) → Clear & Convincing → Beyond Reasonable Doubt (~99%)
Lowest → Middle → Highest
🧠 Investigation Process — "IP-CEAR"
Identify → Preserve → Collect → Examine → Analyze → Report
"I Prefer Collecting Evidence And Reporting"
🧠 Due Care vs Due Diligence
Due Care = Carrying out the right actions (doing)
Due Diligence = Double-checking it's being done right (verifying)
Care = DO it. Diligence = CHECK it.
🧠 Enticement vs Entrapment
Enticement = "Come in if you want" (passive — honeypot = legal)
Entrapment = "I'll make you do it" (coercion — illegal for government)
