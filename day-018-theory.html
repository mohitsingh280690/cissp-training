2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Approximately 30–40% of data breaches involve insider actions (malicious or negligent). Personnel security policies are the governance layer that either enables or prevents these incidents. Without structured pre-employment screening, organisations hire individuals with undisclosed criminal histories; without offboarding procedures, ex-employees retain access for months or years after departure.

High-profile cases: Edward Snowden (insufficient need-to-know controls), numerous banking frauds (absent SoD), countless data thefts by employees exiting to competitors (missing DLP and access revocation). These failures are governance and policy failures, not just technical ones.

3. Pre-Employment Screening

Pre-employment screening reduces the risk of hiring individuals who pose threats to the organisation. Organisations have due diligence obligations to know who they employ — especially in sensitive roles.

Background Check Components

Check TypeWhat It VerifiesNotes
Criminal history checkPrior convictions; pending chargesMust comply with local laws (e.g., "Ban the Box" legislation in some US states); limited to relevant offences
Employment history verificationPrior employers, dates, roles, voluntary vs. involuntary departureSome employers provide only dates and job titles to avoid defamation liability
Education/credential verificationDegrees, certifications, professional licences claimedCertificate fraud is common; CISSP certification can be verified at ISC² website
Credit checkFinancial history, debt levels, bankruptciesRelevant for roles with financial authority; regulated in many jurisdictions (FCRA in US)
Reference checksCharacter, performance, reliability from known referencesLimited value — candidates select favourable references
Drug screeningCurrent substance useJurisdiction-dependent; regulated substance (e.g., cannabis) considerations vary
Social media screeningPublic online presence and behaviourLegal grey area; must be applied consistently to avoid discrimination claims
Key Concept — Ongoing Screening: Background checks at hire are point-in-time snapshots. Personnel security should include periodic re-screening (especially for privileged roles), monitoring for anomalous behaviour, and incident-triggered re-investigation. A clean background at hire doesn't guarantee clean behaviour throughout employment.

✅ Exam Tip: NDAs survive termination — they continue to bind former employees. NCAs have time-limited and geographic-limited restrictions. AUPs must be signed before any access is granted to establish informed consent for monitoring. These are frequently confused on the exam.

5. Onboarding and Offboarding

Onboarding Security Activities

  • Security awareness orientation and training
  • Signing of NDA, AUP, code of conduct
  • Access provisioning based on role and least privilege
  • Assignment of security classification clearance appropriate to role
  • Physical access provisioning (badge, building access)

Offboarding Security Activities

⚠️ Critical Sequence: When an employee exits (voluntary or involuntary), the FIRST security action is ALWAYS to disable/revoke all access (logical and physical). This must happen before or simultaneously with the HR exit meeting — not after.
  • Immediate: Disable all accounts (AD, email, VPN, cloud, SaaS, physical badges)
  • Immediate: Change passwords for shared accounts the employee knew
  • Before departure: Retrieve all organisation assets (laptop, badge, mobile devices, tokens)
  • Before departure: Conduct exit interview (security focused — what data did they access/copy recently?)
  • After departure: Review DLP logs, data transfers, and access history from the employee's last 30–90 days
  • After departure: Transfer knowledge — ensure critical processes don't depend on one now-departed employee
Key Concept — Hostile Terminations: For involuntary terminations (especially those involving legal disputes), access should be revoked before the employee is notified of termination. Allowing a hostile employee to know they're being terminated before access is revoked creates a window for data exfiltration, sabotage, or system destruction.

6. Separation of Duties (SoD)

Separation of Duties (SoD): The security principle that divides critical tasks among multiple individuals so that no single person can complete a sensitive transaction, commit fraud, or make a critical error without involvement of at least one other person. Also known as the "two-person rule" or "four-eyes principle" in high-security contexts.

SoD prevents both fraud and unintentional errors. Classic implementations:

  • No one can both initiate AND approve financial transactions above a threshold
  • No one can both write code AND approve it for production deployment (Dev ≠ Change Approver)
  • No one can both create user accounts AND assign them permissions
  • No one can both process payroll AND create/modify payroll records
⚠️ Collusion Risk: SoD prevents single-person fraud but cannot prevent collusion — where two or more people cooperate to circumvent controls. Audit trails, monitoring, and job rotation help detect collusion over time. No single control eliminates all insider fraud risk.

7. Job Rotation and Mandatory Vacations

Job Rotation: Systematically moving employees between roles or responsibilities. Serves both knowledge-sharing and fraud detection purposes — a rotating employee cannot conceal fraud indefinitely because successors will encounter any hidden manipulations.
Mandatory Vacation (Forced Leave): Requiring employees to take consecutive leave (typically 1–2 weeks) during which they cannot access systems. Designed to force the exposure of fraud schemes that only succeed if the perpetrator is present to manage them continuously.

These controls are used in banking, financial services, and high-security government contexts. The famous "rogue trader" cases (Nick Leeson at Barings Bank) often involved perpetrators who never took extended leave — the absence of mandatory vacation allowed sustained fraud concealment.

✅ Exam Tip: Mandatory vacation primarily detects fraud; job rotation both detects fraud AND builds organisational resilience by eliminating single points of knowledge failure. Both are compensating/detective controls. The CISSP exam sometimes offers both as answer options — choose based on whether the scenario emphasises detection (mandatory vacation) or knowledge resilience (job rotation).

8. Least Privilege and Need to Know

Least Privilege: Users, processes, and systems should be granted only the minimum access permissions necessary to perform their legitimate functions — no more, no less.
Need to Know: Access to specific information is granted only to individuals who require it to perform their specific authorised duties — even if they hold a clearance level that would permit access, they do not receive it without an explicit need.
ConceptFocusExample
Least PrivilegePermissions / capabilitiesA clerk gets read-only access to customer records, not write/delete access
Need to KnowInformation access controlAn analyst with Top Secret clearance cannot access a TS codeword program unless they have specific need for that program

Both principles work together: least privilege limits what people can DO; need to know limits what they can SEE. Together they implement the principle of minimum necessary access, reducing the blast radius of any insider breach.

9. Insider Threat

Insider threats are attacks or incidents caused by individuals with authorised access — employees, contractors, former employees, or business partners. They are among the most damaging and hardest to detect threat vectors because they already have legitimate access.

Insider Threat Categories

TypeDescriptionExample
Malicious insiderIntentional theft, sabotage, espionage, fraudEmployee exfiltrating customer data to sell to competitors before resigning
Negligent insiderUnintentional damage through poor security hygieneEmployee clicking phishing link; leaving sensitive data on public S3 bucket
Compromised insiderUnwitting agent — legitimate user whose credentials have been stolen by an external attackerAttacker uses stolen VPN credentials to operate as a legitimate employee

Personnel Controls That Mitigate Insider Threat

  • Separation of Duties — limits damage any single insider can cause
  • Least Privilege — minimises the data/systems an insider can access
  • Mandatory Vacation / Job Rotation — surfaces hidden fraud
  • User and Entity Behaviour Analytics (UEBA) — detects anomalous access patterns
  • DLP (Data Loss Prevention) — detects and blocks exfiltration attempts
  • Offboarding procedures — closes access promptly for departing staff

10. Vendor, Consultant & Contractor Controls

The CISSP exam outline (1.8) explicitly includes vendor, consultant, and contractor agreements and controls as part of personnel security policies. Third-party personnel represent a significant and often under-managed insider threat vector.

Why Third Parties Are a Personnel Security Risk

Contractors, consultants, and vendors often have privileged access to systems, data, or facilities — sometimes with less rigorous vetting or oversight than permanent employees. Supply chain attacks (e.g., SolarWinds) frequently exploit trusted third-party access.

Key Controls for Third-Party Personnel

ControlDescriptionExam Relevance
Background ChecksCriminal record, identity verification, reference checks — should be equivalent to checks on permanent employees for equivalent access levels. Some contracts (e.g., US federal) require specific clearance levels.Third parties with privileged access require the same (or stricter) vetting as employees in equivalent roles
Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)Legal agreement binding the contractor to confidentiality of company information. Must be signed before any access is granted.NDAs are preventive controls. Violating an NDA is a civil (not criminal) matter unless other laws apply.
Vendor Agreements (MSA / SOW)Master Service Agreement and Statement of Work define the scope of access, security requirements, right-to-audit clauses, and security obligations of the third party.Right-to-audit is critical — without it, you cannot verify the vendor's security posture
Least Privilege for Third PartiesContractors should receive only the minimum access required for their specific task. Time-limited accounts. No persistent access when not actively working.Privileged Access Management (PAM) applies to third parties; third-party accounts should expire automatically
Access Monitoring and LoggingAll third-party access should be logged and monitored. Privileged third-party sessions should be recorded (session recording). Alerts on after-hours or anomalous access.Monitoring third-party access is a detective control; key for insider threat detection
Onboarding/Offboarding for ContractorsFormal process to provision and de-provision access at contract start and end. Immediate revocation on contract termination — no grace period like employees may receive.Contractor accounts left active after project end = a significant and common vulnerability
Security Awareness TrainingThird parties who access company systems should complete the organisation's security awareness training before access is granted.Many breaches begin with contractors who receive less security training than employees
Escorted AccessFor physical access, contractors in sensitive areas should be escorted by employees. Unescorted access is a higher-risk option requiring additional physical security controls.Physical security control for contractors; relevant to facility security and clean desk policies

Contract Security Requirements

Essential Contract Clauses for Security:
  • Right-to-Audit: Organisation may audit the vendor's security practices, systems, or processes at any time with reasonable notice. Without this clause, you cannot independently verify vendor security claims.
  • Breach Notification: Vendor must notify the organisation within a specified timeframe (e.g., 72 hours) if they experience a breach that may affect the organisation's data.
  • Data Return/Destruction: Upon contract termination, vendor must return or certifiably destroy all organisation data.
  • Subcontractor Flow-Down: Security requirements must flow down to sub-contractors the vendor uses — prevents security erosion through tiered outsourcing.
  • Insurance Requirements: Vendor must carry adequate cyber liability insurance with the organisation named as an additional insured.
💡 Exam Tip — "Right-to-Audit" is a Critical Control: If an exam question asks what single contractual clause best provides ongoing assurance of a vendor's security posture, the answer is right-to-audit. SLAs measure performance; right-to-audit enables verification of security controls.
🪤 Exam Trap — Outsourcing and Liability: Outsourcing to a third party does NOT transfer legal liability. If a vendor mishandles your customers' PII and violates GDPR, the data controller (your organisation) remains liable. You can seek indemnification from the vendor through contract, but the regulatory fine falls on you. "You can outsource the work but not the accountability."

11. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

⚠️ Exam Trap — Offboarding Sequence: The CISSP exam may ask about the FIRST action when a key employee resigns or is terminated. The correct answer is REVOKE ACCESS (logical and physical) — not "notify the HR department," not "conduct an exit interview," not "retrieve equipment." Access revocation is first, always.
