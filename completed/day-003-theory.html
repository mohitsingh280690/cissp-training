1. Compliance vs. Regulation vs. Legislation

In the world of information security, organizations do not operate in a vacuum. They are subject to various rules imposed by governments, industry bodies, and business partners. It is crucial to understand the distinction between these terms.

Legislation (Laws): Rules created and passed by a government body (e.g., Congress, Parliament). They apply to everyone within that jurisdiction. Example: The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) was passed by the US Congress.
Regulation: Rules created by a government agency or regulatory body to enforce the legislation. They provide the specific details on how to comply with the law. Example: The Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) creates the specific rules for HIPAA compliance.
Compliance: The act of adhering to, and demonstrating adherence to, laws, regulations, industry standards, and internal policies. It is the organization's effort to ensure they are following the rules.
🔑 Key Concept: Legislation is the "What" (the law itself). Regulation is the "How" (the specific rules to follow the law). Compliance is the "Action" (the organization proving they are following the rules).

2. Key Industry Standards & Regulations

The CISSP exam expects you to be familiar with several major US and international regulations and standards. You don't need to know every single control, but you must know who they apply to and what they protect.

Standard / Law Applies To What it Protects / Focus
PCI-DSS
(Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)		 Any organization that processes, stores, or transmits credit card data. Cardholder data. It is a contractual standard created by major credit card companies (Visa, MasterCard), not a government law.
SOX
(Sarbanes-Oxley Act)		 Publicly traded companies in the US. Financial reporting accuracy. Requires executives to certify financial reports and mandates strict internal controls over financial data to prevent corporate fraud.
HIPAA
(Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)		 Healthcare providers, insurers, and their business associates in the US. Protected Health Information (PHI). Mandates strict security and privacy controls for patient medical records.
GLBA
(Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act)		 Financial institutions in the US (banks, insurance companies, brokerage firms). Consumer financial privacy. Requires financial institutions to explain their information-sharing practices and safeguard sensitive data.
GDPR
(General Data Protection Regulation)		 Any organization processing the data of EU residents, regardless of where the organization is located. Personal data privacy. Grants significant rights to individuals (right to be forgotten, right to access) and imposes heavy fines for non-compliance.
FISMA
(Federal Information Security Management Act)		 US Federal government agencies and their contractors. Requires federal agencies to develop, document, and implement an agency-wide program to provide information security.
💡 Exam Tip: Remember that PCI-DSS is NOT a law. It is an industry standard enforced through contracts with credit card brands. If you violate it, you get fined by the card brands or lose the ability to process cards, but you don't go to jail. SOX, HIPAA, and GLBA are actual laws.

3. Contractual, Legal, and Regulatory Requirements

Organizations must navigate a complex web of requirements that dictate how they handle data and secure their systems. These requirements come from three main sources:

  • Legal Requirements: Mandated by laws passed by legislative bodies (e.g., SOX, HIPAA). Non-compliance can result in civil or criminal penalties.
  • Regulatory Requirements: Mandated by government agencies overseeing specific industries (e.g., SEC for finance, FCC for communications). Non-compliance results in fines or loss of operating licenses.
  • Contractual Requirements: Mandated by agreements between businesses. Examples include PCI-DSS, Service Level Agreements (SLAs), and Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs). Non-compliance results in breach of contract lawsuits or financial penalties.

Trans-border Data Flow

A major compliance challenge today is trans-border data flow—moving data across national boundaries. Different countries have different privacy laws. For example, moving data from the EU to the US requires specific legal mechanisms (like Standard Contractual Clauses) because the EU considers US privacy protections to be generally weaker than GDPR.

4. Compliance Monitoring and Reporting

Compliance is not a one-time event; it is a continuous process. Organizations must constantly monitor their systems and report on their compliance status.

Continuous Monitoring

Organizations use automated tools to continuously monitor their security posture against compliance baselines. This involves:

  • Vulnerability scanning
  • Log analysis and SIEM (Security Information and Event Management)
  • Configuration management (ensuring systems don't drift from secure baselines)

Audits and Assessments

To prove compliance, organizations undergo audits:

  • Internal Audits: Conducted by the organization's own staff to prepare for external audits and find issues early.
  • External Audits: Conducted by independent third parties (e.g., a CPA firm for SOX, or a QSA for PCI-DSS) to provide an objective assessment of compliance.
⚠️ Warning: Security does not equal compliance, and compliance does not equal security. You can be 100% compliant with a standard like PCI-DSS and still suffer a data breach. Compliance is the minimum baseline; security is the actual protection of assets.

5. Key Takeaways

  • Legislation is the law; Regulation is the rule enforcing the law; Compliance is following the rules.
  • PCI-DSS protects credit card data and is a contractual standard, not a law.
  • SOX protects financial reporting for publicly traded companies.
  • HIPAA protects health information (PHI).
  • GLBA protects consumer financial information.
  • Compliance is continuous and requires ongoing monitoring, internal audits, and external audits.
  • Compliance ≠ Security. Compliance is just the baseline.
