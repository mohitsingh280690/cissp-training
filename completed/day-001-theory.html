1. What is CISSP?

The Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP) is a globally recognized certification in information security, administered by (ISC)² — the International Information System Security Certification Consortium.

Definition: CISSP validates a professional's ability to design, implement, and manage a best-in-class cybersecurity program. It is often called the "gold standard" of security certifications.

Requirements to Earn CISSP

  • Experience: 5 years of cumulative, paid, full-time work experience in 2 or more of the 8 domains. A 4-year college degree or approved credential waives 1 year.
  • Exam: Pass the CISSP examination
  • Endorsement: Must be endorsed by an existing (ISC)² certified professional within 9 months of passing
  • Ethics: Agree to the (ISC)² Code of Professional Ethics
  • CPE: Maintain 40 CPE credits per year (120 over 3 years)
💡 Exam Tip: Even if you don't have 5 years of experience, you can pass the exam and become an Associate of (ISC)², then earn the full certification when your experience requirement is met (within 6 years).

2. Exam Format & CAT Overview

Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT)

The CISSP exam uses Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) in English. This means the exam adapts to your ability level in real-time.

ParameterDetail
Number of Questions125 – 175 (CAT adjusts)
Minimum Questions125 (you must answer at least 125)
Question TypesMultiple choice + Advanced innovative (drag & drop, hotspot)
Time Limit4 hours
Passing Score700 out of 1000
Languages (CAT)English
Languages (Linear)French, German, Brazilian Portuguese, Spanish, Japanese, Simplified Chinese, Korean

How CAT Works

  1. Every candidate starts with a question of moderate difficulty
  2. If you answer correctly, the next question is harder
  3. If you answer incorrectly, the next question is easier
  4. The algorithm continuously estimates your ability level
  5. The exam ends when the algorithm is 95% confident you are above or below the passing standard, or at 175 questions
🔑 Key Concept: Finishing at 125 questions does NOT mean you passed or failed — it means the algorithm was confident enough after 125 questions. Many people pass at 125, and many pass at 175. Don't panic based on question count.

Study Strategies

  • Understand, don't memorize: CAT tests depth of understanding, not rote recall
  • Think like a manager/CISO: Choose the "best" or "most important" answer, not just a correct one
  • Protect life first: In any scenario question, human safety always comes first
  • Read every word: Questions are intentionally worded to test nuance
  • Eliminate then choose: Use process of elimination
  • No going back: In CAT format, you cannot return to previous questions

4. The CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is the most fundamental concept in information security. It represents the three core goals that every security program must achieve:

Confidentiality /\ / \ / \ / CIA \ / Triad \ / \ /____________\ Integrity Availability

Confidentiality

Definition: Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to access it. Preventing unauthorized disclosure of data.

Confidentiality means keeping secrets secret. It's about preventing unauthorized access to sensitive information.

Controls that support Confidentiality:

  • Encryption — Transforms readable data into unreadable ciphertext (AES, RSA, TLS)
  • Access Controls — Permissions, ACLs, role-based access
  • Authentication — Verifying identity before granting access (passwords, MFA, biometrics)
  • Data Classification — Labeling data so the right controls are applied
  • Physical Security — Locks, guards, mantraps to prevent physical access
  • Network Security — Firewalls, VPNs, network segmentation
  • Steganography — Hiding the existence of data within other data

Real-world examples of Confidentiality breaches:

  • A hacker steals customer credit card numbers from a database
  • An employee sends sensitive documents to their personal email
  • An unencrypted laptop containing PII is stolen
  • Shoulder surfing to read someone's password

Integrity

Definition: Ensuring that information is accurate, complete, and has not been tampered with. Data should only be modified by authorized entities in authorized ways.

Integrity ensures that data is trustworthy. If someone modifies data without authorization — or if data is corrupted accidentally — integrity has been compromised.

Controls that support Integrity:

  • Hashing — Produces a fixed-size fingerprint (SHA-256, MD5) to verify data hasn't changed
  • Digital Signatures — Combines hashing with asymmetric encryption to prove origin and integrity
  • Access Controls — Preventing unauthorized modifications
  • Version Control — Tracking changes and enabling rollback
  • Input Validation — Ensuring data entered into systems meets expected format
  • Checksums & CRCs — Detecting accidental data corruption
  • Database Constraints — Referential integrity, triggers, stored procedures

Real-world examples of Integrity breaches:

  • A MITM attack modifies data in transit between two systems
  • A virus modifies system files
  • An unauthorized user changes financial records
  • A software bug corrupts a database

Availability

Definition: Ensuring that information and systems are accessible to authorized users when needed. Systems must be reliable, timely, and perform as expected.

Availability means systems are up and running when they're needed. Downtime — whether caused by attacks, hardware failures, or natural disasters — is a threat to availability.

Controls that support Availability:

  • Redundancy — RAID, failover clusters, backup power (UPS, generators)
  • Backups — Regular, tested backups (full, incremental, differential)
  • Load Balancing — Distributing traffic across multiple servers
  • Fault Tolerance — Systems designed to continue operating despite component failure
  • DDoS Protection — CDNs, rate limiting, scrubbing centers
  • Disaster Recovery — Hot/warm/cold sites, DR plans
  • SLAs — Contracts guaranteeing uptime (99.9%, 99.99%)
  • Patch Management — Preventing system crashes from unpatched vulnerabilities

Real-world examples of Availability breaches:

  • A DDoS attack takes down an e-commerce website during Black Friday
  • Ransomware encrypts all files, making them inaccessible
  • A power outage shuts down a data center without backup power
  • A misconfigured firewall blocks legitimate traffic
🔑 Key Concept: The three elements of the CIA Triad can sometimes conflict. For example, very strong encryption (confidentiality) may slow down performance (availability). A security professional must balance all three based on organizational needs and risk appetite.

5. The DAD Triad

The DAD Triad is the opposite of the CIA Triad. It represents the three threats that the CIA Triad aims to defend against:

CIA GoalDAD ThreatDescription
ConfidentialityDisclosureUnauthorized access to or release of sensitive information
IntegrityAlterationUnauthorized modification of data
AvailabilityDestruction (or Denial)Making systems or data unavailable or destroying them
How to remember: CIA is what we want — DAD is what we're fighting against. Every security control exists to prevent one or more elements of DAD from affecting CIA.

Examples of DAD in Action

  • Disclosure: An attacker exfiltrates a database of customer SSNs → violates Confidentiality
  • Alteration: Malware modifies log files to cover its tracks → violates Integrity
  • Destruction: Ransomware encrypts all files and deletes backups → violates Availability

6. CIA vs. DAD — The Relationship

Understanding the relationship between these two triads is fundamental to every security decision:

┌──────────────────┐ OPPOSES ┌──────────────────┐ │ CIA TRIAD │ ◄──────────────────► │ DAD TRIAD │ │ (Security Goals) │ │ (Threat Model) │ ├──────────────────┤ ├──────────────────┤ │ Confidentiality │ ◄─────────────────► │ Disclosure │ │ Integrity │ ◄─────────────────► │ Alteration │ │ Availability │ ◄─────────────────► │ Destruction │ └──────────────────┘ └──────────────────┘ Security Controls bridge the gap: Encryption ──► Prevents Disclosure ──► Protects Confidentiality Hashing ──► Prevents Alteration ──► Protects Integrity Redundancy ──► Prevents Destruction ─► Protects Availability
💡 Exam Tip: When answering scenario questions, first identify which element of CIA is being threatened (which means identifying the DAD threat). This helps you pick the right control. Example: "Data was modified in transit" → Alteration → Integrity threatened → Choose hashing/digital signatures.

7. Defense in Depth

Definition: Defense in Depth (also called layered security) is a security strategy that uses multiple, overlapping layers of controls to protect information assets. If one layer fails, the next layer provides protection.

The concept comes from military strategy — castles had moats, walls, towers, and guards. Each layer made it harder for an attacker to reach the core. In cybersecurity, we apply the same principle.

The Layers of Defense in Depth

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ POLICIES & PROCEDURES │ ← Governance │ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │ │ PHYSICAL SECURITY │ │ ← Guards, locks, fences │ │ ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │ │ │ │ PERIMETER SECURITY │ │ │ ← Firewalls, DMZ │ │ │ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ NETWORK SECURITY │ │ │ │ ← IDS/IPS, segmentation │ │ │ │ ┌─────────────────────┐ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ HOST SECURITY │ │ │ │ │ ← Antivirus, patching │ │ │ │ │ ┌─────────────┐ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ APPLICATION │ │ │ │ │ │ ← Input validation, WAF │ │ │ │ │ │ ┌───────┐ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ DATA │ │ │ │ │ │ │ ← Encryption, DLP │ │ │ │ │ │ └───────┘ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ └─────────────┘ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ └─────────────────────┘ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ └─────────────────────────────┘ │ │ │ │ │ └─────────────────────────────────────┘ │ │ │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘ │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Why Defense in Depth Matters

  • No single control is perfect — every control can be bypassed, fail, or be misconfigured
  • Reduces single points of failure — multiple layers mean multiple chances to stop an attack
  • Increases attacker cost — each layer the attacker must bypass costs time, effort, and increases detection risk
  • Covers different attack vectors — physical, network, application, and social attacks all need different defenses

Controls are also classified by their function:

🔑 Key Concept: A well-designed security program uses controls from all categories (preventive, detective, corrective, etc.) AND all types (administrative, technical, physical) at every layer. This multi-dimensional approach is the essence of Defense in Depth.

8. Thinking Like a Manager

This is perhaps the most important concept for CISSP success. The exam does not test you as a technician — it tests you as a security leader.

The CISSP Mindset

  • Protect human life first — Always. In every scenario. Before data, before equipment, before reputation.
  • Think long-term, not tactical — Choose the answer that provides sustainable security, not a quick fix
  • Align with business objectives — Security exists to support the business, not to block it
  • Risk-based decisions — Not every risk needs to be eliminated. Some risks are accepted if the cost of mitigation exceeds the potential loss.
  • Due care vs. due diligence — Due care = doing the right thing (implementing controls). Due diligence = verifying through research and ongoing checking.
  • Choose the "BEST" answer — Multiple answers may be correct, but only one is the BEST. Think about what a CISO would choose.
⚠️ Common Mistake: Technical professionals often choose the most technical answer. On CISSP, the correct answer is often the most managerial one. "Develop a policy" or "perform a risk assessment" often beats "install a firewall" or "encrypt the data."

Decision Framework for Exam Questions

  1. Is life at risk? → Protect life first
  2. What is the PRIMARY concern? → Identify CIA element being threatened
  3. Who is responsible? → Think about roles (Data Owner, Custodian, CISO)
  4. What is the BEST long-term solution? → Policies and processes over tools
  5. Eliminate obviously wrong answers → Usually 2 answers are clearly wrong
  6. Between remaining 2, choose the managerial answer

9. Key Takeaways

  • CISSP is a management-level certification with 8 domains, requiring 5 years of experience
  • The exam uses CAT format (125-175 questions, 4 hours, 700/1000 to pass)
  • The CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) defines the three goals of security
  • The DAD Triad (Disclosure, Alteration, Destruction) defines the three threats opposing CIA
  • Defense in Depth uses multiple overlapping layers of controls — no single control is enough
  • Controls are categorized by function (preventive, detective, corrective, etc.) and type (administrative, technical, physical)
  • Think like a manager — protect life first, align with business, choose policies over tools
