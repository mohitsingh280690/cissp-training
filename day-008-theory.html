⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • Policies are the WHY — not the how. "The organisation will protect customer data" is a policy. Step-by-step backup instructions are a procedure.
  • ISO 27001 is certifiable; ISO 27002 is guidance/controls catalogue. You cannot be "ISO 27002 certified" — only ISO 27001.
  • NIST CSF is voluntary for the private sector but mandatory for US federal agencies under Executive Order 13800.

2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Why Policies Exist

Without documented policies, there is no baseline for what "good" security looks like, no accountability framework for enforcement, and no way to pass an audit. When a breach occurs, regulatory bodies and courts ask: Did the organisation have policies? Were they enforced? Having policies also defines the employment and disciplinary basis for action against violators.

The Policy-Reality Gap

Many organisations have beautifully written policies that nobody reads and nobody enforces. The CISSP manager mindset is that a policy without implementation, training, monitoring, and enforcement provides almost no security value — and may actually increase liability by demonstrating that the organisation knew what it should do but failed to do it.

Framework Adoption in Practice

  • NIST CSF: Dominant in US organisations, especially any regulated industry or government contractor
  • ISO 27001: Required or preferred in European markets, financial services, and many multinational contracts
  • COBIT: Used when alignment with IT governance and audit is paramount; common in large enterprises with internal audit functions
  • Many organisations use multiple frameworks simultaneously, mapping controls between them

3. Policy Hierarchy Overview

Security documentation follows a strict hierarchy from high-level strategic intent to operational specifics. Each level is more detailed and more frequently updated than the one above it.

┌─────────────────────────────────────┐ │ POLICY │ ← WHY (strategic intent, management directive) │ "What" must be done │ Signed by senior management / board └──────────────┬──────────────────────┘ Rarely changes (years) │ ┌──────────────▼──────────────────────┐ │ STANDARD │ ← WHAT specifically (measurable requirements) │ "This is the minimum bar" │ AES-256 for encryption, 12-char passwords └──────────────┬──────────────────────┘ Changes as technology changes │ ┌──────────────▼──────────────────────┐ │ PROCEDURE │ ← HOW step-by-step (operational instructions) │ "Follow these steps exactly" │ How to provision an account, how to respond └──────────────┬──────────────────────┘ Changes frequently │ ┌──────────────▼──────────────────────┐ │ BASELINE │ ← MINIMUM configuration (hardened defaults) │ "This is the minimum config" │ CIS Benchmark settings for Windows Server └──────────────┬──────────────────────┘ System/platform-specific │ ┌──────────────▼──────────────────────┐ │ GUIDELINE │ ← RECOMMENDATIONS (optional best practices) │ "You should consider this" │ Non-mandatory suggestions; flexible └─────────────────────────────────────┘

Detailed Definitions

DocumentMandatory?Written ByExample
PolicyYes — compliance requiredSenior management / Board"All customer data must be encrypted at rest and in transit."
StandardYes — sets the specific requirement baselineCISO / Security team"Encryption of customer data must use AES-256 for stored data and TLS 1.2+ for transmitted data."
ProcedureYes — must be followed for covered activitiesOperations / IT / SecurityStep-by-step guide for encrypting a new database; steps for onboarding a new user with encrypted drives
BaselineYes — for applicable systemsSecurity / Systems teamsCIS Benchmark for Windows Server hardening configuration; minimum patch level requirements
GuidelineNo — advisory onlySecurity / IT"It is recommended to use a password manager to generate and store complex passwords."
🧠 Policy Hierarchy — "Please Send Procedures Before Guidelines":
Policy → Standard → Procedure → Baseline → Guideline
Or the old standby: "Policies, Standards, Procedures, Baselines, Guidelines"
Mandatory: Policy, Standard, Procedure, Baseline | Optional: Guideline

4. Policy Types

Policies can be further classified by their purpose and audience:

Policy TypePurposeWho It's ForExample
Regulatory PolicyEnsures compliance with laws and regulations; non-compliance has legal consequencesEntire organisationHIPAA compliance policy; PCI-DSS policy; GDPR data protection policy
Advisory PolicyStrongly recommends actions; consequences for non-compliance are outlined but policy is not legally mandatedEmployeesAcceptable Use Policy (AUP); clean desk policy; social media policy
Informative PolicyInforms and educates; no direct compliance requirement; shares organisation's stance or positionInternal and/or publicPrivacy policy (public-facing); IT security awareness notices; "what to do if you suspect a breach" guidance
💡 Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Among the most important advisory policies. Defines authorised use of organisational systems. Employees must sign it, it defines monitoring rights, and it is the basis for disciplinary action if systems are misused. Without a signed AUP, organisations have very limited ability to take action against employees for improper computer use.

5. Policy Document Anatomy

A well-formed policy document typically contains:

  1. Purpose: Why this policy exists and what problem it addresses
  2. Scope: Who and what it applies to (all employees? contractors? specific systems?)
  3. Policy Statements: The actual requirements — clear "shall" or "must" language
  4. Exceptions: How to request exceptions and who approves them
  5. Roles and Responsibilities: Who owns what under the policy
  6. Definitions: Key terms used in the document
  7. Enforcement and Consequences: What happens if the policy is violated
  8. Version History and Review Cycle: When was it last reviewed; who approved it

6. NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

Developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in response to a 2013 US Executive Order on critical infrastructure cybersecurity. Version 2.0 released in 2024 added the "Govern" function.

CSF 2.0 – Six Core Functions

FunctionPurposeKey Activities
GOVERNEstablish context, expectations, and accountability [new in 2.0]Organisational context; risk management strategy; supply chain risk; roles & responsibilities; policies
IDENTIFYUnderstand assets, risks, and gapsAsset management; business environment; governance; risk assessment; supply chain risk
PROTECTSafeguards to limit impactAccess control; awareness training; data security; maintenance; protective technology
DETECTIdentify occurrences of cybersecurity eventsAnomalies & events; continuous monitoring; detection processes
RESPONDActions taken after detecting an incidentResponse planning; communications; analysis; mitigation; improvements
RECOVERRestore capabilities after an incidentRecovery planning; improvements; communications
🧠 NIST CSF 2.0 Functions: "GI-PD-RR"
Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Memory hook: "Good Investigators Protect Detective Results Reliably"
CSF 1.1 (original): IPDRR; CSF 2.0 adds Govern at the start

CSF Three Implementation Tiers

TierNameCharacteristics
1PartialInformal, reactive processes; limited risk awareness; no formal program
2Risk InformedManagement-approved practices; risk-informed but not org-wide
3RepeatableFormal, organisation-wide processes; regularly reviewed and updated
4AdaptiveContinuously learning; adapts based on lessons learned and threat intelligence
NIST CSF Is Not a Checklist: The CSF is a risk-based framework — it's designed to help organisations understand their current cybersecurity state, define their target state, and prioritise improvements. It does not prescribe specific controls. For specific control guidance, use NIST SP 800-53. For critical infrastructure, supplement with NIST SP 800-82 (ICS security).

7. ISO 27001 & ISO 27002

ISO 27001 — The Certifiable Standard

ISO/IEC 27001 defines requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Information Security Management System (ISMS). It is structured around Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) continual improvement.

  • Organisations can be certified to ISO 27001 (third-party audit)
  • Certification demonstrates to customers, partners, and regulators that a formal ISMS is in place
  • Covers 11 clauses — Clauses 4-10 are mandatory requirements; Annex A contains 93 controls (2022 version)
  • Annex A controls are grouped into 4 themes: Organisational, People, Physical, Technological

ISO 27002 — The Controls Catalogue

ISO/IEC 27002 is the supporting code of practice — it provides detailed guidance on implementing the 93 controls referenced in ISO 27001 Annex A. It is guidance, not a certifiable standard. Think of it as the "how-to" companion to ISO 27001's "what".

⚠️ ISO 27001 vs ISO 27002:
ISO 27001 = Requirements (SHALL statements) = Certifiable
ISO 27002 = Guidelines (SHOULD statements) = Not certifiable
"We are ISO 27002 certified" is an incorrect statement — no such certification exists.

ISMS Lifecycle (PDCA)

PhaseActivities
PlanDefine ISMS scope; risk assessment; risk treatment plan; select controls; set objectives
DoImplement controls; training; procedures; Annex A controls
CheckMonitor and review; internal audits; management reviews; measure effectiveness
ActTake corrective and preventive action; improve the ISMS

8. COBIT

COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies): A framework for the governance and management of enterprise IT, developed by ISACA. Current version: COBIT 2019. Focus: aligning IT with business objectives and ensuring effective governance.

COBIT vs. NIST vs. ISO

AttributeCOBITNIST CSFISO 27001
Primary FocusIT governance and business alignmentCybersecurity risk managementInformation security management system
Created ByISACANIST (US government)ISO/IEC
Certifiable?No (individuals can be certified)NoYes (organisational certification)
Primary AudienceIT management, internal audit, governanceSecurity teams, risk managersSecurity managers, compliance teams
Typical UseIT governance alignment with business strategyCybersecurity programme structureFormal ISMS certification

COBIT 2019 Six Principles

  1. Provide Stakeholder Value
  2. Holistic Approach
  3. Dynamic Governance System
  4. Governance Distinct from Management
  5. Tailored to Enterprise Needs
  6. End-to-End Governance System

9. Comparing Frameworks

ScenarioBest FrameworkReason
US healthcare company needs to structure its cybersecurity programmeNIST CSF + HIPAA controlsNIST CSF provides structure; HIPAA provides specific requirements
Company wants to get certified to demonstrate security to European clientsISO 27001Internationally recognised, certifiable
Organisation wants to align security with business strategy and improve IT governanceCOBITDesigned specifically for IT governance and business alignment
US federal agency must comply with a security mandateNIST (RMF + SP 800-53)FISMA requires NIST frameworks for federal agencies
Organisation wants to assess and improve its security maturity levelNIST CSF (Implementation Tiers) or CMMITiers provide maturity model; CMMI for maturity across dimensions
💡 NIST SP 800-53: While the CSF provides the framework structure, NIST SP 800-53 provides the detailed security and privacy controls catalogue. It is the most comprehensive controls reference published by NIST and is mandatory for US federal information systems. The CSF maps to 800-53 controls.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Policy Hierarchy: "Policies Should Probably Be Good"
Policies (WHY — strategic, mandatory)
Standards (WHAT — specific requirements, mandatory)
Procedures (HOW — step-by-step, mandatory)
Baselines (MINIMUM config — platform-specific, mandatory)
Guidelines (SUGGESTIONS — optional best practices)
Only guidelines are optional!
🧠 NIST CSF 2.0 Functions: "GIPDRR"
Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Think: "Generally Intellectual People Don't Resist Recovery"
Govern is NEW in v2.0!
🧠 ISO 27001 vs ISO 27002:
27001 ends in 1 = 1 stop certifiable standard
27002 ends in 2 = 2nd document (the "how-to" guidance)
27001 = "ISMS Requirements" = requirements = CERTIFIABLE
27002 = "Code of Practice" = guidance = NOT certifiable
🧠 Framework Selection Memory Aid:
NIST CSF = US risk-based structure (any industry)
ISO 27001 = International certification (European preference)
COBIT = IT Governance alignment (board/management/audit)
Think: "NIST for Structure, ISO for Cert, COBIT for Governance"
⚠️ Policies Must Be Enforced:
On the exam, "issue a policy" is never the complete answer. Policies must be: communicated, trained, monitored, audited, and enforced. A policy without enforcement is worse than no policy — it demonstrates knowing failure to act.
