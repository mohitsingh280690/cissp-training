2. Real-World Relevance

Security operations is where policy meets practice. You can have the best security architecture and the most comprehensive policies, but without effective day-to-day operations — monitoring, incident response, access management, and physical security — security is just paper.

Key Operational Challenges

  • Insider threats: Employees with excessive privileges cause more damage than external attackers in many breaches
  • Privilege creep: Access accumulates over time as employees change roles without losing old permissions
  • Alert fatigue: SOC analysts overwhelmed by false positives miss real attacks
  • Operational continuity: Balancing security controls with business availability

3. Need-to-Know & Least Privilege

Need-to-Know: A user should only be granted access to information that is necessary for performing their specific job duties. Even if a user has the appropriate security clearance, they should NOT access information unless they have a demonstrated need for that specific information.
Least Privilege: A user, program, or process should be granted only the minimum privileges (permissions, rights, access) necessary to perform their authorized tasks — nothing more. This applies to ALL accounts: user accounts, service accounts, administrator accounts.

Need-to-Know vs Least Privilege

AttributeNeed-to-KnowLeast Privilege
FocusInformation access (WHAT data can you see?)System permissions (WHAT can you do?)
OriginMilitary/intelligence (clearance ≠ access)Computer science / access control
ExampleAn HR manager with Top Secret clearance cannot access military operations data — no need to knowA developer should not have admin access to production servers — only needs read access to logs
EnforcementClassification systems; compartmentalization; data labelingRBAC; permission assignment; access reviews; PAM tools

Implementing Least Privilege

  • Default deny: Start with zero access; add permissions as needed (whitelist approach)
  • Privilege creep prevention: When employees change roles, revoke old permissions before granting new ones
  • Regular access reviews: Quarterly or semi-annual review of all access rights (Day 96 — access review completion rate metric)
  • Time-limited access: Temporary elevated privileges that expire automatically (just-in-time access)
  • Separate accounts: Administrators should have separate admin accounts (not use their daily accounts for admin tasks)
Exam Trap: Need-to-know and least privilege are related but NOT identical. Need-to-know is about INFORMATION access (data classification). Least privilege is about SYSTEM permissions (what operations can you perform). A user can have clearance (authorization level) without need-to-know (specific data access).

4. Separation of Duties (SoD)

Separation of Duties (SoD): No single individual should have sufficient authority to complete a critical task alone. Critical functions are divided among multiple people so that no one person can commit fraud, cause harm, or make unauthorized changes without detection.

SoD Examples

Critical FunctionSeparation Applied
Financial transactionsPerson who initiates payment ≠ person who approves payment ≠ person who reconciles accounts
Code deploymentDevelopers write code; separate team reviews code; different team deploys to production
Access provisioningManager requests access; separate approval authority grants it; security team audits
Backup & restorePerson who performs backups ≠ person who can restore data (prevents unauthorized data recovery)
Security monitoringSystem administrators ≠ security auditors (admins shouldn't review their own logs)

Two-Person Control (Dual Control)

Two-person control (also called dual control or two-man rule) is a stricter form of SoD where two individuals must act together simultaneously to complete a critical action. Neither person alone can complete the task.

  • Nuclear launch codes require two officers turning keys simultaneously
  • Bank vault access requires two key holders present
  • Encryption key ceremonies require multiple key custodians
  • M-of-N key splitting: M out of N custodians must combine their key fragments to reconstruct the master key
SEPARATION OF DUTIES vs TWO-PERSON CONTROL Separation of Duties: Person A: Initiates → Person B: Approves → Person C: Executes (Sequential — different people at different steps) Two-Person Control (Dual Control): Person A + Person B: Both act SIMULTANEOUSLY (Concurrent — both must be present at the same time) Split Knowledge: Person A knows Part 1 of the password Person B knows Part 2 of the password Neither knows the full password alone
⚠️ Collusion: SoD controls can be defeated by collusion — when two or more individuals conspire together. SoD doesn't prevent collusion; it makes fraud DETECTABLE (audit trails) and HARDER (requires conspiracy of multiple people). Combine SoD with monitoring, logging, and periodic audits.

5. Job Rotation & Mandatory Vacations

Job Rotation

Job Rotation: Periodically moving employees between different roles or responsibilities. Serves as both a security control (detecting fraud) and a business continuity control (cross-training ensures no single point of failure).
BenefitSecurity PurposeBusiness Purpose
Fraud detectionNew person in the role may discover irregularities the previous person was hiding—
Reduces single point of failure—Multiple people can perform each critical role; continuity during absences
Prevents collusion buildupPeriodic rotation disrupts long-term collusive relationships—
Cross-training—Employees develop broader skills and organizational understanding

Mandatory Vacations

Mandatory Vacations: Requiring employees (especially those in sensitive positions like finance, IT admin) to take a minimum consecutive period of vacation (typically 1-2 weeks). During their absence, another employee performs their duties and may discover fraud or irregularities.
Why Consecutive Days Matter: Many financial fraud schemes require daily attention to maintain (e.g., manually adjusting ledgers, intercepting audit reports). If the perpetrator is away for an extended continuous period, the scheme may unravel. Weekend-only absences are insufficient — the employee must be away long enough for the replacement to discover anomalies.
Exam Fact: Mandatory vacations are a DETECTIVE control — they help DETECT fraud, not prevent it. Job rotation is both detective (discovers hidden fraud) and preventive (makes it harder to establish long-term schemes).

6. Privileged Account Management (PAM)

Privileged Accounts: Accounts with elevated access rights beyond normal users — root/administrator accounts, service accounts, emergency accounts, application accounts with database admin access. These accounts are the #1 target for attackers because they provide the widest access to systems and data.

Types of Privileged Accounts

Account TypeDescriptionRisk Level
Local AdministratorBuilt-in admin account on individual systems (local root, Administrator)High — one compromised system
Domain AdministratorActive Directory domain admin; full control of all domain-joined systemsCritical — all systems in the domain
Service AccountsAccounts used by applications/services to access resources (often run with elevated privileges)High — frequently have non-expiring passwords; often overlooked in access reviews
Emergency/Break-Glass AccountsAccounts for emergency access when normal authentication failsCritical — must be heavily monitored; usage triggers alerts
Application AccountsDatabase admin accounts, API keys with administrative scopeHigh — may have direct access to sensitive data

PAM Controls

ControlDescription
Password VaultingStore privileged credentials in an encrypted vault; check out for use; automatically rotated after each use
Just-in-Time (JIT) AccessGrant elevated privileges only when needed; automatically expire after a defined period (e.g., 4 hours)
Session RecordingRecord all privileged sessions (keystroke logging, screen recording) for forensic review
Privilege Escalation ApprovalRequire manager or security team approval before granting temporary admin access
Credential RotationAutomatically change passwords after each use or on a regular schedule (daily/weekly)
Break-Glass ProceduresEmergency access process: sealed credentials; usage triggers immediate alert; reviewed within 24 hours

PAM Tools

CyberArk, BeyondTrust, Thycotic (now Delinea), HashiCorp Vault, AWS IAM + STS for cloud.

⚠️ Service Account Risk: Service accounts are among the most dangerous privileged accounts because they often have: non-expiring passwords, no MFA, broad permissions, and no human watching their activity. They are frequently compromised in breaches (SolarWinds, Kaseya). Treat service accounts with the same rigor as admin accounts.

7. Service Level Agreements (SLAs)

Service Level Agreement (SLA): A formal agreement between a service provider and customer that defines measurable service expectations — uptime guarantees, response times, performance targets, and remedies for non-compliance. SLAs should include security requirements alongside performance requirements.

SLA Components

ComponentDescriptionExample
Availability/UptimeGuaranteed system availability percentage99.99% uptime (52.6 min downtime/year)
Response TimeMaximum time to acknowledge an incidentCritical: 15 min; High: 1 hour; Medium: 4 hours
Resolution TimeMaximum time to resolve an incidentCritical: 4 hours; High: 8 hours; Medium: 24 hours
Security RequirementsEncryption, patching, vulnerability management, incident notificationNotify customer of security breach within 72 hours
Penalties/RemediesConsequences for failing to meet SLA targetsService credits: 10% credit per hour of unplanned downtime
ReportingRegular performance reportsMonthly uptime report; quarterly security posture review

The "Nines" of Availability

Availability %Annual DowntimeMonthly Downtime
99% (two nines)3.65 days7.31 hours
99.9% (three nines)8.77 hours43.83 minutes
99.99% (four nines)52.60 minutes4.38 minutes
99.999% (five nines)5.26 minutes26.3 seconds
Exam Tip: SLAs define WHAT is expected. OLAs (Operational Level Agreements) define HOW internal teams will support the SLA internally. An SLA with a customer may promise 99.99% uptime; the OLA between the network team and server team defines each team's responsibilities to achieve that.

8. SOC Structure & Tiers

Security Operations Center (SOC): A centralized team (and often a physical facility) responsible for monitoring, detecting, analyzing, and responding to security incidents in real-time. The SOC is the operational nerve center of an organization's security program.

9. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Need-to-Know vs Least Privilege
Need-to-Know = "What DATA can I see?" (information access)
Least Privilege = "What can I DO?" (system permissions)
Both say "only what's necessary" — but one is about seeing, the other about doing
🧠 SoD Purpose — "No Lone Wolf"
No single person should be able to complete a critical task alone. If one person can initiate, approve, AND execute — there's no SoD.
🧠 Mandatory Vacations — "Vacation = Verification"
The vacation isn't for the employee's benefit (though it helps) — it's for the ORGANIZATION to verify what happens when someone else does the job. Consecutive days required.
🧠 PAM Controls — "VJSPC"
Vault (password vaulting)
JIT (just-in-time access)
Session recording
Privilege escalation approval
Credential rotation
"Very Justified Security Protects Credentials"
🧠 SOC Tiers — "T-I-H-M"
Tier 1: Triage (sort alerts)
Tier 2: Investigate (dig deeper)
Tier 3: Hunt (proactive threat hunting)
Manager: Metrics & management
🧠 The Nines
Each additional "9" reduces downtime by 10×:
99% = ~3.65 days/year
99.9% = ~8.77 hours/year
99.99% = ~52.6 min/year
99.999% = ~5.26 min/year
