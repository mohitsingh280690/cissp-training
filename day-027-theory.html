2. Real-World Relevance

Major organisations have paid millions in sanctions for destroying data after a legal hold obligation arose. Simultaneously, organisations that retain data indefinitely "just in case" expose themselves to greater liability in litigation discovery — the more data you retain, the more that can be used against you. Proper retention schedules manage both risks.

3. Retention Policies and Schedules

Retention Policy: An organisational policy specifying how long each category of data must be retained before it can be destroyed. Based on regulatory requirements, legal obligations, contractual requirements, and operational business needs.

Drivers of Retention Requirements

  • Regulatory: Laws and regulations impose minimum retention periods for specific record types (SOX for financial records, HIPAA for medical records, PCI-DSS for payment data)
  • Legal: Litigation, investigations, and government inquiries may require records to be preserved beyond normal retention periods
  • Contractual: Customer or partner contracts may specify retention requirements for data generated under the contract
  • Operational: Business need to access historical records for reference, trend analysis, or auditing

Records Lifecycle States

  • Active records: Currently being used in daily operations; stored in operational systems
  • Inactive records: No longer actively used but must be retained; moved to archive storage
  • Archival records: Long-term preservation (vital records, historical records); may be retained permanently
  • Vital records programme: Identifies and protects records essential to continuing organisational operations after a disaster (corporate charter, key contracts, financial statements, regulatory licences)

5. Litigation Hold / Legal Hold

Litigation Hold (Legal Hold): A suspension of normal data destruction and retention schedule activities, triggered when an organisation reasonably anticipates litigation, regulatory investigation, or other legal proceedings that will require production of data as evidence.

Key Points

  • The obligation arises when litigation is reasonably anticipated — not when it formally begins. A threatening letter from a lawyer can trigger a hold.
  • Once a hold is in place, any data within the hold's scope must be preserved — even if the normal retention schedule says destroy it
  • Hold must cover all relevant data locations: email servers, backup tapes, cloud storage, mobile devices, employee personal devices used for work
  • HR, Legal, IT, and Records Management must all coordinate on hold execution

Spoliation

⚠️ Spoliation: The intentional or negligent destruction, alteration, or withholding of evidence that is subject to a legal hold. Penalties include: adverse inference jury instructions (jury told to assume destroyed evidence was unfavourable), monetary sanctions, dismissal of claims, default judgement against the spoliating party, and potential criminal obstruction of justice charges.

6. Data Destruction Methods

Logical Destruction Methods

  • Overwriting (DoD 5220.22-M / Gutmann method): Writing known patterns (e.g., all zeros, all ones, random data) over existing data multiple times. Suitable for magnetic HDDs and SSDs (though overwriting SSDs is less reliable due to wear leveling — see Day 30). The DoD 5220.22-M 3-pass method and the Gutmann 35-pass method are well-known standards.
  • Cryptographic erasure: Destroying the encryption keys that protect encrypted data. If the data is encrypted with AES-256 and the key is securely destroyed, the encrypted data is permanently unreadable — effectively destroyed without physically destroying the media. Ideal for cloud storage and SSDs where physical overwriting is impractical.
  • Factory reset: For mobile devices — uses device's built-in cryptographic erasure (modern Android and iOS encrypt storage; factory reset destroys keys). Less reliable on older devices without full device encryption.

Physical Destruction Methods

⚠️ Degaussing Limitation: Degaussing ONLY works on magnetic media. It has absolutely no effect on SSDs, USB flash drives, or optical discs (CDs/DVDs). A degaussed SSD may still contain all its data intact. This is a heavily tested CISSP exam point.

7. Certificate of Destruction

Certificate of Destruction: A formal written document that provides evidence that a specific asset was destroyed by an authorised method on a specific date. It is the accountability record for the Destroy stage of the data lifecycle.

A certificate of destruction should contain:

  • Asset description (type of media, make/model, serial number)
  • Data classification of the asset
  • Destruction method used (degaussing, shredding, incineration, etc.)
  • Date and location of destruction
  • Name and signature of the authorised person who performed or witnessed destruction
  • Name of the destruction vendor (if third-party)

Organisations must require certificates of destruction from third-party media disposal companies. These certificates provide proof of HIPAA, PCI-DSS, and GDPR compliance for data destruction obligations.

8. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Regulatory Retention Numbers:
SOX = 7 years ("Seven-OX")
HIPAA = 6 years ("HIPAA has 5 letters, +1 = 6")
PCI audit logs = 1 year (3 months hot / 9 months warm)
Remember: "SOX=7, HIPAA=6, PCI=1"
🧠 Legal Hold Trigger:
"Anticipated = Activated" — The hold activates when litigation is reasonably anticipated, not when the lawsuit is filed. Destroying data after the anticipation point = Spoliation.
🧠 Degaussing Limitation:
"Degaussing only DEMAGNETISES" — It only affects magnetic media (spinning HDDs, tapes).
SSD = flash memory = no magnetism → Degaussing does NOTHING to an SSD.
