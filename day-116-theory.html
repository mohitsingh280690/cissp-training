2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Workplace safety incidents (fires, natural disasters, active threats) test an organization's preparedness. Legal liability for employee safety is significant — OSHA requirements, duty of care obligations, and workers' compensation all mandate workplace safety programs. International operations face additional challenges: political instability, travel risks, kidnapping threats, and varying safety standards across jurisdictions.

3. Safety of People — The #1 Priority

The CISSP Golden Rule: In ANY scenario — fire, disaster, security breach, or emergency — the SAFETY OF HUMAN LIFE is ALWAYS the first and highest priority. No data, system, or business operation is worth a human life.

Priority order: People → Assets → Business Operations → Data
Exam Application: Whenever an exam question asks "What is the FIRST thing to do?" during any emergency scenario, look for the answer that prioritizes people. Examples:
• Fire in the data center → evacuate people (not save servers or activate suppression first)
• Building flooding → ensure personnel safety (not protect equipment)
• Active shooter → protect lives (not lock down data)
• Toxic chemical spill → evacuate (not contain the spill)

4. Emergency Management

Prevention / MitigationRisk assessment; building codes compliance; fire suppression systems; environmental controls; training; drills
PreparednessEmergency plans; evacuation routes; assembly points; emergency supplies; communication systems; first aid kits; AED locations
ResponseActivate emergency plan; evacuate; notify emergency services; account for all personnel; provide first aid; activate incident command
RecoveryDamage assessment; facility restoration; return to normal operations; counseling/support for affected personnel; lessons learned

Emergency Response Team (ERT)

  • Floor wardens / fire marshals: Assigned per floor; responsible for ensuring complete evacuation of their area
  • First aid responders: Trained employees who provide immediate medical assistance
  • Incident commander: On-scene decision authority; coordinates with emergency services
  • Communications coordinator: Manages internal/external communications during emergency

6. Duress & Coercion Controls

Duress: A situation where a person is forced to act against their will under threat (e.g., armed robbery, hostage situation, coercion to open a vault). Duress controls are safeguards that allow a person under coercion to secretly signal for help.
ControlHow It Works
Duress alarm / panic buttonHidden button at reception/guard desk that silently alerts security or law enforcement without the intruder knowing
Duress code / safe wordA special code or word that, when used in communication, signals coercion. Example: "code blue" during a phone call means "I am under duress"
Duress PINA special PIN that opens the door/safe but simultaneously triggers a silent alarm. Appears to comply with attacker's demands while alerting security
Two-person integrity (TPI)Requires two authorized people to complete a sensitive action; one person under duress cannot act alone
Duress Alarms Are SILENT: The entire point is that the attacker doesn't know the alarm has been triggered. A loud alarm during a robbery endangers the hostage/victim. Silent alarms notify security for a covert response.

7. Travel Security

RiskMitigation
Device theft / lossFull disk encryption; remote wipe capability; minimal data on travel devices; VPN for connectivity; don't leave devices unattended
Eavesdropping / surveillancePrivacy screens; avoid sensitive discussions in public; assume hotel Wi-Fi is compromised; use cellular or VPN
Border / customs searchesDevices may be searched at borders (legal in many jurisdictions); carry clean devices with access to cloud data only; know local laws
Physical safetyTravel advisories; check-in protocols; pre-travel threat briefings for high-risk destinations; emergency contacts
Social engineeringHeightened risk at conferences/trade shows; shoulder surfing; bogus Wi-Fi networks; social-engineering pretexts
Clean Travel Devices: For travel to high-risk countries, provide employees with clean/loaner devices that contain NO sensitive data. Access corporate resources via VPN/cloud. Upon return, devices are reimaged before reconnecting to the corporate network. This eliminates the risk of persistent malware installed during border inspections or hotel intrusions.

8. Workplace Violence Prevention

  • Zero-tolerance policy: Clear organizational policy against workplace violence; communicated to all employees
  • Threat assessment team: Cross-functional team (HR, security, legal, management) to evaluate reported threats
  • Behavioral indicators: Train managers to recognize warning signs — sudden behavior changes, verbal threats, obsessive behavior, substance abuse
  • Reporting mechanisms: Anonymous tip lines; clear escalation path; protection against retaliation for reporters
  • Active shooter response: Run / Hide / Fight training (in that order); practiced through drills
