1. Review Methodology

Mock Exam 2 was intentionally harder than Exam 1 — more scenarios, more "best answer" nuance, and more cross-domain integration. Use this review to:

  1. Compare scores: Did you improve from Exam 1 to Exam 2? Stagnation in a domain signals a fundamental gap
  2. Analyze wrong answers: For each wrong answer, determine WHY you chose incorrectly — was it a knowledge gap, a reading error, or a trap?
  3. Focus on the "why": Exam 2 rewards understanding principles, not memorizing facts
  4. Practice elimination: Even if you got the right answer, could you explain why each wrong answer is wrong?
💡 Exam Strategy: On the actual CISSP CAT exam, your goal is NOT to get every question right. It's to demonstrate consistent competence above the passing standard. Missing hard questions is expected — missing easy ones is what fails candidates.

3. Scenario Deconstruction Technique

Exam 2's scenario-heavy format mirrors the actual CISSP CAT. Use this technique for every scenario question:

The STAR Method for CISSP Scenarios

StepActionExample (Q31: Ransomware)
SituationIdentify the context and constraintsRansomware incident, CISO must decide
TaskWhat specific question is being asked?"PRIMARY consideration" for paying ransom
AnalyzeEvaluate each option against the taskWhich option addresses the primary decision factor?
RoleAnswer as a SENIOR MANAGER, not a technicianA manager considers recovery capability first
💡 Critical Rule: When a question asks for "FIRST," "PRIMARY," or "MOST important" — it's testing your ability to prioritize. ALL answers may be partially correct, but only ONE is the BEST first/primary action.

Priority Ladder (Always Apply This Order)

  1. Life safety — always first, always
  2. Contain/stop the bleeding — prevent further damage
  3. Assess/understand — risk assessment, scope determination
  4. Remediate/fix — address root cause
  5. Recover/restore — return to normal operations
  6. Learn/improve — lessons learned, process improvement

4. Domain 1 & 2 — Advanced Concepts

Governance vs. Management (D1)

Governance = WHAT and WHY (board/senior management) — sets direction, ensures alignment, oversight
Management = HOW and WHEN (CISO/security team) — implements, operates, monitors

Key Risk Concepts Often Confused

ConceptDefinitionExam Trap
Risk AppetiteAmount of risk the org is WILLING to accept (strategic)Set by BOARD, not CISO
Risk ToleranceAcceptable variation from appetite (tactical)Narrower than appetite
Residual RiskRisk remaining AFTER controls appliedMust be ≤ risk appetite
Inherent RiskRisk BEFORE any controlsTheoretical baseline

Data Roles Hierarchy (D2)

🧠 Remember: O-C-P-U
Owner → classifies, accountable (senior management)
Custodian → implements protections (IT/operations)
Processor → processes data on behalf of controller (GDPR term)
User → accesses data per policy (everyone)

Litigation Hold — Critical Concept

Q10 tested litigation hold — this appears frequently on the real exam:

  • Legal hold ALWAYS overrides retention policies
  • Triggered when litigation is reasonably anticipated (not just when filed)
  • Destroying data after hold = spoliation → severe legal sanctions
  • Must preserve ALL potentially relevant data including backups, emails, metadata

5. Domain 3 & 4 — Advanced Concepts

Zero Trust Architecture — Core Principles (D3)

  • Never trust, always verify — regardless of network location
  • Least privilege access — minimum necessary access
  • Assume breach — design as if already compromised
  • Micro-segmentation — granular network boundaries
  • Continuous verification — not one-time authentication

DNSSEC vs DoH vs DoT

DNSSEC: Authenticates DNS responses (integrity), does NOT encrypt
DoH (DNS over HTTPS): Encrypts DNS queries over HTTPS (port 443) — provides confidentiality
DoT (DNS over TLS): Encrypts DNS queries over TLS (port 853) — provides confidentiality

6. Domain 5 & 6 — Advanced Concepts

Separation of Duties vs. Dual Control (D5)

⚠️ Frequently Confused on Exams!
Separation of Duties: Different people handle different STEPS of a process (initiate → approve → execute)
Dual Control: Two or more people must act TOGETHER for the SAME step (both keys needed to open safe)
Q24 specifically tested this distinction — "two approvals for one action" = dual control

Kerberos Key Facts (D5)

  • Uses symmetric encryption
  • Relies on trusted third party: KDC (Key Distribution Center)
  • TGT (Ticket Granting Ticket) has a finite lifetime (typically 8-10 hours)
  • Requires synchronized clocks (±5 minutes tolerance)
  • Vulnerable to pass-the-ticket, golden ticket, and silver ticket attacks

Virtual Patching — Key Concept

Q50 tested this: When a patch exists but needs testing, virtual patching (WAF/IPS rules) provides immediate protection as a compensating control while the real patch is validated. This is the standard approach for critical vulnerabilities with CVSS ≥ 9.0.

9. Exam Traps: Distractors That Sound Correct

Trap 1: The Technical Answer vs. The Management Answer

Scenario: "After a data breach, the CISO should FIRST..."
❌ Trap: "Block the attacker's IP address" (technical/tactical)
✅ Correct: "Activate the incident response plan" (management/process)

Trap 2: "Immediately" Distractors

Rule: If an answer says "immediately" do something drastic, it's usually wrong.
❌ "Immediately fire the employee"
❌ "Immediately deploy the patch to production"
❌ "Immediately disconnect all systems"
✅ Exception: "Immediately evacuate" (life safety) is always correct

Trap 3: "Best" vs. "Only Correct"

CISSP tests BEST answers, not only-correct answers. Multiple options may be correct, but one is MOST correct because it:
• Addresses root cause (not symptoms)
• Is preventive (not reactive)
• Is strategic (not tactical)
• Considers the broadest scope
• Aligns with the question's specific qualifier (FIRST, PRIMARY, MOST)

Trap 4: Regulatory Compliance Conflicts

Q44 tested this directly: when multiple regulations conflict, implement the MORE restrictive requirement. This satisfies all applicable regulations. Never pick one regulation over another.
