Day 18 – Personnel Security Policies

MCQ Practice – Domain 1: Security and Risk Management

Question 1: An employee in the finance department is being terminated for embezzlement. In what order should the following actions occur? 1) Notify HR; 2) Revoke all logical and physical access; 3) Conduct exit interview; 4) Retrieve company assets.

✅ Correct: B. Revoking all access is the FIRST security action — especially in hostile or adversarial terminations. Allowing a terminated employee (especially one suspected of fraud) to retain access even briefly creates the opportunity for sabotage or exfiltration. Access is revoked before — or simultaneously with — the HR notification, then assets retrieved, then exit interview conducted.

Question 2: A bank teller processes customer transactions and also has the ability to create and modify customer account records. Which control principle is MOST directly violated?

✅ Correct: B. Separation of Duties (SoD) is violated when a single individual can both perform AND control a sensitive process end-to-end. A teller processing transactions AND managing account records could commit fraud without needing an accomplice. Least privilege is secondary — the deeper principle violated is SoD.

Question 3: Which personnel control is SPECIFICALLY designed to detect fraud schemes that require the perpetrator's continuous presence to remain undetected?

✅ Correct: C. Mandatory vacation forces the employee to be absent from their role for extended consecutive periods. Fraud schemes that require the perpetrator to actively manage or conceal them are exposed when someone else covers the role. This is why financial institutions require mandatory vacations — a trader who never takes vacation cannot be concealing unauthorised positions if forced to leave for two weeks.

Question 4: An employee signs an agreement at hire that restricts them from disclosing client lists or proprietary algorithms even after leaving the company. This is BEST described as:

✅ Correct: C. A Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) specifically prohibits disclosure of confidential information and survives the end of employment. An NCA restricts working for competitors (not disclosure per se). An AUP governs use of IT resources. The scenario describes a confidentiality obligation that persists post-departure — that is an NDA.

Question 5: A systems administrator is granted administrator rights on all servers organisation-wide even though their role only requires managing the web servers. Which principle is being violated?

✅ Correct: B. Least Privilege requires granting only the minimum access necessary for legitimate job functions. Granting organisation-wide admin rights to someone whose role only requires web server access violates least privilege. This admin has capabilities far beyond what the role requires.

Question 6: An analyst holds a Top Secret security clearance but is denied access to a specific intelligence programme because her current assignment does not involve that programme. Which principle does this demonstrate?

✅ Correct: B. Need to Know goes beyond clearance level. Even if an individual holds the right clearance, they only receive access to specific information when their current duties require it. The analyst has TS clearance but no need for that specific programme — so access is denied. This is the classic definition of the Need to Know principle in government/intelligence contexts.

Question 7: During an internal HR audit, it is discovered that three developers who left the company six months ago still have active VPN accounts. Which offboarding control FAILED?

✅ Correct: C. Access deprovisioning (disabling all accounts — including VPN — promptly upon departure) failed. The six-month gap represents a critical vulnerability. Any of these former employees could have accessed company systems during that period. Timely access revocation is the primary offboarding security control and the one that failed here.

Question 8: Which type of insider threat describes a legitimate employee whose credentials have been stolen and are now being used by an external attacker without the employee's knowledge?

✅ Correct: C. A compromised insider is a legitimate user whose credentials or access have been taken over by an external threat actor. The employee is not acting maliciously — they're an unwitting vehicle. This is distinct from malicious (intentional harm) or negligent (accidental harm) insiders. Compromised insiders are particularly dangerous because their activity appears as that of a known, trusted user.

Question 9: Which control combination BEST addresses both the prevention of single-person fraud AND builds organisational resilience against key-person dependency risks?

✅ Correct: D. Separation of Duties prevents single-person fraud by requiring multiple people for sensitive processes. Job rotation addresses key-person dependency by cross-training and moving employees between roles — ensuring no single employee holds exclusive institutional knowledge. Mandatory vacation also detects fraud but doesn't specifically build knowledge resilience the way job rotation does.

Question 10: An organisation requires all new employees to sign an Acceptable Use Policy on their first day and before any credentials are issued. What is the PRIMARY security purpose of this requirement?

✅ Correct: B. The AUP, signed before access is granted, serves two critical purposes: it sets behavioural expectations, and — crucially — it provides the legal and ethical basis for monitoring employee activity and taking disciplinary action for violations. By signing, employees acknowledge the organisation's right to monitor systems use. Signing before credential issuance ensures informed consent is obtained before access is provided.
