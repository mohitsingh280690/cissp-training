Day 22 – Data Classification Levels

MCQ Practice – Domain 2: Asset Security
📋 Instructions: 10 CISSP-style questions. Pass mark: 7/10 (70%).

Question 1: In the US government classification system, which level is defined as information whose unauthorised disclosure could cause "exceptionally grave damage to national security"?

✅ Correct: C. Top Secret is the highest formal US government classification level. Its definition specifically uses the phrase "exceptionally grave damage to national security." Secret = "serious damage"; Confidential = "damage."

Question 2: An organisation uses a commercial four-level classification scheme. An employee payroll database contains salary information for all staff. Which level is MOST appropriate?

✅ Correct: C. Salary information is a classic example of Private-level data in commercial classification — it's personally sensitive to individuals (employees), internally focused, and disclosure would cause harm to individuals. It doesn't rise to Confidential (trade secrets, M&A plans) but exceeds Sensitive (internal reports without personal harm implications).

Question 3: A government analyst with Top Secret clearance wants to access a compartmented intelligence programme. Their TS clearance alone is insufficient. What additional requirement must be met?

✅ Correct: B. Sensitive Compartmented Information (SCI) requires both Top Secret clearance AND specific access authorization (a "read-on") for each particular compartment or program. Clearance level is necessary but not sufficient — need-to-know for the specific program is the additional gate. This is the government application of the need-to-know principle at the highest classification levels.

Question 4: A company's marketing department produces a quarterly press release for public distribution. What classification level should this document receive?

✅ Correct: D. Information approved for public release is classified as Public (or Unclassified in government). A press release is explicitly intended for public consumption — there are no access restrictions, no encryption requirements, and no special handling needed. Applying a higher classification would be over-classification and create unnecessary friction.

Question 5: A document contains sections classified at Secret and sections classified at Top Secret. How should the overall document be marked?

✅ Correct: C. Documents containing mixed classification levels must be marked at the HIGHEST level present. The overall document marking controls who may access it — a Secret-cleared person cannot access a Top Secret document even if only one paragraph is Top Secret. Portion marking (marking each section individually) can be used in addition to the overall highest-level marking to help readers understand which parts drive the overall classification.

Question 6: An organisation notices employees are labeling almost every internal email as "Confidential" regardless of content, causing the label to become meaningless. This is BEST described as:

✅ Correct: A. Over-classification (applying higher-than-warranted classifications) leads to classification fatigue — employees become desensitised to classification labels because they see them on everything. This causes the labels to lose their meaning and effectiveness. When users ignore all Confidential labels because every email is marked that way, genuinely sensitive data receives no additional attention. Calibrated, accurate classification is more effective than blanket high-classification.

Question 7: In US government classification, "Sensitive But Unclassified" (SBU) data is BEST described as:

✅ Correct: B. SBU (Sensitive But Unclassified) is a designation for information that doesn't meet the national security classification criteria but still requires protection — such as personally identifiable information (PII), law enforcement sensitive (LES) data, or for official use only (FOUO) data. It has no formal damage-to-national-security threshold like the three formal classification levels, but it must not be publicly disclosed.

Question 8: Which commercial classification level is MOST analogous to the US government's Top Secret classification?

✅ Correct: D. Both Top Secret (government) and Confidential/Restricted (commercial) represent the HIGHEST level in their respective schemes. They are the most sensitive tiers requiring the greatest access restrictions, strongest controls, and most rigorous handling procedures. The naming is different, but the position in the hierarchy is equivalent.

Question 9: An organisation fails to update the classification of merger documents after the merger is publicly announced and the deal closes. What risk does this create?

✅ Correct: B. Stale high classifications for data that is no longer sensitive creates over-classification — applying more expensive controls than the data requires, restricting access to users who legitimately need it, and wasting security budget. Classification should be reviewed and updated when circumstances change. Failure to reclassify downward is an inefficiency; failure to reclassify upward is a security risk.

Question 10: An organisation's trade secrets (proprietary formulas not publicly disclosed) would MOST likely be classified as which commercial level?

✅ Correct: D. Trade secrets (proprietary formulas, algorithms, processes) represent the highest-value information assets a commercial organisation possesses. Their disclosure to competitors would cause significant competitive and financial harm — the defining criterion for the Confidential/Restricted classification tier. This data requires the strongest access controls, encryption, and handling procedures.
