2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Quantitative risk analysis translates security risk into the language executives and boards speak: dollars. When a CISO says "we need to spend $200K on a new firewall," the CFO wants to know: what is the expected annual loss we're preventing? Is the $200K justified?

Quantitative analysis enables:

  • Prioritisation of security investments based on financial impact
  • Communication of risk to non-technical stakeholders
  • Objective comparison of control costs vs. risk reduction
  • Insurance decisions (how much cyber insurance to purchase)
  • Regulatory justification for control expenditures

4. Core Quantitative Formulas

Asset Value (AV)

Asset Value (AV): The monetary value of the asset being analysed. May include replacement cost, revenue it generates, data sensitivity value, and cost of loss-of-use.

Exposure Factor (EF)

Exposure Factor (EF): The percentage (0–100%) of the asset's value that would be lost in a single threat occurrence. If a fire would destroy 75% of a data centre, EF = 0.75 (or 75%).

Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)

SLE = AV × EF

SLE represents the expected dollar loss each time a specific threat occurs once. It is the severity of a single event.

Annualised Rate of Occurrence (ARO)

ARO: The estimated frequency with which a threat is expected to occur per year. ARO = 1 means once per year; ARO = 0.1 means once every 10 years; ARO = 10 means 10 times per year.

Annualised Loss Expectancy (ALE)

ALE = SLE × ARO

ALE represents the expected annual dollar loss from a specific threat. This is the primary number used to justify security spending. A control is financially justified if it costs less than the ALE reduction it produces.

🧠 The Quantitative Chain — memorise left to right:
AV × EF = SLE
SLE × ARO = ALE
Full chain: AV → (×EF) → SLE → (×ARO) → ALE
Memory hook: "Assets Exposed Suffer Losses Annually"
AV × EF = SLE | SLE × ARO = ALE

5. Worked Example 1 — Basic ALE Calculation

Scenario

A company has a database server worth $400,000. Security analysts estimate that a ransomware attack would destroy or encrypt 50% of its data and operations. Historical industry data suggests ransomware attacks of this type occur approximately 3 times per year across similar organisations in their sector.

Step-by-Step Calculation

VariableValueExplanation
AV (Asset Value)$400,000Full value of the database server and its data
EF (Exposure Factor)0.50 (50%)Ransomware would destroy/encrypt 50% of value
SLE = AV × EF$400,000 × 0.50 = $200,000Expected loss per ransomware incident
ARO3 (per year)Expected to happen 3 times per year
ALE = SLE × ARO$200,000 × 3 = $600,000Expected annual ransomware loss
Interpretation: The organisation should expect to lose approximately $600,000 per year to ransomware events on this asset if no additional controls are implemented.

Reverse Calculations (Exam Common)

The exam may give you any two values and ask you to calculate the third:

  • Given SLE and ARO → ALE = SLE × ARO
  • Given ALE and ARO → SLE = ALE ÷ ARO
  • Given ALE and SLE → ARO = ALE ÷ SLE
  • Given AV and EF → SLE = AV × EF
  • Given SLE and AV → EF = SLE ÷ AV

6. Worked Example 2 — Cost-Benefit Analysis

Scenario (continued from above)

A vendor offers a backup and ransomware protection solution for $150,000 per year. The vendor claims it will reduce the ransomware exposure factor from 50% to 10% (the remaining 10% represents cases where the backup fails or recovery takes time).

CBA Calculation

StepCalculationResult
Original ALE (before control)$200,000 × 3 = ALE before$600,000/year
New SLE after controlAV × New EF = $400,000 × 0.10$40,000
New ALE (after control)$40,000 × 3$120,000/year
ALE Reduction (value of control)$600,000 – $120,000$480,000/year saved
Annual Cost of Control(given)$150,000/year
Net Benefit$480,000 – $150,000$330,000/year net benefit
Decision: The control should be implemented. It saves $480,000/year in expected losses and costs only $150,000/year — a net benefit of $330,000/year. The return on investment is positive and clear.

CBA Formula

Value of Control = ALE(before) – ALE(after) – Annual Cost of Control
If result is positive → control is financially justified
If result is negative or zero → control may not be financially justified
⚠️ CBA Doesn't Tell the Whole Story:
Some controls are required by law or regulation regardless of CBA. A negative CBA doesn't mean "skip the control" if compliance demands it. CBA is a financial tool, not the final word on whether to implement a control.

7. Return on Security Investment (ROSI)

ROSI (Return on Security Investment): A metric that expresses the financial return from investing in a security control, analogous to ROI in general business.
ROSI = (ALE Reduction – Cost of Control) / Cost of Control × 100%
Or: ROSI = (Risk Mitigated – Control Cost) / Control Cost

ROSI Worked Example

Using the previous scenario:

  • ALE Reduction = $480,000/year
  • Control Cost = $150,000/year
  • ROSI = ($480,000 – $150,000) / $150,000 = $330,000 / $150,000 = 2.2 or 220% ROSI

A ROSI of 220% means for every dollar spent on this control, the organisation gets $2.20 in value (savings). Strong positive ROSI indicates a good security investment.

💡 Limitation of Quantitative Methods: Quantitative analysis is only as good as its inputs. AV, EF, and ARO estimates are often based on limited historical data or industry averages, which may not accurately reflect a specific organisation's risk profile. This is why qualitative methods remain valuable — they capture expert judgment that numbers may miss.

9. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 The ALE Formula Chain — The Most Important CISSP Formulas:
Step 1: AV × EF = SLE (how bad is one hit?)
Step 2: SLE × ARO = ALE (how often does it happen × how bad = yearly cost)

Think of it as: Asset × Exposure → Single Loss → × Annual Rate → Annual Loss
Memory hook: "Avoid Every Single Awful Risk Always" → AV × EF = SLE × ARO = ALE
🧠 CBA Decision Rule:
ALE(before) – ALE(after) = Annual Value of Control
If Annual Value > Cost of Control → BUY THE CONTROL
If Annual Value < Cost of Control → QUESTION the investment (unless legally required)
Memory hook: "Does the control SAVE more than it COSTS?"
🧠 ARO Values to Know:
ARO = 1 → once per year
ARO = 0.5 → once every 2 years
ARO = 0.1 → once every 10 years
ARO = 10 → 10 times per year
ARO is always times/year — not a probability percentage!
⚠️ EF is a Decimal, Not a Whole Number:
EF = 50% should be entered as 0.50 in calculations, not as 50.
SLE = $400,000 × 0.50 = $200,000 ✓
SLE = $400,000 × 50 = $20,000,000 ✗ (wrong — 50× not 50%)
Always convert percentages to decimals before multiplying!
⚠️ ALE Is Annualised, Not Per-Event:
ALE is what you expect to LOSE PER YEAR on average. It's not what you'll lose in any single year — some years may see no events, others may see multiple. ALE is an actuarial average used for budget planning and control justification.
