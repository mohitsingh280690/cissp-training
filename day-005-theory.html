⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • Copyright protects the expression, not the idea. The idea of a sorting algorithm is not copyrightable; the specific code implementing it is.
  • Trade secrets require active protection measures — simply keeping something secret without formal procedures does NOT give legal protection if you later claim misappropriation.
  • Trademarks need to be actively defended or they can be lost ("genericisation").

2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Business Impact of IP

For technology companies, IP often constitutes 80%+ of total enterprise value. The source code, algorithms, customer databases, product designs, and brand identity are worth far more than physical assets. A CISSP must understand how to classify, protect, and respond to the theft or misuse of intellectual property.

Incident Reality

  • SolarWinds (2020): Among the most devastating IP theft elements was access to source code repositories — the attacker's goal was partly to understand and weaponise proprietary software.
  • Waymo v. Uber (2017): An engineer left Google's self-driving unit and allegedly took 14,000 confidential files to Uber. Settled for $245 million in Uber equity. Classic trade secret case.
  • Economic Espionage: The FBI estimates that IP theft costs the US economy $225–600 billion annually, predominantly from state-sponsored actors.

Who Owns IP in Your Organisation?

Work-for-hire doctrine: in most jurisdictions, IP created by an employee during the scope of employment is owned by the employer, not the employee. This must be explicitly addressed in employment contracts. Contractors require specific IP assignment agreements — without them, the contractor may retain rights.

4. Copyright

Copyright: Automatically protects original creative works fixed in any tangible medium of expression. The moment a work is created and fixed (written, recorded, compiled), copyright exists.

What Copyright Covers in Technology

  • Software source code and object code
  • User interfaces (to a degree — look and feel is more complex)
  • Documentation, manuals, technical writing
  • Databases (the selection and arrangement of data, not the raw facts)
  • Website content
  • Training materials, presentations

Copyright Does NOT Protect

  • Ideas, concepts, methods, or principles (only their expression)
  • Facts or data themselves (only the arrangement/selection)
  • Works not fixed in tangible form (extemporaneous speech)
  • Government works created by federal employees in their official capacity (US public domain)

Exclusive Rights of Copyright Holder

The copyright holder has exclusive rights to reproduce, create derivative works, distribute, publicly perform, publicly display, and (for digital audio works) digitally transmit the work. All others need permission (license).

Fair Use Doctrine

Fair use is a legal defence that permits limited use without permission. Courts weigh four factors:

  1. Purpose and character of use (commercial vs. non-profit, educational; transformative uses favoured)
  2. Nature of the work (factual works more permissible than creative)
  3. Amount and substantiality of the portion used
  4. Effect on the market for the original work

The Berne Convention

International copyright treaty with 181 member countries. Key principle: automatic protection — copyright exists upon creation in any member country, and member countries must protect works from other member countries as they protect their own. This established the international baseline of life + 50 years (US extends to life + 70).

💡 DMCA: The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998) added two key provisions: (1) prohibits circumvention of technological protection measures (anti-circumvention); (2) safe harbour for internet service providers that promptly respond to takedown notices (ISPs not liable for user-uploaded infringing content if they comply). Relevant when your organisation uses third-party hosting platforms.

5. Trademarks

Trademark (™): A word, name, symbol, design, or combination that identifies the source of goods or services and distinguishes them from competitors. Service marks (℠) apply specifically to services.

™ vs ®

SymbolMeaningWhen to Use
™Trademark claimed but NOT federally registeredCan use immediately upon claiming the mark; provides limited protection
®Trademark officially registered with the USPTO (or equivalent authority)Only after registration completed; provides stronger legal protection and presumptions
℠Service mark claimed but not registeredSame as ™ but for services rather than goods

Trademark Duration & Loss

Trademarks can last indefinitely with continued use and periodic renewals. However, trademark rights can be lost through:

  • Abandonment: Ceasing use with intent not to resume
  • Genericisation: When a trademark becomes the generic name for a product category. Former trademarks lost to genericisation: aspirin, escalator, thermos, zipper. Current risks: Velcro actively fights this.
  • Improper Licensing: Failing to control quality of licensed goods can lead to loss
Cybersquatting: Registering domain names that contain trademarks of others with the intent to profit from them is addressed by the Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA) and the Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy (UDRP) administered by ICANN. Relevant for protecting an organisation's brand online.

6. Patents

Patent: A government-granted exclusive right to make, use, and sell an invention for a limited time, in exchange for public disclosure of the invention. Patents are the strongest but most expensive form of IP protection.

Requirements for Patentability

To be patentable, an invention must be:

  1. Novel: Not previously known or used before the filing date
  2. Non-obvious: Not an obvious next step to a person having ordinary skill in the field
  3. Useful: Has some practical utility (very low bar)
  4. Patent-eligible subject matter: Laws of nature, abstract ideas, and natural phenomena cannot be patented (highly contested in software — Alice Corp v. CLS Bank, 2014)

Types of Patents

TypeCoversDuration (from filing)Example
Utility PatentProcesses, machines, manufactures, compositions of matter20 yearsNew software method, cryptographic algorithm, hardware device
Design PatentOrnamental design of a functional item15 yearsUnique appearance of a device, GUI icon design
Plant PatentNew asexually reproduced plant variety20 yearsNot relevant to CISSP
⚠️ Patent Trolls (Non-Practising Entities): Organisations that acquire patents not to use them but to sue others for licensing fees. This is a significant threat in the software industry. CISSPs advising on IP strategy should be aware of the risk and factor it into patent portfolio management.
💡 Software Patents: Software patents are controversial and increasingly limited after Alice Corp v. CLS Bank International (2014), where the Supreme Court held that merely implementing an abstract idea on a computer does not make it patent-eligible. Security algorithms and protocols may still be patentable if they solve a specific technical problem in a non-abstract way.

7. Trade Secrets

Trade Secret: Any information — formula, process, algorithm, business method, customer list, pricing strategy — that provides a competitive business advantage and is kept confidential through reasonable security measures.

What Can Be a Trade Secret?

  • Software source code
  • Cryptographic keys and algorithms
  • Customer/prospect lists and pricing data
  • Manufacturing processes and formulas (Coca-Cola recipe is the most famous example)
  • Business strategies and plans
  • Security architecture details

Advantages Over Patents

  • No disclosure required: Patents require full public disclosure; trade secrets remain completely hidden
  • No expiration: Trade secrets last indefinitely if maintained; patents expire after 20 years
  • Immediate protection: No registration, filing, or waiting period

Requirements for Trade Secret Protection

The secret must have commercial value AND the owner must take reasonable steps to maintain secrecy. Courts examine:

  • Physical security measures (access restrictions, clean-desk policies)
  • Access control — need-to-know basis, least privilege
  • Employee agreements (NDAs, non-compete clauses)
  • Marking of confidential documents
  • Third-party confidentiality agreements
  • Security training on protecting proprietary information
⚠️ Critical: If you do NOT take reasonable protective measures, you cannot later claim trade secret misappropriation. An organisation that leaves source code on a publicly accessible server, doesn't require NDAs, or shares details casually in meetings has essentially abandoned trade secret protection through its own negligence.

Key Laws

LawYearKey Provisions
Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA)2016Created a federal civil cause of action for trade secret misappropriation; allows a plaintiff to sue in federal court; "inevitable disclosure" doctrine; allows seizure of property without notice in extreme cases
Economic Espionage Act (EEA)1996Federal criminal law; makes it a crime to steal trade secrets for the benefit of a foreign government; up to 15 years imprisonment and $5 million fines for foreign economic espionage
Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA)1979/1985Model state law providing consistent civil trade secret protection across the 48 states that adopted it

8. IP in Software

Open Source Software Licensing

Open source software is protected by copyright but licensed with minimal restrictions. However, license types MATTER — using the wrong open source component can "infect" proprietary code:

License TypeKey RequirementRisk to Proprietary CodeExamples
Copyleft (GPL)Derivative works must also be licensed under GPL and source code made availableHIGH — if you incorporate GPL code into proprietary software and distribute it, you may be required to release your source codeLinux kernel (GPL v2), GNU tools
Lesser GPL (LGPL)Libraries can be used by proprietary applications if linked dynamically; modifications to the library itself must be sharedMEDIUM — safe to link against, risky to modifyMany GNU libraries
Permissive (MIT, Apache, BSD)Use for any purpose with attribution; no requirement to share sourceLOW — can use in proprietary software freelyApache, jQuery, Node.js packages
Creative CommonsVaries by CC variant (CC-BY, CC-SA, CC-NC, CC-ND)Depends on variant — CC-SA requires share-alike; CC-NC prohibits commercial useWikipedia content, many public datasets
💡 GPL Copyleft "Infection": If your proprietary software statically links to a GPL library, you may be required to release your entire product's source code. This is why many enterprises prohibit GPL components in products they distribute commercially. Software composition analysis (SCA) tools exist to audit open source components and their licenses.

Software Asset Management (SAM): The process of managing and optimising the purchase, deployment, maintenance, utilisation, and disposal of software applications. A CISSP should understand SAM as a control to mitigate legal liability from unlicensed software and to reduce the attack surface from outdated or unknown software.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Four IP Types: "Can't Take People's Stuff"
Copyright → Creative works (auto-protected on creation)
Trademark → Trade identity (brand names, logos)
Patent → Physical/process inventions (20 years)
Secret → Trade Secrets (unlimited if protected)
Or alternatively: "C-T-P-S" = "Clever Thinkers Patent Secrets"
🧠 Copyright Duration:
"Life of the Author PLUS seventy" → Life + 70 years (US post-1978)
Works made for hire: 95 years from publication OR 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter
🧠 Patent Duration — "2020 Utility, 15 Design":
Utility patent = 20 years from filing date
Design patent = 15 years from grant date
Memory hook: Utilities last twenty; Designs (like a fancy number) last fifteen
🧠 Trade Secret Requirements — "VAP":
Value — must provide competitive advantage
Active — must take reasonable active protective measures
Private — must actually be kept confidential
No VAP = No trade secret protection!
🧠 Open Source License Risk Levels:
GPL = Give back (copyleft — high risk for proprietary code)
LGPL = Limited copyleft (use library, but share library changes)
MIT/Apache/BSD = Free for all (permissive — safe for commercial use)
Memory: "GPL = Gives, LGPL = Lends, MIT = Frees"
⚠️ Copyright Expression vs. Idea:
The IDEA of building a to-do list app is NOT copyrightable.
The SPECIFIC CODE you write to implement it IS copyrightable.
The exam may present scenarios where an idea is "stolen" — copyright doesn't help there; it only protects the specific fixed expression.
⚠️ Trade Secret vs. Patent Choice:
If you PATENT something, you MUST publicly disclose it — and protection expires in 20 years.
If you keep it a TRADE SECRET, you disclose nothing — but if someone independently discovers or reverse-engineers it, you have NO protection. Patents give legal exclusivity even against independent discoverers; trade secrets do not.
