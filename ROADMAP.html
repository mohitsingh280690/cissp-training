How It Works

  1. Request a day: Ask me to prepare the next day's exercise
  2. I create: A theory.html + interactive mcq.html in exercises\
  3. You study: Read the theory (with mnemonics, CISSP context, and org context), then attempt the MCQ
  4. Submit answers: Tell me your answers or score
  5. I review: Check your answers, explain mistakes, move files to completed\
  6. Repeat: Ask for the next day

Theory & Exercise Content Standards

Every theory and MCQ file generated in this course must meet the following mandatory standards.

Theory File Requirements

1. CISSP Context — Where This Fits in the Exam (Mandatory)

Each theory file must open with a "Where This Fits in CISSP" section that explains:

  • Which domain this topic belongs to and that domain's exam weight percentage
  • Why CISSP tests this: the management or security principle driving it
  • Exam question style: whether questions will be scenario-based, definition-based, calculation-based, or policy/best-practice oriented
  • Cross-domain connections: how this topic appears or connects to other domains
  • Exam traps: common wrong-answer traps the exam uses for this topic

2. Organizational Context — Where This Fits in the Real World (Mandatory)

Each theory file must include a "Real-World Organizational Relevance" section that explains:

  • Organizational ownership: who owns, implements, or governs this topic (CISO, data owner, IT operations, legal, HR, audit, etc.)
  • Implementation reality: how real organizations actually apply this (policies, tools, processes, teams, budget)
  • Failure modes: what goes wrong when this is ignored, skipped, or done poorly — real-world breach examples where relevant
  • Business impact: why the board, executives, and auditors care about this topic

3. Mnemonics & Memory Tricks (Mandatory for any topic with lists or processes)

Each theory file must include a dedicated "Memory Tricks" callout box for:

  • Any list of 3 or more items → provide an acronym mnemonic or memorable phrase
  • Any multi-step process → provide a first-letter mnemonic or visual metaphor
  • Any commonly confused pair or group → provide a contrast callout ("Not X — it's Y because…")
  • Key formulas → provide a plain-English translation alongside the equation
  • Exam traps → flag with a distinct ⚠️ warning callout explaining the distinction the exam exploits

4. Comprehensiveness & Depth

  • Cover the topic to the depth of both the OSG (Official Study Guide) and the AIO (All-in-One Exam Guide)
  • Explain mechanisms, not just definitions — answer "how does this work?" and "why was this designed this way?", not just "what is it?"
  • Include edge cases and exam-favourite exceptions that trip up candidates
  • Use comparison tables for any two or more concepts that are frequently confused
  • Link forward and backward to adjacent days' topics to build cumulative understanding

MCQ Writing Standards

Every MCQ must:

  • Have exactly 4 options (A–D), with exactly one unambiguously correct answer
  • Test understanding and application, not pure recall wherever possible — favour scenario-based stems
  • Include a detailed explanation (2–4 sentences) covering: why the correct answer is right AND why the most tempting distractor is wrong
  • Avoid "all of the above" / "none of the above" as options
  • Have distractors that are plausible — never obviously wrong
  • At least 3 of 10 questions should be scenario-based ("A company has...", "During an audit...", "A security manager is asked to...")
  • Cover the full breadth of the day's topic — not just the first section

Mnemonics & Memory Tricks Master Reference

All theory files reference mnemonics from this master list. Build these into long-term memory through spaced repetition.

Domain 1: Security and Risk Management

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
CIA Triad C.I.A. — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Same initials as the spy agency: they keep secrets (C), ensure accurate intel (I), and must always be reachable (A)
DAD Triad (attacks on CIA) D.A.D. — Disclosure, Alteration, Destruction. DAD attacks CIA: D→C, A→I, D→A
STRIDE threat model "STRIDE right past attackers" — Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege
DREAD scoring "DREAD the score" — Damage, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected users, Discoverability. Higher = worse
NIST CSF functions "I Protect Dangerous Rogue Robots" — Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
Policy hierarchy (most to least binding) "Policies Set Procedures — Baselines Guide" — Policy → Standard → Procedure → Baseline → Guideline
Risk formula chain AV × EF = SLE then SLE × ARO = ALE — "Assets times Exposure = Single Loss; Single Loss times Annual Occurrence = Annual Loss"
Risk response options "MATA" — Mitigate, Accept, Transfer, Avoid
BCP recovery metric order MTD ≥ RTO + WRT; RPO is separate — MTD is the hard deadline (max tolerable downtime); RTO + WRT must fit within it; RPO is about data age, not time to recover
BCP metric plain English MTD = "how long we can survive offline"; RTO = "when systems must be back"; WRT = "time to validate/test restored systems"; RPO = "how old the backup data can be"
Personnel security controls "SL MJ-A" — Separation of duties, Least privilege, Mandatory vacation, Job rotation, Account review
Due care vs Due diligence Due care = doing the right thing (action). Due diligence = verifying it was done right (verification). "Care = act; Diligence = check"

Domain 2: Asset Security

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
Government classification levels "TS-S-C-SBU-U" — Top Secret, Secret, Confidential, Sensitive But Unclassified, Unclassified. "Top Spies Carefully Secure Underground intel"
Data roles Owner decides/classifies → Custodian stores/protects → Steward maintains quality → User consumes. "Owner decides, Custodian guards, Steward cleans, User uses"
Data lifecycle "C-SUSAD" — Create, Store, Use, Share, Archive, Destroy. "Can Students Usually Share Archived Documents?"
Three data states "RUT" — at Rest, in Use, in Transit. "Data is always in some RUT"
NIST 800-88 sanitization levels "CPD" — Clear (logical overwrite), Purge (degauss/crypto-erase), Destroy (physical shred/melt). "Increasingly permanent — C → P → D"
3-2-1 backup rule 3 copies, on 2 different media types, 1 stored off-site. "321: three, two, one — backup launch!"
DLP coverage types "NEC" — Network DLP, Endpoint DLP, Cloud/CASB DLP
GDPR key roles Controller decides the purpose → Processor processes it → Data Subject is the person → DPO oversees compliance → Supervisory Authority enforces. "Controller commands; Processor processes; Subject owns their data"
Anonymization vs Pseudonymization Anonymization = irreversible (no re-identification possible). Pseudonymization = reversible with a key. "Anon = gone forever; Pseudo = a pseudonym that can be reversed"

Domain 3: Security Architecture and Engineering

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
Bell-LaPadula (BLP) — Confidentiality model "No Read Up, No Write Down" (NRU / NWD). Keeps secrets from leaking. Think: a secret agent can't read a file above their clearance level, and can't copy top-secret info into a public memo
Biba — Integrity model "No Read Down, No Write Up" (NRD / NWU). Keeps data clean. The exact opposite of BLP
BLP vs Biba quick contrast BLP = "Below Level? Prohibited to read up — keeps Confidentiality". Biba = "Below me is Impure — can't read down — keeps Integrity"
Clark-Wilson — Integrity via transactions "CDI = trusted data; UDI = untrusted; IVP validates; TP transforms". "Can Untrusted Input be Validated and Transformed? Yes — Clark-Wilson says so"
Brewer-Nash (Chinese Wall) Prevents conflict of interest. "Once you touch Coke files, a Wall blocks you from Pepsi — you picked a side"
Saltzer & Schroeder (8 principles) "FLEE COLS" — Fail-safe defaults, Least privilege, Economy of mechanism, Encapsulation, Complete mediation, Open design, Least common mechanism, Separation of privilege
CPU protection rings Ring 0 = Kernel (most privileged). Ring 3 = User (least privileged). "0 is ground zero — maximum power". Ring 0 → OS kernel; Ring 3 → your apps
Common Criteria EAL scale EAL 1–2 = functionally tested. EAL 3–4 = methodically tested. EAL 5–7 = formally verified/designed. "Higher EAL = more trust = more expensive to certify"
Block cipher modes "ECB is Evil" (identical plaintext blocks → identical ciphertext — reveals patterns). CBC chains blocks (better). CTR uses a counter (fast, parallelizable). GCM = CTR + authentication tag (use this). "ECB? Never. GCM? Always"
Symmetric vs Asymmetric Symmetric = same key = fast (AES, 3DES). Asymmetric = key pair = slow but solves key distribution (RSA, ECC). "Sym = same key = speedy; Asym = asymmetric keys = solves the sharing problem"
Hash function properties Must be: One-way, Deterministic, Fixed-length output, Collision-resistant. "ODFC" — "Only Deterministic Fixed Collisions-be-gone"
PKI vs Web of Trust PKI = hierarchical (Root CA at top, chains down). PGP Web of Trust = peer vouching. "PKI = pyramid; PGP = peer group"
Key sizes rule of thumb AES: 128/192/256-bit (all strong). RSA: ≥2048-bit. ECC: ≥256-bit (much smaller than RSA for same strength). "ECC punches above its weight"

Domain 4: Communication and Network Security

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
OSI Layers — bottom to top "Please Do Not Throw Sausage Pizza Away" — Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application
OSI Layers — top to bottom "All People Seem To Need Data Processing"
OSI PDUs per layer Layer 1: Bits → Layer 2: Frames → Layer 3: Packets → Layer 4: Segments → Layer 5–7: Data. "B-F-P-S-D"
TCP 3-way handshake SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK. "I want to talk (SYN). OK, I hear you — and you? (SYN-ACK). Yes, confirmed (ACK)"
DHCP DORA process Discover → Offer → Request → Acknowledge. "DORA the Explorer finds a network home"
Firewall evolution Packet filtering → Stateful inspection → Application proxy → NGFW. "Each generation got smarter"
IPSec modes Transport = host-to-host, protects payload only (leaves IP header). Tunnel = gateway-to-gateway, wraps entire packet in new IP header. "Tunnel buries everything; Transport travels light"
IPSec protocols AH = Authentication Header (integrity + auth, no encryption). ESP = Encapsulating Security Payload (encryption + auth). "AH = proves who; ESP = hides what"
WPA evolution WEP (broken RC4, weak IV) → WPA (TKIP patch) → WPA2 (AES-CCMP) → WPA3 (SAE handshake). "Each step replaced a broken piece"
DNS record types A = IPv4 address. AAAA = IPv6 (4× the bits, 4× the letters). MX = mail. CNAME = alias. PTR = reverse (IP→name). "AAAA = 4 times bigger"
Private IP ranges (RFC 1918) 10.0.0.0/8, 172.16–31.0.0/12, 192.168.0.0/16. "10 is huge (A class), 172.16 is medium (B range), 192.168 is home (most familiar)"

Domain 5: Identity and Access Management

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
IAAA framework "I Am Always Accountable" — Identification, Authentication, Authorization, Accountability
Authentication factor types Type 1 = Something you Know. Type 2 = Something you Have. Type 3 = Something you Are. "Know-Have-Are"
Biometric error types FRR = False Reject Rate = Type I (a real user is wrongly rejected — inconvenience). FAR = False Accept Rate = Type II (an impostor is wrongly accepted — security hole). "Type II is the dangerous one — frauds get in"
CER / EER The point on the curve where FRR = FAR. Lower CER = better system. "Crossover = the balance point"
Kerberos flow Client → AS (Authentication Server, gives TGT) → TGS (Ticket Granting Server, gives service ticket) → Service. "First get the ticket book (TGT), then get the specific ticket (service ticket), then get in"
Access control models DAC = owner Decides (ACLs). MAC = labels and clearances (government). RBAC = Roles (enterprise standard). ABAC = Attributes/context (most flexible). "D = Discretionary = owner Decides; M = Mandatory = government Mandates; R = Role"
OAuth vs SAML vs OIDC OAuth = Authorization (what you can do — API access delegation). SAML = Authentication (who you are — enterprise SSO). OIDC = Authentication layer built on top of OAuth. "OAuth = authorises; SAML = authenticates; OIDC = identity via OAuth"
Zero Trust "Never trust, always verify" — no implicit trust inside or outside the perimeter; every request is authenticated and authorized every time

Domain 6: Security Assessment and Testing

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
Test knowledge levels Black box = zero knowledge (outsider attack simulation). White box = full knowledge (insider / code review). Gray box = partial knowledge (partner or limited-access tester)
Pen test phases "P-R-S-E-P-R" — Planning, Reconnaissance, Scanning, Exploitation, Post-exploitation, Reporting. "Prepare → Recon → Scan → Exploit → Post-exploit → Report"
SOC report types SOC 1 = financial reporting controls (SSAE 18, for auditors). SOC 2 = security/availability/trust (for customers). SOC 3 = publicly shareable summary of SOC 2. "1 = finance; 2 = security; 3 = public"
SOC Type I vs Type II Type I = design at a point in time (snapshot). Type II = operational effectiveness over a period (movie vs. photo). "Type II takes time — far more assurance"
CVSS score components Base = inherent severity (unchanging). Temporal = current exploit availability. Environmental = your org's specific context. "Base never changes; Temporal and Environmental are adjustable"
BCDR test progression Checklist → Tabletop → Simulation → Parallel → Full Interruption. "Each step is more disruptive but more realistic and valuable"
Vulnerability scan types Authenticated scan = agent or credentials = deeper, finds more. Unauthenticated = external attacker view = fewer findings but realistic. "Authenticated = insider view; unauthenticated = attacker view"

Domain 7: Security Operations

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
IR lifecycle (NIST SP 800-61) "Prepare → Detect & Analyze → Contain / Eradicate / Recover → Post-Incident Review". Short form: "PDCERP" — Prepare, Detect, Contain, Eradicate, Recover, Post-Incident
Order of volatility Most volatile first: Registers → Cache → RAM → Swap/Virtual memory → Disk → Network logs → Off-site/remote. "Really Cool Robots Store Data Nightly Off-site"
Chain of custody rule Every person who touched the evidence must be documented. "If you can't prove who touched it, you can't use it in court"
Recovery site types Hot = fully operational, available now (most expensive). Warm = partially configured, ready in hours. Cold = empty shell, ready in days (cheapest). "Temperature = readiness = cost"
RAID levels 0 = striping only (speed, zero redundancy). 1 = mirroring. 5 = striping + single parity (min 3 disks, tolerates 1 failure). 6 = double parity (tolerates 2 failures). 10 = mirror + stripe. "1 mirrors; 5 has parity; 0 has nothing but speed"
Backup types Full = everything, every time (longest backup, fastest restore). Differential = everything since last Full (grows daily, moderate restore). Incremental = only changes since last backup of any type (fastest backup, slowest restore). "Full resets the clock; Differential grows from Full; Incremental is lean"
3-2-1 backup rule 3 copies, 2 different media types, 1 off-site. "321: three, two, one — backup launch!"
Locard's Exchange Principle Every contact leaves a trace. Digital equivalent: every intrusion leaves artifacts in logs, memory, or disk. "The attacker always leaves fingerprints somewhere"

Domain 8: Software Development Security

Concept Mnemonic / Memory Trick
SDLC phases "I Design, Develop, Operate, And Dispose" — Initiation, Design, Development, Operations/Maintenance, Disposal. "IDDOAD" (or map to your org's naming)
DevSecOps shift-left "Shift Left" = move security testing earlier in the pipeline. "Cheaper to fix a design flaw than a deployed flaw"
OWASP Top 10 (2021) "BCI IS VAILS" — Broken access control, Cryptographic failures, Injection, Insecure design, Security misconfiguration, Vulnerable components, Authn failures, Integrity failures, Logging failures, SSRF
Input validation rule "Allowlist beats Denylist" — always prefer whitelisting valid input patterns over blacklisting known bad input. "You can't blacklist what you haven't imagined"
SQL injection prevention "Parameterize everything" — parameterized queries / prepared statements eliminate SQL injection entirely
XSS types Reflected = in the response immediately (non-persistent). Stored = saved in the DB (persistent, more dangerous). DOM-based = manipulates the DOM in the browser. "Stored = stored danger; Reflected = bounces back at you"
SAST vs DAST vs IAST SAST = Static analysis (source code, no execution needed). DAST = Dynamic analysis (running app, black-box). IAST = Interactive (agent instrumented inside the running app). "SAST looks at code; DAST attacks from outside; IAST lives inside"
Software testing order Unit → Integration → System → Acceptance (UAT). "U go first, then Integrate, then the whole System, then the client Accepts"

Study Tips (Embedded in Exercises)

Exam Mindset

  • Think like a manager: CISSP tests management and policy perspective, not hands-on technical skill — when in doubt, choose the most preventive, policy-driven answer
  • Protect life first: In any scenario involving safety vs. security, human life is always priority #1
  • Know the "why": Understand the principle behind each control — the exam tests understanding, not memorization
  • Eliminate wrong answers: Narrow to two candidates, then pick the most complete or most preventive answer
  • Read every word: Scenario questions hide the key detail in one sentence — missing it means picking the wrong answer
  • Domain integration: Real exam questions frequently span two or more domains — recognize the cross-domain signals

Mnemonic Strategy

  • Use the master list: Refer to the Mnemonics & Memory Tricks Master Reference section above before each study session
  • Build your own: If a provided mnemonic doesn't click for you, immediately invent a personal one — yours will stick better
  • Say them aloud: Vocalize mnemonics while studying — hearing and speaking reinforces retention far better than reading alone
  • Anchor to what you know: Link new concepts to something already familiar (e.g., Kerberos TGT = "a day-pass for the office")
  • Spaced repetition: Revisit mnemonics on Day 2, Day 4, Day 7, and Day 14 after first encounter — don't just re-read, actively recall
  • Teach-back test: Close your notes and explain the mnemonic (and every item it stands for) aloud to an imaginary student — gaps become obvious immediately

Quick Session Routine

  • Before MCQs: Spend 2 minutes mentally running through the day's mnemonics from memory
  • During MCQs: When stuck, ask yourself "What is the mnemonic for this list or process?" — then reason from it
  • After MCQs: For every wrong answer, trace it back to the concept, find the mnemonic or distinction that would have saved you, and write it down
