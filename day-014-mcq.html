Domain 1 – Security and Risk Management

1A procurement manager presents a SOC 2 Type I report from a cloud storage vendor dated 18 months ago during a vendor onboarding review. The security team must decide whether this report provides sufficient assurance. What is the MOST appropriate response?
✅ Correct: B. SOC 2 Type I assesses whether controls are suitably designed at a single point in time — it does NOT evaluate whether controls operated effectively over time. SOC 2 Type II covers a period (typically 6–12 months) and tests operating effectiveness. 18 months is stale regardless of type — most organisations require reports no older than 12 months. For vendor due diligence, a current Type II report provides substantially stronger assurance. CISSP candidates must know the difference: Type I = design sufficiency at a point in time; Type II = operational effectiveness over a period.
2In 2020, attackers compromised the SolarWinds Orion software build pipeline, inserting malicious code that was compiled into legitimate, digitally signed software updates distributed to approximately 18,000 organisations. Which supply chain attack vector does this BEST illustrate?
✅ Correct: B. The SolarWinds Sunburst attack compromised the Orion build pipeline — attackers modified source code or build scripts so the compiled output contained the SUNBURST backdoor. The resulting binaries were legitimately code-signed by SolarWinds, passing all integrity checks. This is a build pipeline / CI-CD compromise — the most dangerous supply chain vector because: the attacker rides inside legitimate, trusted distribution infrastructure; standard verification (code signing, hash checks) provides false assurance; victims have no reason to suspect updates from their trusted vendor. Counter-measures: build pipeline integrity monitoring, reproducible builds, build environment isolation, software attestation.
3US Executive Order 14028 (May 2021) mandates Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) requirements for software sold to the federal government. What is the PRIMARY security purpose of an SBOM in the context of supply chain risk management?
✅ Correct: B. An SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) is a formal, machine-readable inventory of all software components: first-party code, third-party libraries, open-source dependencies, and their versions. Its primary supply chain risk management value: when a critical vulnerability (like Log4Shell CVE-2021-44228) is disclosed, organisations with SBOMs can immediately query their inventory to determine which products and systems are affected and prioritise response. Without SBOMs, organisations must manually scan every application — often taking weeks. SBOM standards include SPDX (Linux Foundation) and CycloneDX (OWASP). EO 14028 established SBOM as a federal procurement requirement to improve software supply chain transparency.
4An organisation is evaluating three SaaS vendors for a system that will process classified HR data. Vendor A provides a recent SOC 2 Type II with zero exceptions. Vendor B has ISO 27001 certification renewed last year. Vendor C provides both an ISO 27001 certificate and a SOC 2 Type II report from the past 6 months with no exceptions. Which combination provides the STRONGEST third-party assurance?
✅ Correct: C. ISO 27001 certification validates that a vendor has implemented a systematic Information Security Management System (ISMS) across 93 Annex A controls — a comprehensive framework covering governance, risk management, HR security, physical security, etc. SOC 2 Type II provides independent auditor testing of operational effectiveness for defined trust service criteria (Security, Availability, Confidentiality, etc.) over a period. Together they are complementary: ISO 27001 proves the framework is comprehensive; SOC 2 Type II proves controls operated effectively. For sensitive HR data, the combination of a certified ISMS + independently audited operational controls provides the strongest available third-party assurance.
5A hospital uses a critical patient monitoring application hosted by a vendor whose SLA guarantees 99.9% availability (approximately 8.7 hours of downtime per year). The hospital's business continuity requirement for this system is 99.99% availability (approximately 52 minutes per year). How should the hospital address this SLA gap?
✅ Correct: B. When a vendor SLA cannot meet the organisation's availability requirements, two remediation paths exist: (1) Contract renegotiation — require contractually committed SLA uplifts with financial penalties for non-compliance; (2) Architectural redundancy — deploy multi-vendor, multi-region, or hybrid on-premise failover so the organisation's effective availability exceeds any single vendor's guarantee. For life-critical systems (patient monitoring), the organisation cannot rely solely on vendor SLAs — they must architect resilience independently. SLA gap analysis should happen during vendor selection, not after go-live. Monitoring alone (D) provides visibility but does not improve availability.
6An organisation outsources its IT infrastructure to a Managed Service Provider (MSP). The MSP hosts its servers in a colocation facility owned by a third company. That colocation facility suffers a cooling failure causing outages. From the organisation's perspective, this outage is caused by a risk arising at which supply chain tier?
✅ Correct: C. Supply chain risk tiers: First-party = the organisation itself. Third-party = direct vendors/suppliers. Fourth-party = vendors of vendors (sub-suppliers, sub-processors). The colocation facility serves the MSP (third party), making it a fourth-party from the organisation's perspective. Fourth-party risk is pervasive and often unconsidered — organisations frequently have no direct contractual or visibility relationship with fourth parties. Mitigation: include vendor sub-contractor disclosure requirements in contracts; extend due diligence questionnaires to fourth-party processors; require concentration risk analysis from primary vendors; implement right-to-audit clauses that flow through the supply chain.
7An organisation is contracting an offshore software development firm for a project involving sensitive customer data. Which set of contractual provisions MOST comprehensively addresses the supply chain security risks of this arrangement?
✅ Correct: B. Offshore development contracts with access to sensitive data require comprehensive contractual protections: Background screening — verifies personnel trustworthiness before data access; NDA — prohibits disclosure; IP assignment — ensures all code produced belongs to the contracting organisation; Data handling restrictions — limits data access to minimum necessary; Right-to-audit — enables the organisation to verify compliance without relying solely on vendor representations; Secure coding standards — mandates OWASP-aligned development practices; Incident notification — requires timely breach notification. An NDA alone (A) is insufficient — it only addresses post-breach remedies, not prevention. Insurance (D) compensates losses but does not prevent them.
8An organisation procures server hardware through an authorised reseller of a reputable manufacturer. A security team discovers that components in five delivered servers do not match the manufacturer's genuine component database. What is the MOST significant security concern raised by this finding?
✅ Correct: B. Hardware supply chain compromise — counterfeit or backdoored components — is one of the most serious and hardest to detect supply chain threats. Hardware implants (evidenced by the Bloomberg "Big Hack" reporting and documented in intelligence community advisories) can: operate at the firmware or BMC level, survive OS wipes; be invisible to operating system or network security tools; exfiltrate data or provide persistent remote access. Counterfeit components may also contain malicious firmware from the counterfeit manufacturer. Countermeasures: Trusted Platform Module (TPM) verification; hardware provenance attestation; purchase from authorised channels with tamper-evident packaging; component authentication programmes.
9NIST SP 800-161 (Supply Chain Risk Management Practices for Federal Information Systems) recommends that organisations flow security requirements down through their entire supply chain. A primary vendor argues that this obligation ends at the first-tier supplier level. What is the MOST accurate position according to NIST 800-161?
✅ Correct: B. NIST SP 800-161 explicitly addresses multi-tier supply chain risk — it requires organisations to establish SCRM requirements that flow through the entire supply chain, not just first-tier suppliers. This means prime vendors must: include equivalent security requirements in their own supplier contracts; ensure their sub-suppliers also flow requirements further down. The rationale: supply chain attacks frequently exploit the weakest link in sub-tier supply chains (as demonstrated by SolarWinds, where the build pipeline compromise happened below the level visible to end customers). NIST 800-161 Rev. 1 (2022) expanded C-SCRM (Cyber Supply Chain Risk Management) guidance to cover deeper supply chain tiers.
10An organisation's IT operations depend on a single MSP that manages 90% of its infrastructure. During a board risk review, the CISO highlights that a simultaneous failure, price increase, or insolvency of this MSP could critically disrupt operations. What supply chain risk does this scenario MOST specifically illustrate?
✅ Correct: B. Concentration risk is the risk arising from excessive dependency on a single supplier, geography, technology, or counterparty such that a single failure event has outsourced disproportionate impact to the organisation. In supply chain risk management, concentration risk is identified when any single vendor represents too large a proportion of critical services. It is a multi-dimensional risk: operational (single point of failure), financial (price leverage / renegotiation power), security (single MSP compromise = total compromise), and continuity (MSP insolvency = immediate crisis). Mitigations: multi-vendor strategy, vendor diversification requirements, exit strategy and transition planning, escrow arrangements for critical configurations and data.
