2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Physical security is the foundation — all logical controls fail if an attacker can physically access systems. Social engineering attacks often exploit physical security gaps: tailgating into buildings, impersonating vendors, accessing unlocked server rooms. Data center physical security is audited for compliance (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, PCI DSS). A single physical breach can compromise an entire network.

3. Physical Security Layered Model — Defense in Depth

Physical security uses concentric layers (outside → inside):
Layer 1: Site perimeter — fences, gates, bollards, lighting, signage
Layer 2: Building exterior — walls, doors, windows, locks, guards
Layer 3: Building interior — reception, access controls, CCTV, alarms
Layer 4: Restricted areas — server rooms, vaults, mantraps, biometrics
Layer 5: Asset-level — locked cabinets, cable locks, port blockers

4. Perimeter Security

Fencing

Fence HeightDeterrence Level
3-4 feet (1-1.2 m)Deters casual trespassers only — psychological boundary
6-7 feet (1.8-2.1 m)Too tall to easily climb; deters most intruders
8+ feet (2.4+ m) with barbed wire/razor wireDeters determined intruders; signals high-security facility
Fence Heights — Exam Favorite: 3-4 ft = casual deterrent; 6-7 ft = too tall to climb easily; 8 ft + barbed/razor wire = deters determined intruders. CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design) uses fencing as a territorial reinforcement strategy.

Gates & Barriers

  • Bollards: Short vertical posts that prevent vehicle approach; protects against vehicle-borne attacks (rams)
  • Vehicle barriers: Active (pop-up bollards, drop-arm gates) and passive (concrete planters, berms)
  • Sally port: A controlled double-gate system — only one gate opens at a time (vehicle equivalent of a mantrap)

Lighting

  • Purpose: Deter intruders; enable CCTV effectiveness; allow guard observation
  • Standard: Critical areas illuminated to at least 2 foot-candles (fc) at 8 feet height
  • Types: Continuous/standby/movable/emergency; LED preferred for efficiency and instant-on
  • Placement: No dark spots or shadows; uniform lighting prevents concealment

6. Interior Security — Restricted Areas

Mantraps (Access Control Vestibules)

A mantrap (modern term: access control vestibule) is a small room with two doors where only one door can be open at a time. A person enters door 1, which closes and locks, then must authenticate to open door 2. Primary purpose: prevent piggybacking/tailgating — only one person can pass through at a time.

Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility (SCIF)

A SCIF is an enclosed area within a building used to process classified information. Requirements include: RF shielding (prevents eavesdropping), soundproofing, intrusion detection, continuous monitoring, strict access controls, and TEMPEST compliance. Used by government and military.

Faraday Cage

An enclosure made of conductive material that blocks electromagnetic fields. Prevents electronic signals from entering or leaving. Used for: TEMPEST protection, protecting sensitive equipment from EMP, preventing wireless eavesdropping, and RF shielding for SCIFs.

8. Environmental Controls

ControlData Center Standard
Temperature64-81°F (18-27°C) per ASHRAE; optimal ~68-72°F; hot aisle/cold aisle layout
Humidity40-60% RH. Too low → electrostatic discharge (ESD); Too high → condensation/corrosion
PowerUPS (battery backup for minutes); Generator (long-term, 10-30 sec start); dual utility feeds; PDUs
Water detectionSensors under raised floors and near pipes; water is the #2 cause of data center damage after fire
Humidity — Two Extremes:
• Too LOW humidity → Electrostatic Discharge (ESD) — damages components
• Too HIGH humidity → Condensation and corrosion — damages equipment
Target: 40-60% relative humidity

Fire Suppression Systems

SystemHow It WorksProsCons
Wet pipe sprinklerPipes always filled with water; sprinkler heads activate individually at ~165°FSimplest; most reliable; auto-activatesWater damages electronics; freeze risk; accidental discharge
Dry pipe sprinklerPipes filled with compressed air; water released only when head activatesNo freeze risk; slight delay allows false alarm checkStill uses water; slower activation; more complex
Pre-action sprinklerDry pipe + requires TWO triggers: (1) heat detection AND (2) sprinkler head activationTwo-step reduces accidental discharge; protects water-sensitive areasComplex; higher cost; slower response
DelugeAll heads open simultaneously when triggered; massive water flowFast coverage of large areasExtreme water damage; used for high-hazard areas, NOT data centers
FM-200 (HFC-227ea)Clean agent gas — suppresses fire by chemical reaction; safe for humans at design concentrationNo water damage; safe for electronics; safe for occupied spaces; Halon replacementExpensive; enclosed space required; limited supply per discharge
Novec 1230Clean agent liquid-to-gas; zero ozone depletion; very low global warming potentialEnvironmentally superior to FM-200; safe for equipment and personnel; fast-actingExpensive; enclosed space required
CO₂Displaces oxygen to suppress fireVery effective; no residueLETHAL to humans — displaces breathable air; NOT for occupied spaces
⚠️ CO₂ Is Deadly: CO₂ fire suppression works by removing oxygen. This WILL kill people in the room. CO₂ is only appropriate for unoccupied areas (e.g., unmanned computer rooms with warning alarms and time delay for evacuation). For occupied data centers, use FM-200 or Novec 1230.
Halon Replacement: Halon 1301 was the gold standard for data center fire suppression but depletes the ozone layer. Its production was banned by the Montreal Protocol (1987). Replacements: FM-200 (most common replacement) and Novec 1230 (newest, most environmentally friendly). Both are safe for humans and electronics.
Pre-action = Best Sprinkler for Data Centers: Pre-action requires two separate triggers (detection system + sprinkler head), which dramatically reduces accidental water discharge. This is the preferred water-based system for data centers and areas with water-sensitive equipment.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Fence Heights — "3-6-8"
3-4 ft = casual deterrent (neighborhood fence)
6-7 ft = most people can't climb (commercial)
8+ ft with wire = determined intruder deterrent (high security)
🧠 Fire Suppression for Data Centers — "FM = For Machines"
FM-200 = For Machines (safe for electronics AND humans)
CO₂ = Chokes Oxygen (kills people — unoccupied only!)
Pre-action = Preferred sprinkler for data centers (two triggers)
Wet pipe = Worst for electronics (water always ready to flow)
🧠 Humidity — "Low = zap, High = drip"
Low humidity → electroStatic discharge (ESD) — "Static = Shocking"
High humidity → condensation and corrosion — "Humid = H₂O problems"
🧠 Mantrap Purpose
"Mantrap Traps one person = prevents Tailgating"
