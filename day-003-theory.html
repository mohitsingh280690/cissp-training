2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Organizational Ownership

Compliance ownership sits with Legal/General Counsel (for regulatory requirements), the CISO (for security controls that evidence compliance), Internal Audit (for assessing compliance), and Business Unit Leaders (for operational compliance in their domain). The CFO owns financial compliance obligations (SOX). HR owns personnel-related compliance (background checks, training records).

Implementation Reality

Most organisations maintain a compliance programme with a dedicated compliance officer or team. The programme maps each regulatory obligation to specific controls, control owners, and evidence requirements. A "compliance gap" — where a required control does not exist or is not documented — triggers a remediation project with a defined timeline.

Failure Modes

  • British Airways (2019): GDPR non-compliance — inadequate security controls led to 500,000 customer records compromised. ICO issued a £20M fine (reduced from initial £183M). The breach involved script injection on their payment page — a PCI-DSS violation that occurred because security controls were not maintained.
  • Anthem (2015): HIPAA — 78.8 million health insurance records stolen. No encryption at rest (not required but considered best practice); inadequate access controls. Settled for $16M — largest HIPAA settlement at the time.

Business Impact

Compliance is no longer optional — it is a condition of operating. Loss of PCI-DSS compliance means card networks (Visa, Mastercard) can prohibit a company from processing card payments. HIPAA violations can result in criminal prosecution of individuals. GDPR fines can reach 4% of global annual turnover. For a company like Google, that is billions of dollars.

Critical Distinction: A standard becomes mandatory in two ways: (1) a regulation references it as the required method of compliance (e.g., HIPAA's "Security Rule" effectively mandates certain NIST controls); or (2) a contract requires it (e.g., a merchant agreement with Visa requires PCI-DSS compliance as a contractual obligation, not a law).

4. PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

PCI-DSS: A contractual standard (not a law) created and maintained by the PCI Security Standards Council (PCI SSC), founded by the major card brands: Visa, Mastercard, American Express, Discover, and JCB. Any entity that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data must comply.

PCI-DSS is enforced through contractual obligation — when a merchant signs an agreement to accept card payments, they agree to comply with PCI-DSS. Non-compliance can result in fines from card networks and ultimately loss of the ability to process card payments.

PCI-DSS Six Goals and Twelve Requirements (Summary)

Implement strong access control7–9Restrict access; assign unique IDs; restrict physical access

Maintain an information security policy12Maintain a policy that addresses information security for all personnel

💡 Key Point: PCI-DSS is not a law — it is a contractual requirement. Violation does not result in prosecution by government; it results in fines from card brands and potentially losing the right to process card payments.

5. SOX – Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002)

SOX: US federal law passed in 2002 in response to accounting scandals (Enron, WorldCom). Applies to all US publicly traded companies and their auditors. Mandates internal controls over financial reporting and significant accountability for executives.

Key SOX Sections for Security

SectionContentSecurity Relevance
Section 302CEO and CFO must personally certify quarterly/annual financial reportsExecutives are personally liable — cannot claim ignorance of material misstatements
Section 404Management must assess internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR) annually; external auditor must attestIT general controls (ITGC) — access management, change management, computer operations, data backup — are assessed
Section 802Criminalises altering, destroying, or falsifying records in federal investigationsAudit logs must be tamper-proof; records must be retained per policy
Section 409Real-time disclosure of material changes to financial conditionSecurity breaches that are "material" (financially significant) must be disclosed promptly
SOX and IT/Security: SOX does not prescribe specific technical controls — it requires management to demonstrate that financial data is accurate and protected. In practice, auditors assess IT General Controls (ITGCs) against frameworks like COBIT. Common ITGC areas: logical access controls, change management, data backup/recovery, incident management.

6. HIPAA – Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996)

HIPAA: US federal law protecting the privacy and security of Protected Health Information (PHI). Applies to covered entities (healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses) and their business associates (vendors handling PHI on their behalf).

PHI – Protected Health Information

PHI is any individually identifiable health information that relates to a person's past, present, or future health condition, the provision of healthcare, or the payment for healthcare. This includes: name, address, dates, phone numbers, Social Security numbers, medical record numbers, health plan numbers, account numbers, and more — when combined with health information.

ePHI = electronic PHI. The HIPAA Security Rule specifically governs ePHI.

HIPAA's Key Rules

RuleFocusKey Requirements
Privacy RuleWho can use and disclose PHIMinimum necessary standard; patient rights (access, amendment, accounting of disclosures); Notice of Privacy Practices
Security RuleProtecting ePHIAdministrative, Physical, and Technical safeguards; risk analysis required; addressable vs. required specifications
Breach Notification RuleWhat to do after a breachNotify affected individuals within 60 days; notify HHS; if >500 individuals in a state, notify prominent media; annual report for small breaches
Enforcement RulePenaltiesCivil: $100–$50,000 per violation; $1.5M annual cap per violation category. Criminal: up to $250K and 10 years imprisonment
Business Associate: Any person or organisation that performs functions involving PHI on behalf of a covered entity. Examples: cloud hosting provider, billing service, law firm reviewing patient records, IT support with access to ePHI systems. Business Associates must sign a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) to legally handle PHI.
💡 Addressable vs. Required Specifications (HIPAA Security Rule): Required specifications must be implemented as stated — no flexibility. Addressable specifications must be assessed: if reasonable and appropriate, implement as stated; otherwise, document why and implement an equivalent alternative. Encryption of ePHI is addressable — meaning if you decide not to encrypt, you must document that decision and implement compensating controls.

7. GLBA – Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999)

GLBA (also known as the Financial Services Modernization Act): US federal law requiring financial institutions to protect the privacy and security of customer Non-Public Personal Information (NPPI / NPI). Applies to banks, insurance companies, investment firms, mortgage lenders, tax preparers, and others providing financial services.

Three Key GLBA Rules

RulePurposeKey Requirement
Financial Privacy RuleGoverns collection and sharing of NPPICustomers must receive a privacy notice; must be given an opt-out option for sharing with non-affiliated third parties
Safeguards RuleRequires security program for NPPIDesignate a security coordinator; risk assessment; employee training; vendor oversight; incident response plan; written comprehensive security program
Pretexting RuleProhibits social engineering to obtain NPPIMakes it illegal to use false pretences to acquire customer financial information

8. FERPA & Other Key Regulations

FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)

FERPA: US federal law that protects the privacy of student education records. Applies to educational institutions that receive federal funding (effectively all public schools and most private universities). Parents have rights over records until the student turns 18.

FISMA (Federal Information Security Management Act)

FISMA: US federal law requiring federal agencies to develop, document, and implement information security programs. Mandates risk-based security for federal information systems. Agencies must use NIST frameworks (RMF, 800-53). Annual IG reports submitted to Congress.

GDPR (Preview — Covered Fully on Day 6)

The General Data Protection Regulation — EU regulation protecting personal data of EU residents. Key for the CISSP: enormous fines (up to 4% global annual turnover), strict breach notification (72 hours to supervisory authority), explicit consent requirements, data subject rights. Applies to any organisation worldwide processing EU resident data.

9. Contractual, Legal, and Regulatory Obligations

Compliance obligations come from three sources, and organisations must meet all three:

SourceNatureExamplesConsequence of Breach
RegulatoryGovernment-imposed rulesGDPR, HIPAA, SOX, FISMAFines, licence revocation, criminal prosecution
LegalCommon law obligations, tort lawNegligence (duty of care), breach of confidence, computer fraud lawsCivil lawsuits, damages, injunctions, criminal charges
ContractualObligations agreed between partiesPCI-DSS (via merchant agreement), SLAs, NDAs, BAAs, Data Processing AgreementsContract termination, financial penalties, loss of status (e.g., losing merchant status)
Important Principle — Compliance ≠ Security: Meeting compliance requirements does not guarantee security. Compliance sets a minimum baseline. An organisation can be fully PCI-DSS compliant and still be breached — TJX was breached while pursuing PCI compliance certification. Security must go beyond compliance minimums.

10. Compliance Monitoring and Reporting

Compliance is not a one-time event — it requires ongoing monitoring, evidence collection, and structured reporting.

Monitoring Mechanisms

  • Continuous monitoring: Automated tools (SIEM, vulnerability scanners, DLP) constantly assess the control environment and alert on deviations.
  • Internal audits: Periodic reviews by an independent internal function to assess whether controls are operating effectively.
  • External audits: Third-party auditors (Big Four or specialist firms) assess compliance against regulations and issue formal reports.
  • Self-assessment questionnaires (SAQ): Used in PCI-DSS for lower-level merchants; organisation attests to compliance through a structured questionnaire.
  • Regulatory inspections: Regulatory bodies (ICO, OCR, FTC) may conduct announced or unannounced inspections.

Evidence Types and Retention

Compliance programmes must retain evidence of control operation. Common evidence types:

  • System logs (access logs, change logs, audit trails)
  • Configuration screenshots and records
  • Training completion records
  • Signed policy acknowledgements
  • Vulnerability scan reports and remediation records
  • Business Associate Agreements (HIPAA)
  • Minutes of governance meetings
💡 Retention: Evidence retention periods are driven by the regulation. SOX requires 7 years. HIPAA requires 6 years from creation or last effective date. When in doubt on the exam, the answer involving longer retention and more thorough documentation is usually correct.

12. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Regulation Applicability — Industry Anchors:
PCI = Payment Card Industry — anything with card transactions
HIPAA = Health = Hospital / Healthcare
SOX = Stock market / public company financial Scandals
GLBA = Gramm-Leach-Bliley = financial industry (Banks and insurance)
FERPA = Family Education = schools and universities
Memory hook: "Pay Healthy Stocks Going For Education"
🧠 HIPAA Three Rules:
Privacy Rule = who can Peek at PHI
Security Rule = how to Secure ePHI
Breach Notification = what to do when things go Bad
Memory hook: "PSB — Privacy, Security, Breach"
🧠 GDPR 72-Hour Rule:
"GDPR gives you 3 days to tell the regulator" — 72 hours to report a breach to the supervisory authority (e.g., ICO in UK). Customer notification depends on risk of harm — high risk = must notify; low risk = may not need to.
⚠️ Compliance ≠ Security:
The exam may present a scenario where an organisation is compliant but still breached. The correct answer in such cases will acknowledge that compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. "What additional steps should be taken?" — more rigorous controls beyond compliance minimums.
⚠️ PCI-DSS is contractual, not statutory:
Non-compliance with PCI-DSS is enforced by card brands through merchant agreements — not by government prosecution. This distinguishes it from HIPAA, SOX, GDPR, and GLBA.
