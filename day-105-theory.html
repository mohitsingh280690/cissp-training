2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

The IR lifecycle is not academic — it is the difference between a contained incident costing thousands and an uncontained one costing millions. The 2017 Equifax breach demonstrated the cost of poor detection (vulnerability known for months before exploitation) and poor containment. The 2021 Colonial Pipeline incident showed the business impact of rushed eradication decisions (paying ransom vs systematic recovery).

3. Phase 1: Preparation

Preparation: Everything done BEFORE an incident occurs to ensure the organization can respond effectively. This is the proactive, ongoing phase — it never ends.

Key Preparation Activities

ActivityDetails
Policy & authorizationApprove IR policy; define authority to contain (disconnect systems, block IPs, disable accounts); obtain management sign-off
Team buildingRecruit and train IRT/CSIRT; define roles; establish on-call rotations; identify external resources (retainer agreements with forensic firms, legal counsel)
Tool deploymentSIEM; IDS/IPS; EDR; forensic imaging tools; write-blockers; packet capture capability; secure communication channels
DocumentationIR plan; procedures for each incident type; playbooks/runbooks; contact lists; escalation matrices
Training & exercisesTabletop exercises; simulation drills; red/blue/purple team exercises; annual IR training
Baseline & intelligenceDocument normal network behavior (baselines); subscribe to threat intelligence feeds; join ISACs
Jump bag / Go bagPre-assembled kit: forensic laptop, write-blockers, cables, blank media, forms, contact lists, credentials (separate from daily-use credentials)
Exam Favorite — Jump Bag Contents: A prepared forensic toolkit ("jump bag") includes: forensic laptop (non-domain-joined), write-blockers, forensic imaging software, blank hard drives, network cables, chain of custody forms, incident report templates, contact lists, and documentation of the organization's standard configurations (to identify anomalies).

4. Phase 2: Detection & Analysis

Detection Sources

SourceExamples
Automated monitoringSIEM alerts; IDS/IPS signatures; antimalware detections; EDR behavioral alerts; DLP alerts
User reportsHelp desk calls; user notices unusual behavior; phishing email reported
External notificationLaw enforcement contact; vendor notification; ISP abuse report; threat intelligence report; media report
Threat huntingProactive searching for indicators of compromise not caught by automated tools

Indicators of Compromise (IoC) vs Indicators of Attack (IoA)

TypeDefinitionExamplesTiming
IoCArtifacts that indicate a system has been compromised — evidence of past or current breachKnown malware hashes; C2 IP addresses; unusual registry keys; unauthorized accounts; data exfiltration patternsReactive — detected after compromise has occurred
IoABehaviors that indicate an attack is in progress — focus on attacker methodology and intentReconnaissance scanning; lateral movement; privilege escalation attempts; anomalous login patterns; data stagingProactive — can detect attacks before damage is done
IoC vs IoA — Exam Distinction: IoCs are forensic artifacts (think "fingerprints left behind"). IoAs are behavioral patterns (think "catching the attacker in the act"). Modern detection combines both: signature-based detection (IoC) + behavioral/anomaly detection (IoA).

Analysis — Triage and Scoping

  • Validate: Is this a true positive or false positive?
  • Categorize: What type of incident? (malware, unauthorized access, data breach, etc.)
  • Prioritize: Severity + urgency = priority. Impact on critical business functions is the primary factor
  • Scope: How many systems are affected? Is the attack still active? What data may be compromised?
  • Document: Start the incident record immediately — who, what, when, where, how discovered

5. Phase 3: Containment

Short-Term vs Long-Term Containment

Containment TypeGoalActionsTiming
Short-termImmediately stop the bleeding; prevent further damageIsolate systems from network (unplug/VLAN); block malicious IPs at firewall; disable compromised accounts; take system snapshot for forensicsMinutes to hours
Long-termMaintain containment while allowing business operations to partially resumePatch vulnerabilities; apply temporary firewall rules; rebuild affected systems on clean infrastructure; implement enhanced monitoring on contained systemsHours to days
⚠️ Containment Decision — Business vs Security: Containment always involves a trade-off between stopping the attack and maintaining business operations. Disconnecting a production database server stops the attacker but also stops the business. The IR team must coordinate with business stakeholders to make containment decisions that balance security risk with business impact. This is why management authorization for containment actions should be established DURING preparation (Phase 1), not during the crisis.

Evidence Preservation During Containment

Containment is the CRITICAL window for evidence preservation:

  • Capture volatile evidence (RAM dump, process list, network connections) BEFORE isolation
  • Create forensic images of affected systems AFTER short-term containment
  • If the decision to involve law enforcement is likely, engage them BEFORE containment actions alter evidence

6. Phase 3: Eradication

Eradication: Removing the root cause of the incident from the environment — malware removal, closing vulnerabilities, eliminating attacker access, removing persistence mechanisms.

Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

Before eradication, you must identify the root cause — otherwise you are treating symptoms, not the disease:

RCA QuestionExamples
How did the attacker get in?Unpatched vulnerability; phishing email; stolen credentials; misconfigured service
How did the attacker maintain access?Backdoor; new user account; scheduled task; web shell; modified startup scripts
How did the attacker move laterally?Pass-the-hash; service account abuse; RDP; SMB; admin shares; trust relationships
What was the attacker's objective?Data theft; ransomware deployment; cryptomining; espionage; destruction

Eradication Activities

  • Reimaging systems: Most reliable method — wipe and reinstall from known-good media (do NOT trust "cleaned" systems)
  • Patching vulnerabilities: Apply patches for the exploited vulnerability
  • Credential reset: Reset all potentially compromised credentials (especially privileged accounts, service accounts, and Kerberos tickets)
  • Remove persistence: Delete backdoors, unauthorized accounts, scheduled tasks, registry modifications, web shells
  • Verify eradication: Scan for remaining IoCs; monitor for re-infection indicators
Exam Point — Reimaging vs Cleaning: For CISSP, reimaging (rebuild from scratch using clean media) is the PREFERRED method for eradication. Simply removing detected malware files is insufficient — there may be undetected persistence mechanisms. The only way to be certain is to wipe and rebuild.

7. Phase 3: Recovery

Recovery Activities

StepActivityDetails
1Restore from known-good backupsVerify backup integrity (pre-compromise); restore data to rebuilt systems
2Validate system functionalityTest applications, services, integrations; confirm systems operate correctly
3Gradually return to productionPhase systems back in with enhanced monitoring; don't restore everything at once
4Enhanced monitoringIncrease logging, alerting, and review for recovered systems; watch for re-infection or attacker return
5Business validationBusiness owners confirm systems are functioning correctly for their operations
Recovery Validation: Recovery is NOT complete when systems are technically operational. Recovery is complete when: (1) systems are confirmed clean, (2) business operations are restored, (3) enhanced monitoring is in place, and (4) there is no evidence of continued compromise. Set a monitoring period (30-90 days) before declaring the incident fully resolved.

8. Phase 4: Post-Incident Activity

Lessons Learned Meeting

Lessons Learned (Post-Mortem): A formal meeting held after incident resolution to review what happened, what went well, what went poorly, and how to improve. Should be held within 1-2 weeks of incident closure while details are fresh.

Lessons Learned Questions

Question CategorySpecific Questions
What happened?What was the timeline? What was the root cause? What was the impact?
DetectionHow was the incident detected? Could we have detected it sooner? What detection gaps exist?
Response effectivenessDid the IR plan work? Were procedures followed? Were the right people available? Was communication effective?
Containment & eradicationWas containment effective? Was eradication complete? Did the attacker regain access?
RecoveryHow long did recovery take? Were backups adequate? Were RTO/RPO met?
ImprovementsWhat should we do differently? What tools, training, or process changes are needed? What worked well?

Post-Incident Deliverables

  • Incident report: Full documentation of the incident for management, compliance, and historical record
  • Updated IoCs: Share indicators of compromise with threat intelligence community, ISACs, and internal detection systems
  • Process improvements: Update IR plan, procedures, playbooks based on lessons learned
  • Training updates: Address any skill gaps identified during the response
  • Evidence retention: Preserve evidence according to legal hold requirements and organizational retention policy
Exam Emphasis: The MOST important post-incident activity is the lessons learned meeting. The exam asks about this frequently. The primary PURPOSE of lessons learned is to IMPROVE future incident response — not to assign blame.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 IR Lifecycle Phases — "P-DCER-P"
Preparation → Detection → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Post-Incident
"Prepare, Detect, Contain, Eradicate, Recover, Post-mortem"
🧠 Containment: Short → Long
Short-term: "Stop the bleeding" — isolate immediately
Long-term: "Controlled tourniquet" — maintain containment while enabling partial operations
"First aid, then surgery"
🧠 IoC vs IoA
IoC = Fingerprints (evidence left behind — reactive)
IoA = Footsteps (behavior being observed — proactive)
"Fingerprints are found after; footsteps are heard during"
🧠 Eradication: Reimage > Clean
"When in doubt, wipe it out." Reimaging is always preferred over attempting to clean a compromised system in place.
