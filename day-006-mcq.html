1An EU customer purchases a product from an online retailer. After the product is delivered, the customer submits a GDPR erasure request for all their personal data. The retailer is legally required to delete it. Which GDPR right is the customer exercising, and what is a key limitation of this right?
✅ Correct: B. The Right to Erasure (GDPR Article 17) allows data subjects to request deletion when the original processing purpose has been fulfilled. However, the right is NOT absolute — controllers may retain data when required by law (e.g., 7-year tax/financial record retention obligations override the erasure right). Controllers must fulfill erasure requests for data with no remaining lawful basis, while retaining only what legal obligations require. Response deadline is one calendar month (extendable by two more months for complex requests).
2A US hospital (data controller) contracts a cloud analytics company to process patient appointment data for trend analysis. The analytics company only processes data according to the hospital's documented instructions and has no independent right to use the data for its own purposes. Under GDPR, the analytics company is classified as a:
✅ Correct: B. A Data Processor (GDPR Article 4(8)) processes personal data on behalf of and under the documented instructions of a Data Controller. The key distinguishing factor is that the processor has no independent decision-making authority over the data's purpose or means. GDPR Article 28 requires a written Data Processing Agreement between controller and processor. If the analytics company started using the data for its own unrelated purposes, it could be reclassified as a controller (and potentially a joint controller).
3A company's security team discovers on a Tuesday morning that their systems experienced a breach on Sunday evening, exposing EU residents' personal data. Under GDPR Article 33, when must the supervisory authority be notified, and what if that window has already passed?
✅ Correct: A. GDPR Article 33 requires notification to the supervisory authority within 72 hours of the data controller becoming aware of the breach (not when it occurred). If 72 hours has passed, notification must be made without further delay with a documented explanation of the delay. The 72-hour clock starts when the organisation has sufficient information to understand a breach occurred — not at the first suspicion. Data subject notification (Article 34) may also be required but is a separate obligation and does not need to precede supervisory authority notification.
4A multinational corporation with €10 billion in global annual revenue is found to have systematically processed EU personal data without any lawful basis for two years, affecting millions of data subjects. Under GDPR, what is the MAXIMUM administrative fine applicable?
✅ Correct: B. GDPR has a two-tier fine structure. Tier 2 (the most serious violations, including processing without lawful basis — Article 6) carries fines up to €20 million OR 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is HIGHER. For a €10B revenue company, 4% = €400M — which exceeds €20M, so €400M is the applicable cap. Tier 1 violations (e.g., minor technical failures) carry up to €10M/2%. In practice, regulators consider proportionality — the cap is the maximum, not the guaranteed fine.
5A health analytics company purchases a dataset from a hospital. The hospital used the HIPAA Safe Harbor de-identification method, removing all 18 specified identifiers. The analytics company uses this dataset to build predictive models. Which statement MOST accurately describes HIPAA's applicability?
✅ Correct: B. HIPAA's de-identification standard (§164.514) specifies that data is no longer PHI once properly de-identified — either by removing all 18 identifiers (Safe Harbor Method) or by expert statistical certification (Expert Determination Method). Once no longer PHI, HIPAA does not govern the recipient's use of that data. BAAs are only required when a Business Associate handles PHI — de-identified data does not qualify as PHI. Key CISSP distinction: de-identification converts PHI to non-PHI; anonymisation under GDPR similarly removes it from GDPR scope.
6A California resident contacts an e-commerce company and asks: (1) what personal information the company has collected about them, (2) to delete their profile and purchase history, and (3) to stop selling their data to advertising partners. Which US privacy law provides these rights, and what is the correct set?
✅ Correct: A. The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), as amended by CPRA, provides California residents: Right to Know (what data is collected, its sources, purpose, and third parties), Right to Delete, Right to Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing, Right to Non-Discrimination, and Right to Correct. These are distinctly different from GDPR rights (portability, objection to automated decisions) — mixing them is a common CISSP exam trap. GLBA gives consumers an opt-out of certain data sharing — not deletion.
7A US company uses Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) to transfer EU employee data to its US HR system. Following the Schrems II ruling, what additional obligation does the company have regarding these SCCs?
✅ Correct: B. The CJEU's Schrems II ruling (2020) invalidated the EU-US Privacy Shield and held that SCCs, while valid in principle, require the data exporter to perform a Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) for each destination country. The TIA evaluates whether the destination country's laws (e.g., US FISA 702, EO 12333) impair the SCCs' protections in practice. If protections are inadequate, supplementary measures must be added or the transfer stopped. The EU-US Data Privacy Framework (2023) now provides an adequacy mechanism, but SCCs remain an alternative.
8A development team is designing a new customer registration form. Under GDPR Article 25 (data protection by design and by default), which design decision BEST reflects this principle?
✅ Correct: C. Privacy by Design requires embedding privacy into system architecture from the start. Privacy by Default means the strictest privacy settings are the default — users must actively opt-IN to less private settings, not opt-out. Pre-ticking marketing consent boxes violates this principle (it requires opt-OUT). Collecting only minimum necessary data reflects data minimisation. Privacy notices (A) are transparency requirements. Opt-out models (B) violate "by default." Encryption (D) is a security control, not a privacy-by-design principle.
9The OECD Privacy Guidelines include the "Collection Limitation Principle." Which statement MOST accurately describes what this principle requires?
✅ Correct: B. The OECD Collection Limitation Principle (Principle 1 of the 1980 guidelines) addresses: limiting data collected to what is necessary; using lawful and fair means; and obtaining consent or having a legal basis. Option A describes the Purpose Specification Principle. Option C describes the Data Quality Principle. Option D describes the Individual Participation Principle. These four are distinct OECD principles — a common exam question type is distinguishing between them. OECD principles also include Use Limitation, Security Safeguards, Openness, and Accountability.
10A European hospital is implementing a new patient management system using a cloud vendor who will store and process patient records on the hospital's behalf. Under GDPR Article 28, what document is legally required before the vendor begins processing?
✅ Correct: B. GDPR Article 28 mandates a written Data Processing Agreement (DPA) between every data controller and data processor. The DPA must specify: duration, nature and purpose of processing, type of data, categories of subjects, and controller's obligations and rights. It must also require the processor to: process only on documented controller instructions, ensure confidentiality, implement security measures, assist with data subject rights, and allow audits. NDAs address confidentiality only. BAAs are a HIPAA concept. SCCs (C) are for international data transfers, not domestic processor relationships.
