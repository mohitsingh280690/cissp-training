Domain 1: Security and Risk Management

20 days with identified traps & concepts
Day 01: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The exam may ask which CIA property is primarily affected by a scenario. Read carefully — a ransomware attack violates all three (encrypted = confidentiality; tampered = integrity; locked out = availability), but the primary goal of ransomware is to attack Availability to extort payment.
⚠️ Common Mistake: Many candidates over-index on Domains 3 (crypto) and 4 (networking) because those are the most technical. Domain 1 — the largest single domain at 15% — is often under-studied. Risk management and governance questions can be the margin between pass and fail.
⚠️ Key Distinction — Threat vs. Vulnerability: A threat is what might happen (fire). A vulnerability is the weakness that lets it happen (no sprinklers). A risk is the combination. You can have a threat with no vulnerability (fire in a fireproof building = low risk), or a vulnerability with no credible threat (equally low risk). Risk requires both.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Residual vs. Total Risk:
Total Risk = risk before controls. Residual Risk = risk after controls. The goal is never to eliminate all risk (impossible) but to reduce residual risk to an acceptable level. The exam will test whether you know that residual risk always exists.
🧠 Mnemonic — DAD attacks CIA:
Disclosure attacks Confidentiality → D→C
Alteration attacks Integrity → A→I
Destruction attacks Availability → D→A

Memory hook: "DAD destroys CIA's work"
🧠 CIA Triad:
C.I.A. — same as the intelligence agency: they keep secrets (Confidentiality), ensure accurate intel (Integrity), and must always be reachable (Availability).
Or: "Can I Always trust this data?" — Can=Confidentiality, I=Integrity, Always=Availability
🧠 DAD Triad:
D.A.D. — Disclosure, Alteration, Destruction
"DAD destroys CIA's work" — D→C, A→I, D→A
(Disclosure attacks Confidentiality; Alteration attacks Integrity; Destruction attacks Availability)
🧠 Domain Weights (largest to smallest):
D1(15%) → D3/D4/D5/D7(13%) → D6(12%) → D8(11%) → D2(10%)
Memory hook: "Risk rules the room" — Domain 1 (Risk Management) is always #1 by weight.
🧠 Defense in Depth — "The Castle":
Think of a medieval castle: moat → outer wall → drawbridge → inner wall → keep → throne room.
Your modern equivalent: perimeter firewall → DMZ → internal segment → host firewall → application controls → data encryption.
Each layer is a drawbridge an attacker must lower.
🧠 Non-repudiation memory anchor:
Non-repudiation = "you can't deny you did it." Think of a signed cheque — your signature is your private key. The bank (verifier) has your public signature on file. You cannot claim you didn't write it.
Day 02: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The exam distinguishes sharply between who makes decisions (senior management / data owner) and who implements decisions (IT custodian). Mixing these up is the most common error in Domain 1 governance questions.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Data Owner is NOT IT: The data owner is always a business person — a VP, department head, or senior manager. The IT department (custodian) holds and protects the data but does NOT decide who can access it or how sensitive it is. Never confuse these roles.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Who signs off on security policy?
Security policies must be signed off by senior management, not the CISO alone. The CISO develops and recommends; senior management (CEO, Board, or equivalent) approves. This grants the policy authority to be enforced.
⚠️ Exam Trap — ISO 27001 vs. ISO 27002:
ISO 27001 is certifiable (you get audited against it). ISO 27002 is a guidance document (a list of controls to reference). The exam will test this distinction.
🧠 NIST CSF Functions: Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Mnemonic: "GI Protects Dangerous Roads Rapidly"
🧠 Due Care vs. Due Diligence:
Due Care = Completion of action — "I cared enough to do it"
Due Diligence = Deep Dig to verify — "I dug deep to verify it works"

Memory hook: "Care = Act; Diligence = Check"
🧠 Governance vs. Management vs. Operations:
Governance = Guides (sets direction, Board level)
Management = Maps (plans how to get there, CISO/Managers)
Operations = Operates (does the work, analysts/admins)
"Go Make Operations happen"
🧠 Data Owner vs. Custodian vs. Steward:
Owner = the boss — decides classification, approves access (a VP, not IT)
Custodian = the janitor — keeps it clean and safe (IT sysadmin)
Steward = the librarian — ensures data quality and accurate cataloguing
Memory hook: "Owner Decides, Custodian Guards, Steward Cleans"
🧠 Strategic / Tactical / Operational:
Strategic = Sky view (5 years, where are we going?)
Tactical = Treetop view (1 year, how do we get there?)
Operational = On the ground (today, doing the work)
Memory hook: "Sky, Treetop, Ground"
🧠 NIST CSF — I Protect Dangerous Roads Rarely:
Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Day 03: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The exam tests which regulation applies to a scenario, not the detailed technical requirements. Know: PCI-DSS = payment cards; HIPAA = healthcare data (US); SOX = financial reporting (public companies); GLBA = financial industry; FERPA = student education records. Mix-ups between these are extremely common.
⚠️ Compliance ≠ Security:
The exam may present a scenario where an organisation is compliant but still breached. The correct answer in such cases will acknowledge that compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. "What additional steps should be taken?" — more rigorous controls beyond compliance minimums.
⚠️ PCI-DSS is contractual, not statutory:
Non-compliance with PCI-DSS is enforced by card brands through merchant agreements — not by government prosecution. This distinguishes it from HIPAA, SOX, GDPR, and GLBA.
🧠 Regulation Applicability — Industry Anchors:
PCI = Payment Card Industry — anything with card transactions
HIPAA = Health = Hospital / Healthcare
SOX = Stock market / public company financial Scandals
GLBA = Gramm-Leach-Bliley = financial industry (Banks and insurance)
FERPA = Family Education = schools and universities
Memory hook: "Pay Healthy Stocks Going For Education"
🧠 HIPAA Three Rules:
Privacy Rule = who can Peek at PHI
Security Rule = how to Secure ePHI
Breach Notification = what to do when things go Bad
Memory hook: "PSB — Privacy, Security, Breach"
🧠 GDPR 72-Hour Rule:
"GDPR gives you 3 days to tell the regulator" — 72 hours to report a breach to the supervisory authority (e.g., ICO in UK). Customer notification depends on risk of harm — high risk = must notify; low risk = may not need to.
Day 04: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The CISSP is not a legal exam — you are not expected to know specific statutes in detail. You are expected to know: (1) which type of law applies in a scenario, (2) the distinction between criminal and civil liability, (3) what constitutes due care, and (4) basics of evidence admissibility.
⚠️ Critical Distinction: A single incident can result in all three types simultaneously. A healthcare breach could trigger: (1) criminal prosecution of the attacker (criminal law), (2) a patient class action (civil law), and (3) an HHS OCR investigation and fine (administrative/regulatory law). These proceed independently of each other.
⚠️ Hack-Back is Illegal:
No matter how certain you are of an attacker's identity, launching counter-attacks ("hack-back") is illegal under the CFAA and equivalent laws in most jurisdictions. The legally correct response is to: (1) contain the attack, (2) collect evidence forensically, (3) report to law enforcement. Advising "hack-back" on the exam is wrong.
⚠️ Jurisdiction Doesn't Mean Prosecution:
Having jurisdiction to prosecute does not mean prosecution will happen. Political factors, resource constraints, evidence challenges, and lack of extradition treaties all limit practical enforcement — especially against nation-state actors.
🧠 Criminal vs. Civil: "The Government went Criminal; the Victim went Civil"
Criminal = Government acts → sends attacker to prison
Civil = Victim acts → gets money damages
Burden of proof: "Criminal needs CERTAINTY (beyond reasonable doubt); Civil needs 51%"
🧠 Four Elements of Negligence: "D-B-C-D"
Duty → Breach → Causation → Damages
Memory hook: "Duty Broken Causes Damage"
🧠 Four Legal System Types:
Common law (UK, US — Court precedent)
Civil/Code law (France, Germany — Comprehensive codes)
Religious law (Islamic, Jewish, Canon — Religious texts)
Customary law (traditions — Community customs)
Memory hook: "Common Codes, Religious Customs" or just "3 C's + R"
🧠 Criminal vs. Civil vs. Administrative:
Criminal = Government puts you in jail — requires near certainty
Civil = Victim takes your money — requires 51%
Administrative = Regulator fines your company — regulatory action
Memory hook: "Jail, Money, Fine"
🧠 Negligence — "Duty Broken Causes Damage":
  1. Duty of care
  2. Breach of duty
  3. Causation
  4. Damages
All four must be present for negligence to be established.
🧠 Evidence Admissibility — "R-R-L":
Relevant (to the case)
Reliable (collected properly)
Legally obtained (lawful process)
Memory hook: "Really Reliable and Legal"
Day 05: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • Copyright protects the expression, not the idea. The idea of a sorting algorithm is not copyrightable; the specific code implementing it is.
  • Trade secrets require active protection measures — simply keeping something secret without formal procedures does NOT give legal protection if you later claim misappropriation.
  • Trademarks need to be actively defended or they can be lost ("genericisation").
⚠️ Patent Trolls (Non-Practising Entities): Organisations that acquire patents not to use them but to sue others for licensing fees. This is a significant threat in the software industry. CISSPs advising on IP strategy should be aware of the risk and factor it into patent portfolio management.
⚠️ Critical: If you do NOT take reasonable protective measures, you cannot later claim trade secret misappropriation. An organisation that leaves source code on a publicly accessible server, doesn't require NDAs, or shares details casually in meetings has essentially abandoned trade secret protection through its own negligence.
⚠️ Copyright Expression vs. Idea:
The IDEA of building a to-do list app is NOT copyrightable.
The SPECIFIC CODE you write to implement it IS copyrightable.
The exam may present scenarios where an idea is "stolen" — copyright doesn't help there; it only protects the specific fixed expression.
⚠️ Trade Secret vs. Patent Choice:
If you PATENT something, you MUST publicly disclose it — and protection expires in 20 years.
If you keep it a TRADE SECRET, you disclose nothing — but if someone independently discovers or reverse-engineers it, you have NO protection. Patents give legal exclusivity even against independent discoverers; trade secrets do not.
🧠 Four IP Types: "Can't Take People's Stuff"
Copyright → Creative works (auto-protected on creation)
Trademark → Trade identity (brand names, logos)
Patent → Physical/process inventions (20 years)
Secret → Trade Secrets (unlimited if protected)
Or alternatively: "C-T-P-S" = "Clever Thinkers Patent Secrets"
🧠 Copyright Duration:
"Life of the Author PLUS seventy" → Life + 70 years (US post-1978)
Works made for hire: 95 years from publication OR 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter
🧠 Patent Duration — "2020 Utility, 15 Design":
Utility patent = 20 years from filing date
Design patent = 15 years from grant date
Memory hook: Utilities last twenty; Designs (like a fancy number) last fifteen
🧠 Trade Secret Requirements — "VAP":
Value — must provide competitive advantage
Active — must take reasonable active protective measures
Private — must actually be kept confidential
No VAP = No trade secret protection!
🧠 Open Source License Risk Levels:
GPL = Give back (copyleft — high risk for proprietary code)
LGPL = Limited copyleft (use library, but share library changes)
MIT/Apache/BSD = Free for all (permissive — safe for commercial use)
Memory: "GPL = Gives, LGPL = Lends, MIT = Frees"
Day 06: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • GDPR breach notification = 72 hours to supervisory authority; HIPAA = 60 days to HHS. Do NOT swap these.
  • GDPR applies to ANY organisation processing EU resident data — regardless of where the organisation is located.
  • Privacy ≠ Security. Privacy is about appropriate data use and individual rights; security is about protecting data from unauthorised access. Privacy requires security but extends well beyond it.
⚠️ HIPAA vs. GDPR Breach Notification:
HIPAA breach notification to HHS = 60 days
GDPR breach notification to supervisory authority = 72 hours
This is a very common exam trap. GDPR is much stricter.
⚠️ Privacy Shield History: The EU-US Privacy Shield was invalidated by the Court of Justice of the EU in Data Protection Commissioner v. Facebook Ireland (Schrems II) in July 2020, due to insufficient protection against US government surveillance. It was replaced by the EU-US Data Privacy Framework in 2023, though this too faces potential legal challenges.
⚠️ Privacy ≠ Security:
Security without privacy = You protect data from hackers but still misuse it yourself (data brokers selling behavioural data).
Privacy without security = You promise to be responsible with data but can't stop breaches.
True compliance requires BOTH. GDPR Article 5(1)(f) — Integrity & Confidentiality — explicitly ties security to privacy.
⚠️ GDPR Extraterritorial Reach:
A US company with no EU offices that runs a website, sells products to EU users, or uses tracking cookies on EU visitors IS subject to GDPR. "We're not in the EU" is not a defence.
🧠 GDPR 7 Principles: "Laughter Per Day Achieves Solid Integrating Accountability"
Lawful → Purpose limited → Data minimised → Accurate → Storage limited → Integrity/Confidentiality → Accountable
🧠 OECD 8 Principles: "Collection Quality, Purpose Use, Security Open, Individual Accountability"
Pairs: Collection + Quality | Purpose + Use | Security + Open | Individual + Accountability
Or: "CQ-PU-SO-IA" — think: "See Queue — Push — Show — IA (intelligent agent)"
🧠 GDPR 7 Principles: Acronym "LPDA-SIA"
Lawful, fair, transparent → Purpose limitation → Data minimisation → Accuracy → Storage limitation → Integrity & Confidentiality → Accountability
Memory hook: "Lawyers Prefer Data About Stuff In Archives"
🧠 Breach Notification Timelines:
GDPR = 72 hours (to supervisory authority) — think: "GD-PR = 72 hours = 3 days"
HIPAA = 60 days (to HHS) — think: "HIPAA = Healthcare = 60 days — give hospitals time"
Visual: GDPR is much stricter (72 h vs 1,440 h)
🧠 GDPR Data Subject Rights: "I-A-R-E-R-P-O-A"
Informed → Access → Rectification → Erasure → Restriction → Portability → Object → Automated decision rights
Memory hook: "I Always Request Everything, Rarely Paying Or Appealing"
🧠 OECD 8 Principles in Pairs:
Collect Carefully → Collection Limitation + Data Quality
Purpose Properly → Purpose Specification + Use Limitation
Secure Openly → Security Safeguards + Openness
Individuals Are Accountable → Individual Participation + Accountability
Day 07: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The four (ISC)² canons are listed in a specific priority order. When canons conflict, the first listed takes precedence. The order is: Society → Client → Profession → Self. This order is tested!
⚠️ "Obey your employer" is NOT a CISSP ethic:
The exam will present scenarios where following your employer's instruction conflicts with society's interest. Always choose to protect society first. "My boss told me to do it" is not a valid ethical defence.
⚠️ CEI vs (ISC)² vs RFC 1087:
These are three DIFFERENT ethics documents from three different bodies:
• (ISC)² Code = mandatory for CISSP certification holders; 4 canons
• Computer Ethics Institute = 10 Commandments; academic/general use
• RFC 1087 = IAB internet ethics statement; historical foundation
Both CEI and RFC 1087 appear on the exam as knowledge references, but (ISC)² Code is the primary professional obligation.
🧠 Four Canons Priority Order: "Society, Client, Profession, Self"
Society first → Client second → Profession third → Self last
Memory hook: "Some Citizens Prefer Service" (Society / Citizens/Clients / Profession / Self)
When in conflict, ALWAYS choose the higher-order canon.
🧠 (ISC)² Four Canons — "SCPS" in Priority Order:
Society (Canon I — HIGHEST)
Client/Principals (Canon II — Honorably + III — Diligent service)
Profession (Canon IV — Advance the profession)
Self (implied — last in all cases)
Memory hook: "Super Cool Professionals Serve" or "Society Comes Practically Simultaneously"
When in conflict: Society ALWAYS wins.
🧠 Which Canon Applies?
Public safety threatened? → Canon I
Need to be honest or legal? → Canon II
Need to serve client competently? → Canon III
Need to support the profession? → Canon IV
Two canons conflict? → Lower number wins (I beats II beats III beats IV)
🧠 Responsible Disclosure — "NVT-P"
Notify the vendor privately
Vendor gets time to patch (typically 90 days)
Then coordinate public disclosure
Publish if vendor fails to act
Memory hook: "Notify, Wait, Then Publish"
Day 08: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • Policies are the WHY — not the how. "The organisation will protect customer data" is a policy. Step-by-step backup instructions are a procedure.
  • ISO 27001 is certifiable; ISO 27002 is guidance/controls catalogue. You cannot be "ISO 27002 certified" — only ISO 27001.
  • NIST CSF is voluntary for the private sector but mandatory for US federal agencies under Executive Order 13800.
⚠️ ISO 27001 vs ISO 27002:
ISO 27001 = Requirements (SHALL statements) = Certifiable
ISO 27002 = Guidelines (SHOULD statements) = Not certifiable
"We are ISO 27002 certified" is an incorrect statement — no such certification exists.
⚠️ Policies Must Be Enforced:
On the exam, "issue a policy" is never the complete answer. Policies must be: communicated, trained, monitored, audited, and enforced. A policy without enforcement is worse than no policy — it demonstrates knowing failure to act.
🧠 Policy Hierarchy — "Please Send Procedures Before Guidelines":
Policy → Standard → Procedure → Baseline → Guideline
Or the old standby: "Policies, Standards, Procedures, Baselines, Guidelines"
Mandatory: Policy, Standard, Procedure, Baseline | Optional: Guideline
🧠 NIST CSF 2.0 Functions: "GI-PD-RR"
Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Memory hook: "Good Investigators Protect Detective Results Reliably"
CSF 1.1 (original): IPDRR; CSF 2.0 adds Govern at the start
🧠 Policy Hierarchy: "Policies Should Probably Be Good"
Policies (WHY — strategic, mandatory)
Standards (WHAT — specific requirements, mandatory)
Procedures (HOW — step-by-step, mandatory)
Baselines (MINIMUM config — platform-specific, mandatory)
Guidelines (SUGGESTIONS — optional best practices)
Only guidelines are optional!
🧠 NIST CSF 2.0 Functions: "GIPDRR"
Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Think: "Generally Intellectual People Don't Resist Recovery"
Govern is NEW in v2.0!
🧠 ISO 27001 vs ISO 27002:
27001 ends in 1 = 1 stop certifiable standard
27002 ends in 2 = 2nd document (the "how-to" guidance)
27001 = "ISMS Requirements" = requirements = CERTIFIABLE
27002 = "Code of Practice" = guidance = NOT certifiable
🧠 Framework Selection Memory Aid:
NIST CSF = US risk-based structure (any industry)
ISO 27001 = International certification (European preference)
COBIT = IT Governance alignment (board/management/audit)
Think: "NIST for Structure, ISO for Cert, COBIT for Governance"
Day 09: Unknown
⚠️ Most Important Exam Traps:
  • Risk cannot be eliminated — only managed. The exam may offer "eliminate the risk" as an option — it is always wrong. The correct options are mitigate, transfer, accept, or avoid.
  • Residual risk is the risk remaining AFTER controls are applied. It is always non-zero.
  • Vulnerability is not the same as a threat. A vulnerability is a weakness; a threat exploits that weakness.
⚠️ Threat ≠ Vulnerability ≠ Risk:
Threat = the aggressor or event that could cause harm
Vulnerability = the weakness that could be exploited
Risk = the probability that the threat exploits the vulnerability × impact
The exam tests this distinction repeatedly. A locked door (control) reduces risk but doesn't eliminate the threat.
🧠 Risk Relationship Chain: "TAV → Risk → Impact"
Threat exploits Vulnerability in an Asset → Creates Risk → Risk materialises → Impact
Controls break any link in this chain to reduce risk.
🧠 NIST RMF 7 Steps: "PC-SIAAM"
Prepare → Categorise → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorise → Monitor
Memory hook: "Please Consider Security Is Always A Must"
🧠 Core Risk Terms Chain: "TAV-RIC"
Threat exploits → Asset's → Vulnerability → Risk × Impact = Harm → Reduce with Controls
Or: "Threats Attack Vulnerable things, creating Risks with Impacts, Countered by controls"
🧠 Risk Cannot Be Eliminated:
Zero risk is impossible in any real system. The correct goal is risk management (reduce to acceptable level), not risk elimination. Always choose: Mitigate / Transfer / Accept / Avoid — never "eliminate."
🧠 NIST RMF Steps: "Please Consider Security Is Always A Must"
Prepare, Categorise, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorise, Monitor
🧠 Risk Appetite Hierarchy:
CAPACITY (maximum the org can survive) > TOLERANCE (acceptable variation) > APPETITE (desired level)
Think of a glass of water: Capacity = size of the glass; Tolerance = how full before it's uncomfortable; Appetite = how full you want it.
Day 10: Unknown
⚠️ Critical Exam Fact: You MUST memorise: SLE = AV × EF and ALE = SLE × ARO. These two formulas and their components are tested heavily. The exam will give you values and ask you to calculate SLE, ALE, residual ALE, or determine whether a control is worth purchasing.
⚠️ CBA Doesn't Tell the Whole Story:
Some controls are required by law or regulation regardless of CBA. A negative CBA doesn't mean "skip the control" if compliance demands it. CBA is a financial tool, not the final word on whether to implement a control.
⚠️ EF is a Decimal, Not a Whole Number:
EF = 50% should be entered as 0.50 in calculations, not as 50.
SLE = $400,000 × 0.50 = $200,000 ✓
SLE = $400,000 × 50 = $20,000,000 ✗ (wrong — 50× not 50%)
Always convert percentages to decimals before multiplying!
⚠️ ALE Is Annualised, Not Per-Event:
ALE is what you expect to LOSE PER YEAR on average. It's not what you'll lose in any single year — some years may see no events, others may see multiple. ALE is an actuarial average used for budget planning and control justification.
🧠 The Quantitative Chain — memorise left to right:
AV × EF = SLE
SLE × ARO = ALE
Full chain: AV → (×EF) → SLE → (×ARO) → ALE
Memory hook: "Assets Exposed Suffer Losses Annually"
AV × EF = SLE | SLE × ARO = ALE
🧠 The ALE Formula Chain — The Most Important CISSP Formulas:
Step 1: AV × EF = SLE (how bad is one hit?)
Step 2: SLE × ARO = ALE (how often does it happen × how bad = yearly cost)

Think of it as: Asset × Exposure → Single Loss → × Annual Rate → Annual Loss
Memory hook: "Avoid Every Single Awful Risk Always" → AV × EF = SLE × ARO = ALE
🧠 CBA Decision Rule:
ALE(before) – ALE(after) = Annual Value of Control
If Annual Value > Cost of Control → BUY THE CONTROL
If Annual Value < Cost of Control → QUESTION the investment (unless legally required)
Memory hook: "Does the control SAVE more than it COSTS?"
🧠 ARO Values to Know:
ARO = 1 → once per year
ARO = 0.5 → once every 2 years
ARO = 0.1 → once every 10 years
ARO = 10 → 10 times per year
ARO is always times/year — not a probability percentage!
Day 11: Unknown
⚠️ Qualitative Scores Are NOT Dollar Values:
A qualitative risk score of "4" does NOT mean $4 or $4 million. It means "high risk" on whatever scale your organisation uses. Organisations must define their scales clearly and consistently for qualitative analysis to be meaningful and comparable across assessments.
🧠 Delphi = "Anonymous Iterative Expert Consensus"
Key words: Anonymous (no one knows who said what) + Iterative (multiple rounds) + Expert (subject matter experts) + Consensus (goal is agreement)
Memory hook: "The Oracle at Delphi gave wisdom anonymously through repeated consultation"
Exam trigger: "consensus without group influence" → always Delphi
🧠 Risk Matrix Zones — "AMHC" (traffic light + one):
🟢 Accept → Low scores (1–4)
🟡 Monitor/Mitigate → Medium scores (5–10)
🟠 High priority mitigate → High scores (11–16)
🔴 Critical immediate action → Critical scores (17–25)
Memory: "Always Monitor High-risk Critical items"
🧠 Qualitative vs. Quantitative Quick Decision:
Need DOLLARS → Use Quantitative (ALE formulas)
Need PRIORITISATION quickly → Use Qualitative (matrix)
Need CONSENSUS without bias → Use Delphi
Need INITIAL broad scan → Use Brainstorming
Day 12: Unknown
⚠️ Top Exam Traps:
1. Risk Transfer does NOT eliminate risk or legal liability — you remain accountable
2. Risk Acceptance requires FORMAL, DOCUMENTED management decision — not inaction or neglect
3. "Risk Avoidance" means stopping the activity that creates the risk — not avoiding the risk by adding controls
⚠️ Critical: Transfer ≠ Liability Elimination
Purchasing cyber insurance or outsourcing security does NOT remove legal and regulatory accountability. An insurance policy pays money — it doesn't prevent a regulatory fine, class-action lawsuit, or reputational damage. The organisation remains the responsible party under GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, etc. "You cannot outsource accountability."
⚠️ Acceptance MUST Be Formal:
Risk acceptance should be formally documented with: (1) description of the risk, (2) why it's being accepted, (3) residual risk level, (4) signature of the accountable manager/executive, (5) review/expiry date. Informal acceptance = negligence risk.
🧠 The Four Responses — "MATA":
Mitigate → Reduce it
Accept → Live with it
Transfer → Pay someone to absorb the financial hit
Avoid → Stop doing the thing that creates it
Memory sentence: "My Attitude Toward (risk) Actually (determines outcome)"
🧠 Control Functions — "PDCRDC":
Preventive → Stop it before
Detective → Find it happening
Corrective → Fix it after
Recovery → Restore after
Deterrent → Discourage it
Compensating → Alternative when primary can't work
Memory: "Please Don't Cry — Rather Deter and Compensate"
🧠 Control Categories — "ATP":
Administrative (people + policies)
Technical (logical / systems)
Physical (real-world barriers)
Exam tiebreaker: If a control involves a policy or procedure → Administrative. If it's a device or software → Technical. If you can touch it → Physical.
🧠 Transfer vs. Accept Confusion:
Transfer = You STILL OWN the risk, you shared the FINANCIAL PAIN
Accept = You KNOW the risk, you chose to DO NOTHING (but documented it)
"Transfer your wallet, not your guilt"
Day 13: Unknown
⚠️ Top Exam Traps:
1. STRIDE is for identifying threats to software/systems — it is not a risk scoring method
2. PASTA is business/attacker-centric — often described as the most complete methodology
3. DREAD is largely deprecated in industry (Microsoft stopped using it ~2008) — but still occasionally tested
4. MITRE ATT&CK describes REAL attacker TTPs — it is not a design-time framework, it's used for detection and response
🧠 STRIDE — "STRIDE is what attackers do to violate security properties":
Spoofing → violates Authentication
Tampering → violates Integrity
Repudiation → violates Non-repudiation
Information Disclosure → violates Confidentiality
Denial of Service → violates Availability
Elevation of Privilege → violates Authorisation
Memory sentence: "Six Threats Really Irritate Diligent Experts"
Note: STRIDE maps almost perfectly to CIA Triad + Authentication + Authorisation + Non-repudiation
🧠 PASTA Stages — "DO TA VV AR":
Define objectives → Objective setting
Technical scope → Application decomposition
Vulnerability analysis → Value (threat) analysis
Attack modeling → Risk assessment
Simpler: PASTA = "Purpose: Align Technical Security to Attacker goals"
🧠 Framework Selection Quick Guide:
Software component threat identification → STRIDE
Business-risk-aligned comprehensive analysis → PASTA
Visualise compound attack paths → Attack Trees
Describe/detect real attacker TTPs → MITRE ATT&CK
Privacy-specific threats → LINDDUN

🧠 ATT&CK Hierarchy — "Tactics Tell Techniques, Procedures Prove":

Day 15: Overview
⚠️ Top Exam Traps:
1. BCP is NOT just about IT — it covers people, facilities, processes, AND technology
2. MTD ≥ RTO + WRT (always — if RTO+WRT exceeds MTD, the business fails before it recovers)
3. RPO determines backup frequency — it is NOT the same as RTO
4. Full Interruption Testing is the most realistic but also most risky — used rarely and only for mature programmes
5. Senior management must be the BCP Project Sponsor — IT cannot own this
⚠️ Senior Management Must Sponsor BCP: The BCP must have executive sponsorship — it cannot be driven solely by IT. Senior management must: commit resources, demonstrate leadership commitment, and accept residual risk. An IT-only BCP will fail the CISSP exam as the correct answer.
🧠 BCP vs. DRP — "BCP = Business Keeps Processing; DRP = Data and tech Recovered Properly":
BCP = keeps the WHOLE BUSINESS running (people, facilities, processes)
DRP = restores IT SYSTEMS after the event
DRP is a SUBSET of BCP — never the other way around
🧠 MTD vs. RTO vs. RPO — "The Three Clocks":
MTD = the DEADLINE clock — how long before the business dies (set by management)
RTO = the RECOVERY clock — how fast IT must bring systems back (must beat MTD)
RPO = the DATA LOSS clock — how far back in time can we roll (determines backup frequency)
WRT = the VALIDATION clock — time to confirm systems work after restore
Critical formula: MTD ≥ RTO + WRT — if violated, recovery plan is inadequate
🧠 BCP Lifecycle — "PI BRSD TM":
Project initiation → Impact analysis (BIA) → Business risk assessment → Strategy development → Development of plans → Testing → Maintenance
Simpler: "Please Investigate Before Strategy — Document, Test, Maintain"
🧠 BCP Test Types — In order: "Clean Tables Simulate Perfect Fires":
Checklist → Tabletop → Simulation → Parallel → Full Interruption
Each step is more disruptive AND more realistic than the last.
Only run Full Interruption when you are confident in the plan — it intentionally takes production offline.
🧠 RPO Drives Backup Frequency — easy anchor:
RPO = 24 hours → backup daily
RPO = 4 hours → backup every 4 hours
RPO = 0 → real-time synchronous replication (most expensive)
"RPO tells you how old your oldest acceptable data can be when disaster strikes"
Day 16: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: Candidates confuse RTO with MTD. RTO is the target recovery time (what you plan to achieve). MTD is the limit (how long the business can survive). RTO must always be less than MTD. Also: recovery is complete only when WRT finishes, so RTO + WRT ≤ MTD is the correct constraint.
⚠️ Common Confusion: RPO is not "how often do we back up" — it's "how much data loss can the business tolerate." Backup frequency is the mechanism to achieve the RPO requirement. The RPO requirement comes first; the backup design follows.
⚠️ Critical Risk: Single Points of Failure (SPOFs) are the most dangerous finding in a BIA. An SPOF is any person, process, system, or third party whose failure immediately impacts a critical function with no redundant path. Every SPOF identified in the BIA must be either eliminated through redundancy or explicitly accepted as residual risk by senior management.
⚠️ Exam Trap — RPO Direction: RPO is the maximum data loss measured backwards in time from the recovery point. An RPO of 4 hours means you can lose at most 4 hours of data — so the last backup must be no older than 4 hours. Many candidates read this backwards.
🧠 MTD vs RTO — The Bridge Analogy:
MTD = the bridge's weight limit (maximum before it collapses — the business cannot go beyond this).
RTO = how fast your truck (IT recovery) must cross the bridge.
Your truck (RTO) + unloading time (WRT) must finish before the bridge limit (MTD) is reached.
RTO + WRT ≤ MTD — Always.
🧠 BIA Metrics Order — "Mr. Wolf": M·R·R·W
MTD → maximum time the business can survive
RTO → time target to restore the system
RPO → oldest data acceptable on recovery
WRT → time to reconcile after restore
MRR·W = "Mr. Wolf" (he fixes things fast — just like your BCP should!)
🧠 MTBF vs MTTR:
MTBF = "**B**reaks **B**etween **F**ailures" → Higher is Better (more time between breaks)
MTTR = "**R**epair **R**apidly" → Lower is Better (fix it fast)
You WANT high MTBF and low MTTR.
🧠 Availability = MTBF ÷ (MTBF + MTTR)
"Divide the good time by all the time" — uptime divided by (uptime + downtime)

⚠️ Exam Trap: The exam often asks about the FIRST step when a disaster is declared. The answer is almost always: ensure the safety of personnel. Never start recovering systems before accounting for human safety.
⚠️ Reciprocal Agreement Weakness: Reciprocal agreements sound attractive but are rarely reliable. Both organisations may be victims of the same regional disaster; the partner may not have sufficient spare capacity; the agreement may not be regularly tested. The exam often uses reciprocal agreements as a tempting-but-wrong answer when reliability is required.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Hot Site ≠ Always Correct: Hot sites are expensive. If the business's MTD is 72 hours, a cold site may be sufficient and more cost-effective. The correct recovery strategy is the CHEAPEST one that still satisfies the MTD, RTO, and RPO requirements. Don't automatically choose hot site just because it sounds more secure.
🧠 Recovery Site Temperatures: HOT→WARM→COLD = READY→SOON→LATER
Hot = Ready now (minutes/hours) — expensive
Warm = Soon (hours/days) — medium cost
Cold = Later (days/weeks) — cheap but slow
Temperature matches urgency: hotter = faster = more expensive.

"Best Done Carefully — Otherwise Costly Indeed"

Day 18: Unknown
⚠️ Exam Trap: The exam frequently asks about the FIRST action when an employee is terminated or suspected of malicious activity. The answer almost always involves revoking access BEFORE any other action (before escorting out, before HR meeting in some models). Know the sequence.
⚠️ Critical Sequence: When an employee exits (voluntary or involuntary), the FIRST security action is ALWAYS to disable/revoke all access (logical and physical). This must happen before or simultaneously with the HR exit meeting — not after.
⚠️ Collusion Risk: SoD prevents single-person fraud but cannot prevent collusion — where two or more people cooperate to circumvent controls. Audit trails, monitoring, and job rotation help detect collusion over time. No single control eliminates all insider fraud risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Offboarding Sequence: The CISSP exam may ask about the FIRST action when a key employee resigns or is terminated. The correct answer is REVOKE ACCESS (logical and physical) — not "notify the HR department," not "conduct an exit interview," not "retrieve equipment." Access revocation is first, always.
🧠 Employment Lifecycle Controls — "HEAPS":
Hire (background screening before access)
Employment agreements (NDA, AUP, NCA)
Access (least privilege, need to know, SoD from day one)
Practice (mandatory vacations, job rotation during employment)
Separation (revoke access immediately upon departure)
🧠 SoD vs. Rotation vs. Vacation:
SoD = Prevents fraud by requiring TWO people for sensitive actions (simultaneous control)
Job Rotation = Detects fraud by REPLACING the person periodically (eventual exposure)
Mandatory Vacation = Detects fraud by REMOVING the person temporarily (absence-triggered exposure)
All three are complementary — layered fraud detection.
🧠 Least Privilege vs. Need to Know:
Least Privilege = WHAT you can DO (permissions: read, write, execute, delete)
Need to Know = WHAT you can SEE (information: this file, this project, this classification)
LP = verbs; NtK = nouns

The correct answer is almost always security awareness training (not a technical control like email filtering). Training addresses root cause; technical controls are compensating. When the scenario involves user behaviour, lean toward training as the answer.
⚠️ Ethical Consideration: Phishing simulations using highly distressing pretexts (e.g., impersonating HR to announce fake layoffs) can damage employee morale and organisational trust. The CISSP exam expects professional, ethically designed programmes. Effective awareness does not require emotional manipulation of employees.

Education — deep understanding — SECURITY PROFESSIONALS

⚠️ Remember: "Ignore" is not a valid risk response. Accepting risk is a conscious, documented decision made by management. Ignoring risk is a failure of governance. The CISSP exam will not accept "ignore" as an answer — the closest valid response is "accept" (with documented management sign-off).
⚠️ Trap 1 — Ignore ≠ Accept: "Ignore" is not a valid risk response. The correct term is "Accept" — a documented management decision to bear the risk. Don't confuse the two.
⚠️ Trap 2 — Guideline vs. Standard: Guidelines are optional recommendations; Standards are mandatory requirements. "Best practice guidance documents" are guidelines; "You MUST use AES-256" is a standard.
⚠️ Trap 3 — Due Care vs. Due Diligence: Due Care = acting (implementing controls); Due Diligence = researching and monitoring. Confusion on these terms appears frequently.

⚠️ Never Forget: Government "Confidential" = LOW tier (3rd of 3 formal levels). Commercial "Confidential" = HIGH tier (1st of 4 levels). Same word; opposite ends of their respective scales.

Confidential → Damage

O = Owner = DECIDES: classification, access rights, retention, declassification

🧠 The Six Stages — "Can Somebody Use Some Archives Daily?"
Create → Store → Use → Share → Archive → Destroy
🧠 What Protects Data In Each State?
At REST → Encryption at rest (AES-256, FDE)

"REST needs a blanket (encryption), TRANSIT needs a tunnel (TLS), USE needs a guard (access control)"

⚠️ #1 Exam Trap: Anonymisation vs. Pseudonymisation. After true anonymisation, GDPR does NOT apply. After pseudonymisation, GDPR STILL applies — the data is still considered personal data because re-identification is theoretically possible.
⚠️ Re-identification Risk: What is anonymised today may be re-identifiable tomorrow with new techniques or datasets. The Netflix Prize dataset (anonymised by removing names) was partially re-identified by researchers who combined it with public IMDB reviews. True anonymisation requires ongoing assessment.
🧠 Anonymisation vs. Pseudonymisation — The GDPR Test:
Anonymisation = NO identity link possible → GDPR does NOT apply
Pseudonymisation = Identity COULD be restored → GDPR STILL applies
"Anon = Gone. Pseudo = Still there (but hidden)."
🧠 Masking vs. Tokenisation — "Format vs. Vault":
Masking = Visual hiding — show only partial data (****-1234) — original data unchanged in storage
Tokenisation = Replacement — store random token, original value separated into vault
"Masking hides the view; Tokenisation changes the value"

The default is maximum protection. Users choose to share — not choose not to share.

⚠️ Exam Trap: When litigation is "reasonably anticipated," the legal hold obligation activates — even before formal legal proceedings begin. Destroying data after this point is called spoliation and can result in severe legal sanctions including adverse inference instructions to a jury.
⚠️ Spoliation: The intentional or negligent destruction, alteration, or withholding of evidence that is subject to a legal hold. Penalties include: adverse inference jury instructions (jury told to assume destroyed evidence was unfavourable), monetary sanctions, dismissal of claims, default judgement against the spoliating party, and potential criminal obstruction of justice charges.
⚠️ Degaussing Limitation: Degaussing ONLY works on magnetic media. It has absolutely no effect on SSDs, USB flash drives, or optical discs (CDs/DVDs). A degaussed SSD may still contain all its data intact. This is a heavily tested CISSP exam point.
🧠 Regulatory Retention Numbers:
SOX = 7 years ("Seven-OX")
HIPAA = 6 years ("HIPAA has 5 letters, +1 = 6")
PCI audit logs = 1 year (3 months hot / 9 months warm)
Remember: "SOX=7, HIPAA=6, PCI=1"

"Anticipated = Activated" — The hold activates when litigation is reasonably anticipated, not when the lawsuit is filed. Destroying data after the anticipation point = Spoliation.
🧠 Degaussing Limitation:
"Degaussing only DEMAGNETISES" — It only affects magnetic media (spinning HDDs, tapes).
SSD = flash memory = no magnetism → Degaussing does NOTHING to an SSD.

⚠️ Important Distinction: Watermarking does NOT prevent unauthorised copying or distribution. It identifies the source after the fact. For preventing distribution, use DRM/IRM. For proving source after a breach, use watermarking. These are complementary controls used together.

Watermarking = REVEALS the source — proves who leaked it, after the fact
"DRM stops the leak; Watermark names the leaker"
