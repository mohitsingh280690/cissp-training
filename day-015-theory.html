2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Every organisation — regardless of size — faces disruption events: hardware failures, ransomware, natural disasters, power outages, pandemics, or key personnel loss. Without a tested BCP, organisations improvise under pressure, leading to extended downtime, permanent data loss, regulatory penalties, and potentially fatal business damage.

COVID-19 (2020) exposed thousands of organisations that had BCP documents "on the shelf" but had never tested them or considered pandemic scenarios. Organisations that had tested remote working capabilities recovered within days; others took months to adapt.

The BCP connects directly to organisational risk appetite — the frequency of backups (RPO) and speed of recovery (RTO) must be funded at a level that reflects the business's declared tolerance for downtime.

3. BCP vs. DRP – Critical Distinction

AttributeBCP (Business Continuity Plan)DRP (Disaster Recovery Plan)
ScopeENTIRE organisation — people, processes, facilities, technology, communicationsIT systems and data restoration
GoalKeep business OPERATING during and after a disruptionRESTORE IT systems after a disaster
TimingDuring the disaster (keeping things running)After the disaster (restoring from failure)
Who Owns ItSenior business management (Executive Sponsor)IT management / CTO
CoversWorkforce, facilities, alternate sites, communications, suppliers, manual workaroundsBackup systems, alternate data centres, recovery time objectives, data replication
RelationshipBCP is broader; DRP is a subset/component of BCPDRP supports BCP by ensuring IT recovery meets business continuity requirements
Key Mental Model:
BCP = "How do we KEEP THE BUSINESS RUNNING even if our primary facilities and systems are unavailable?"
DRP = "How do we GET THE SYSTEMS BACK once the disaster event is over?"
BCP is proactive/concurrent; DRP is reactive/sequential.

4. BCP Lifecycle

Phase 1: Project Initiation

Obtain senior management commitment and executive sponsorship. Define scope (which business units, processes, and systems are in scope). Establish the BCP team. Allocate budget. Define project plan.

Phase 2: Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Identify critical business functions. Determine MTD, RTO, RPO, and WRT for each. Understand financial, operational, legal, and reputational impact of various outage durations. This is the most important phase — everything else derives from the BIA.

Phase 3: Risk Assessment

Identify threats and vulnerabilities that could cause a disruption. Assess likelihood and impact using the qualitative/quantitative methods from Days 9–11. Identify risks not adequately covered by existing controls.

Phase 4: Strategy Development

Develop continuity strategies for each critical function: alternate sites (hot/warm/cold), manual workarounds, data replication, remote work capabilities, alternative supply chain arrangements, communications plans.

Phase 5: Plan Development

Document the BCP in detail: activation criteria, escalation procedures, roles and responsibilities, contact lists, recovery procedures per function, communication scripts, resource lists.

Phase 6: Testing and Exercise

Test the plan using progressively more rigorous testing methods (tabletop → walkthrough → simulation → parallel → full interruption). Identify gaps and update the plan.

Phase 7: Maintenance

BCP is a living document. Review and update after: significant organisational changes, technology changes, after any activation, and at minimum annually. Integrate with change management processes.

5. Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Business Impact Analysis (BIA): The process of identifying the organisation's critical business functions, the resources required to support them, and the financial/operational/legal/reputational impact of disrupting those functions over time. The BIA is the foundation of all BCP strategy and investment decisions.

BIA Process Steps

  1. Identify all business processes/functions
  2. Identify the resources required (IT, people, facilities, suppliers) for each function
  3. Determine dependencies between functions
  4. Assess the impact of disruption at various time intervals (1 hour, 1 day, 1 week, 1 month)
  5. Determine the MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime) for each function
  6. Define the RTO and RPO targets consistent with business impact findings
  7. Prioritise recovery order based on criticality and dependencies

BIA Impact Categories

Impact TypeExamples
FinancialLost revenue per hour; contractual penalties; customer refunds; market share loss
OperationalInability to serve customers; supply chain disruption; capacity loss
Legal/RegulatorySLA breach penalties; regulatory fines; reporting obligation violations
ReputationalMedia coverage; customer confidence loss; brand damage; competitive disadvantage

6. BIA Metrics — MTD, RTO, RPO, WRT

These four metrics are the most heavily tested BCP concepts on the CISSP exam. Understanding their definitions AND their mathematical relationship is essential.

MTD — Maximum Tolerable Downtime (also called MTO — Maximum Tolerable Outage): The absolute maximum time a business function can be unavailable before the impact becomes catastrophic and potentially fatal to the organisation. Set by senior business management.
RTO — Recovery Time Objective: The target time to restore a system or process to operational status after a disruption. Must be less than MTD. Defines how fast IT must restore systems.
RPO — Recovery Point Objective: The maximum amount of data loss that is acceptable, measured in time. The RPO determines how frequently backups or data replication must occur. Example: RPO = 4 hours means at most 4 hours of data can be lost.
WRT — Work Recovery Time: The time required to verify recovered systems are functioning correctly and to restore business processes after the systems have been brought back online. Includes data validation, testing, queue clearing, and business process resumption.

Critical Relationship

MTD ≥ RTO + WRT Where: RTO = time to restore systems technically WRT = time to verify and resume business operations MTD = total tolerable downtime limit set by business If (RTO + WRT) > MTD → BUSINESS IMPACT IS FATAL Recovery must be faster than the business can tolerate being down.
Example:
Payroll system: MTD = 48 hours
RTO target = 24 hours (IT restores system)
WRT = 16 hours (verify data integrity, reconcile transactions, validate payroll runs)
RTO + WRT = 40 hours < 48 hours MTD ✅ — recovery plan is viable

If IT sets RTO = 36 hours: 36 + 16 = 52 hours > 48 hours MTD ❌ — plan must be accelerated

RPO — Backup Frequency Implication

RPO ValueRequired Backup FrequencyTechnology Solution
24 hoursDaily backup minimumNightly full backup to tape/disk
4 hoursEvery 4 hoursIncremental backups + transaction logs
1 hourContinuous or hourlyDatabase log shipping; incremental snapshots
MinutesNear-real-timeSynchronous or asynchronous replication
Zero (0)Real-time synchronous replicationExpensive — mirrored writes to both sites simultaneously

⚠️ Senior Management Must Sponsor BCP: The BCP must have executive sponsorship — it cannot be driven solely by IT. Senior management must: commit resources, demonstrate leadership commitment, and accept residual risk. An IT-only BCP will fail the CISSP exam as the correct answer.

8. BCP Testing Types

BCP testing runs in order of increasing disruption risk and realism:

Test TypeDescriptionDisruption RiskRealism
Checklist ReviewTeam members review the plan individually and identify gaps, outdated contacts, or incorrect proceduresNoneVery Low
Tabletop Exercise / Structured WalkthroughTeam walks through a hypothetical scenario verbally around a table; discusses responses without any system activationNoneLow
Simulation TestMore detailed tabletop that simulates realistic decision-making; may use role-play, injects, and time pressure; no actual system activationMinimalModerate
Parallel TestAlternate recovery systems (e.g., DR site) are brought online and tested while primary systems remain in production — both run simultaneouslyLow — primary still onlineHigh
Full Interruption TestPrimary systems are intentionally taken offline and the organisation actually operates from the recovery site — most realistic but highest riskHigh — production disruptionVery High
💡 Exam Test Sequence — "Worst First for Risk, Best First for Realism":
Checklist → Tabletop → Simulation → Parallel → Full Interruption
Least disruptive → Most disruptive
Least realistic → Most realistic
Best practice: Start with tabletop; progress to parallel before attempting full interruption.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 BCP vs. DRP — "BCP = Business Keeps Processing; DRP = Data and tech Recovered Properly":
BCP = keeps the WHOLE BUSINESS running (people, facilities, processes)
DRP = restores IT SYSTEMS after the event
DRP is a SUBSET of BCP — never the other way around
🧠 MTD vs. RTO vs. RPO — "The Three Clocks":
MTD = the DEADLINE clock — how long before the business dies (set by management)
RTO = the RECOVERY clock — how fast IT must bring systems back (must beat MTD)
RPO = the DATA LOSS clock — how far back in time can we roll (determines backup frequency)
WRT = the VALIDATION clock — time to confirm systems work after restore
Critical formula: MTD ≥ RTO + WRT — if violated, recovery plan is inadequate
🧠 BCP Lifecycle — "PI BRSD TM":
Project initiation → Impact analysis (BIA) → Business risk assessment → Strategy development → Development of plans → Testing → Maintenance
Simpler: "Please Investigate Before Strategy — Document, Test, Maintain"
🧠 BCP Test Types — In order: "Clean Tables Simulate Perfect Fires":
Checklist → Tabletop → Simulation → Parallel → Full Interruption
Each step is more disruptive AND more realistic than the last.
Only run Full Interruption when you are confident in the plan — it intentionally takes production offline.
🧠 RPO Drives Backup Frequency — easy anchor:
RPO = 24 hours → backup daily
RPO = 4 hours → backup every 4 hours
RPO = 0 → real-time synchronous replication (most expensive)
"RPO tells you how old your oldest acceptable data can be when disaster strikes"
