2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Organizational Ownership

Security governance is owned at the Board and C-suite level: the Board sets risk appetite, the CEO is ultimately accountable, the CISO develops and implements the security program, and the CIO aligns IT strategy. This is not an IT function — it is a business function.

Implementation Reality

In mature organisations, security governance manifests as a Security Steering Committee — a group that includes the CISO, CIO, CFO, CLO (Chief Legal Officer), and business unit heads. This committee meets regularly to review risk posture, approve major security investments, and ensure security strategy supports business objectives.

Governance documents include: Information Security Policy, Risk Management Framework, Security Charter, and Acceptable Use Policies — all requiring senior management sign-off, not just IT approval.

Failure Modes

  • Security treated as an IT problem: When the CISO reports only to the CIO (rather than CEO or Board), security becomes subordinate to IT operational goals. Budget is cut when systems need upgrading; risk escalations die in middle management.
  • Equifax (2017): Governance failure — the Board had no dedicated cyber committee; the CISO reported to the CIO, not the CEO. A known vulnerability (Apache Struts) went unpatched for months because no governance mechanism enforced patching SLAs. Result: 147 million records compromised.

Business Impact

Good governance provides the authority, accountability, and resources needed for security to function. Without board-level governance, security is always underfunded, under-prioritised, and under-enforced. Auditors, regulators, and cyber insurers all assess governance maturity as a first-order signal of organisational security posture.

3. What is Security Governance?

Security Governance: The set of responsibilities, practices, policies, and procedures exercised by an organisation's leadership to provide strategic direction, ensure objectives are achieved, manage risk appropriately, and verify that organisational resources are used responsibly.

Security governance is the top-down framework that determines how security decisions are made and enforced. It sits above operations — it is not about configuring firewalls; it is about deciding what kind of security posture the organisation will maintain and why.

Governance vs. Management vs. Operations

LevelFocusExampleWho
GovernanceDirection & oversight — what should we achieve?Board approves risk appetite; CISO establishes security strategyBoard, C-suite, CISO
ManagementPlanning & control — how do we achieve it?Security manager develops policy; assigns budget; monitors KPIsCISO, Security Manager, Department Heads
OperationsExecution — doing it day-to-daySOC analyst responds to alerts; sysadmin applies patchesIT staff, Security Analysts, Administrators
Key Principle: Security governance provides three things: (1) Authority — security decisions are backed by executive power; (2) Accountability — specific people are responsible for specific outcomes; (3) Alignment — security objectives support business objectives, not fight them.

4. Aligning Security with Business Strategy

One of the most important governance principles is that security must enable the business, not merely restrict it. Security that makes the business impossible to run will be bypassed or cut. Effective CISOs understand the business model and align security investments with business-critical assets and processes.

The Security-Business Alignment Model

Business Objectives │ ▼ Business Strategy (3–5 year goals) │ ▼ IT Strategy (how technology supports business strategy) │ ▼ Security Strategy (how security enables IT and business strategy) │ ▼ Security Program (policies, controls, people, processes, technology)

If security strategy is built bottom-up (starting with technology and building up), it is often misaligned. If built top-down (starting from business objectives), it naturally maps to what matters most.

Concrete Alignment Examples

Business GoalSecurity ImplicationSecurity Investment
Launch SaaS product in EUMust comply with GDPRData classification, DPO role, privacy-by-design in SDLC
Process credit card paymentsMust comply with PCI-DSSEncryption, network segmentation, quarterly scans
M&A — acquire a companyMust assess acquired company's security postureSecurity due diligence, integration risk assessment
Enable remote work globallyExpanded attack surface; untrusted networksZero Trust architecture, MFA, endpoint security

5. Organizational Processes That Touch Security

Security governance must be embedded into major organisational processes — it cannot be bolted on afterwards.

Acquisitions (M&A)

When an organisation acquires another, it inherits the acquired company's security posture, liabilities, and vulnerabilities. Key security activities during M&A:

  • Security due diligence: Understand the target's vulnerabilities, data assets, regulatory obligations, and outstanding incidents before completing the deal.
  • Integration risk assessment: Connecting two networks without proper assessment can expose both organisations to significant risk.
  • Intellectual property protection: Ensure the acquired IP (patents, trade secrets, source code) is properly classified and protected during transition.

Divestitures

When selling a division or subsidiary, the organisation must ensure that data does not travel with the sold entity unless legally required. This includes:

  • Data destruction certificates for customer data that belongs to the parent company
  • Account deprovisioning for divested employees
  • Review of shared system access that may need to be split

Governance Committees

Formal governance bodies that exist in mature organisations:

6. Security Roles & Responsibilities

The CISSP exam is heavily focused on who owns what in a security governance structure. These roles must be understood precisely.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Data Owner is NOT IT: The data owner is always a business person — a VP, department head, or senior manager. The IT department (custodian) holds and protects the data but does NOT decide who can access it or how sensitive it is. Never confuse these roles.

The RACI Model in Security

RACI Matrix: A responsibility assignment matrix defining who is Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome), Consulted (provides input), and Informed (receives updates) for each task or decision.

In security governance, a task like "classify a new data set" would be: R = Data Steward (does the work); A = Data Owner (owns the classification decision); C = Legal, IT Security (consulted); I = CISO, IT Custodian (informed of the decision).

7. Governance Frameworks

Organisations do not design governance from scratch. Established frameworks provide structure, vocabulary, and best-practice guidance.

COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies)

COBIT: A framework published by ISACA for IT governance and management. It maps IT goals to business goals and provides a comprehensive set of control objectives.
  • Provides best practices for aligning IT with business objectives
  • Widely used for audit and compliance purposes (SOX audits often reference COBIT)
  • Current version: COBIT 2019; previous widely-used version: COBIT 5
  • Defines five governance principles: Meeting stakeholder needs; Covering the enterprise end-to-end; Applying a single integrated framework; Enabling a holistic approach; Separating governance from management

ISO/IEC 27001 and 27002

StandardPurpose
ISO 27001Specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and improving an ISMS (Information Security Management System). Organisations can be certified against ISO 27001.
ISO 27002Provides guidance and best-practice controls (not certifiable — it's a reference). Lists 93 controls in 4 themes: Organizational, People, Physical, Technological.
💡 Key Distinction: ISO 27001 is the certifiable standard (auditors check conformance). ISO 27002 is the guidance document (a handbook of controls to draw from). You comply with 27001; you implement 27002.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

Developed by NIST for US critical infrastructure but widely adopted globally. Six core functions (updated in CSF 2.0, February 2024) provide a management-level view of security activities:

🧠 NIST CSF Functions: Govern → Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
Mnemonic: "GI Protects Dangerous Roads Rapidly"
FunctionWhat It Covers
GovernOrganisational context, risk management strategy, roles/responsibilities, policy, oversight, supply chain risk (new in CSF 2.0)
IdentifyAsset management, business environment, risk assessment, improvement
ProtectAccess control, awareness training, data security, processes/procedures, maintenance, protective technology
DetectAnomalies/events, continuous monitoring, detection processes
RespondResponse planning, communications, analysis, mitigation, improvements
RecoverRecovery planning, improvements, communications

8. Strategic, Tactical, and Operational Planning

Security planning occurs at three time horizons, each with different ownership and focus:

LevelTime HorizonOwnerFocusExample
Strategic3–5 yearsCISO / BoardLong-term direction, alignment with business strategy, risk appetite"We will achieve ISO 27001 certification and move to Zero Trust architecture by 2028"
Tactical6–18 monthsSecurity Manager / Department HeadsProjects and programmes to implement strategic goals"Deploy MFA across all 12,000 employees in Q1; migrate to SASE by Q3"
OperationalDay-to-daySecurity Analysts / IT StaffExecuting procedures, responding to events, maintaining controlsDaily vulnerability scan review; responding to phishing alerts; patching servers weekly
💡 Exam Tip: When a question describes a 5-year security roadmap with executive sign-off, that is strategic. A project to deploy encryption technology this quarter is tactical. A help desk analyst resetting passwords is operational. The distinction often appears in role-based questions.

9. Due Care and Due Diligence

These two concepts appear throughout Domain 1 (and in legal contexts in Domain 1's law topics). They are frequently confused.

Due Care: Doing what a reasonable and prudent person would do in the same circumstances. It is the active exercise of responsible behaviour — taking the expected protective action. Also called the prudent man rule.
Due Diligence: The continuous effort to understand and manage risk; verifying that the right things are being done and that protective actions are actually working. It is the ongoing investigation and verification of security practices.
ConceptNatureExample
Due CareAction — doing somethingPatching known vulnerabilities within SLA; encrypting customer data; training employees on phishing
Due DiligenceVerification — checking it's workingRunning vulnerability scans to confirm patching worked; auditing encryption implementation; reviewing phishing simulation results
🧠 Due Care vs. Due Diligence:
Due Care = Completion of action — "I cared enough to do it"
Due Diligence = Deep Dig to verify — "I dug deep to verify it works"

Memory hook: "Care = Act; Diligence = Check"
Legal Significance: Organisations that exercise due care and due diligence can often reduce legal liability when breaches occur. Failure to exercise due care ("we knew about the vulnerability and did nothing") is the basis for negligence claims. Providing evidence of due diligence ("we patched it and audited the patch") demonstrates a reasonable security program.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Governance vs. Management vs. Operations:
Governance = Guides (sets direction, Board level)
Management = Maps (plans how to get there, CISO/Managers)
Operations = Operates (does the work, analysts/admins)
"Go Make Operations happen"
🧠 Data Owner vs. Custodian vs. Steward:
Owner = the boss — decides classification, approves access (a VP, not IT)
Custodian = the janitor — keeps it clean and safe (IT sysadmin)
Steward = the librarian — ensures data quality and accurate cataloguing
Memory hook: "Owner Decides, Custodian Guards, Steward Cleans"
🧠 Strategic / Tactical / Operational:
Strategic = Sky view (5 years, where are we going?)
Tactical = Treetop view (1 year, how do we get there?)
Operational = On the ground (today, doing the work)
Memory hook: "Sky, Treetop, Ground"
🧠 NIST CSF — I Protect Dangerous Roads Rarely:
Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover
⚠️ Exam Trap — Who signs off on security policy?
Security policies must be signed off by senior management, not the CISO alone. The CISO develops and recommends; senior management (CEO, Board, or equivalent) approves. This grants the policy authority to be enforced.
⚠️ Exam Trap — ISO 27001 vs. ISO 27002:
ISO 27001 is certifiable (you get audited against it). ISO 27002 is a guidance document (a list of controls to reference). The exam will test this distinction.
