2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Ethics Violations in Security — Real Consequences

  • Edward Snowden (2013): NSA contractor who leaked classified surveillance programmes. Whether viewed as whistleblower or criminal, this case illustrates the tension between loyalty to employer/government, public interest obligations, and personal ethics.
  • Aaron Swartz (2012): Hacker/activist who downloaded millions of JSTOR academic articles; faced decades of federal prison time. Raised questions about proportional legal response and the ethics of information access.
  • Volkswagen Emissions Scandal (2015): Engineers installed defeat device software to cheat emissions tests. Classic case of professionals following unethical orders — resulted in billions in fines and criminal convictions.

Ethics Programs in Organizations

Organisations establish codes of ethics, ethics hotlines, and ethics officers (Chief Ethics & Compliance Officer) to create a culture where ethical violations are reported and addressed. Security professionals are uniquely positioned — they have privileged access to systems and data, making their ethical behaviour especially critical.

3. Why Ethics Matters in Security

Security professionals have extraordinary power: root access to production systems, the ability to monitor all network traffic, access to highly confidential data, and knowledge of vulnerabilities. This power creates profound ethical obligations:

  • Knowledge of vulnerabilities: Should a researcher who discovers a zero-day exploit immediately disclose it publicly, or notify the vendor first (responsible disclosure)?
  • Monitoring capabilities: May an administrator read employee emails while investigating a leak? What about after hours?
  • Privacy: Must a security analyst who discovers personal health information during an investigation report it or discard the knowledge?
  • Loyalty conflicts: What if an employer asks you to do something that violates the law or harms the public?
Why Security Ethics is Different: Most professionals (doctors, lawyers) have well-established ethical codes with centuries of development. Information security ethics is still maturing. The (ISC)² Code, CEI Ten Commandments, and RFC 1087 are among the foundational references, but the ethical challenges in security evolve faster than formal codes can keep up.

4. (ISC)² Code of Professional Ethics

All (ISC)² members and certified professionals must commit to the (ISC)² Code of Ethics. The code has a preamble and four mandatory canons, listed in priority order (first takes precedence when conflicts arise).

The Preamble

"The safety and welfare of society and the common good, duty to our principals, and to each other, requires that we adhere, and be seen to adhere, to the highest ethical standards of behavior. Therefore, strict adherence to this Code is a condition of certification."

I
Protect Society, the Common Good, Necessary Public Trust and Confidence, and the Infrastructure

This is the highest obligation. A CISSP's first duty is to society — not to the employer, client, or self. If an action harms society or public safety, it must be refused or reported, even if it benefits the employer.

II
Act Honorably, Honestly, Justly, Responsibly, and Legally

Tell the truth. Disclose potential conflicts of interest. Provide diligent and competent service. Respect intellectual property rights. Avoid illegal actions. Preserve the value of the profession.

III
Provide Diligent and Competent Service to Principals

A "principal" is anyone you have a security relationship with — employer, client, or both. Provide diligent (thorough, committed) and competent (qualified, skilled) service. Do not misrepresent your qualifications. Maintain and grow your competencies through CPE.

IV
Advance and Protect the Profession

Advance the profession through education, mentoring, and peer support. Do not bring dishonour to the profession — report other members who violate the Code to (ISC)². Support the community of security professionals.

🧠 Four Canons Priority Order: "Society, Client, Profession, Self"
Society first → Client second → Profession third → Self last
Memory hook: "Some Citizens Prefer Service" (Society / Citizens/Clients / Profession / Self)
When in conflict, ALWAYS choose the higher-order canon.

5. Computer Ethics Institute – Ten Commandments

The Computer Ethics Institute (CEI) developed the "Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics" as a straightforward guide to ethical computer use. Adopted widely in academic and professional settings.

  1. Thou shalt not use a computer to harm other people.
  2. Thou shalt not interfere with other people's computer work.
  3. Thou shalt not snoop around in other people's computer files.
  4. Thou shalt not use a computer to steal.
  5. Thou shalt not use a computer to bear false witness.
  6. Thou shalt not copy or use proprietary software for which you have not paid.
  7. Thou shalt not use other people's computer resources without authorisation or proper compensation.
  8. Thou shalt not appropriate other people's intellectual output.
  9. Thou shalt think about the social consequences of the program you are writing or the system you are designing.
  10. Thou shalt always use a computer in ways that ensure consideration and respect for your fellow humans.
💡 For the Exam: You are unlikely to need to recall all 10 commandments verbatim. However, you should know this document exists, what organisation produced it (Computer Ethics Institute), and that commandments 9 and 10 are the most "systems thinking" ones — reflecting the responsibility to consider downstream social consequences of security decisions.

6. RFC 1087 – Ethics and the Internet

RFC 1087 (January 1989) — "Ethics and the Internet" — issued by the Internet Activities Board (IAB), predecessor to the Internet Architecture Board. While technically old, it remains the foundational statement of internet ethics.

RFC 1087 identifies activities considered unethical and unacceptable on the internet:

  • Any activity that purposely seeks to gain unauthorised access to internet resources
  • Disruption of the intended use of the internet
  • Wasting resources (people, capacity, computer) through such actions
  • Destroying the integrity of computer-based information
  • Compromising the privacy of users
  • Negligently breaking the security of any network
Historical Context: RFC 1087 was written when the internet was a research and academic network. Its principles pre-date commercial internet, yet they remain foundational. The CISSP exam may reference it to test awareness of the historical ethical foundations of internet security.

7. Ethical Frameworks and Theories

Understanding philosophical frameworks helps reason through novel ethical dilemmas not explicitly covered by any code:

FrameworkCore PrincipleSecurity Application
Consequentialism / UtilitarianismChoose actions that maximise total welfare/happiness; greatest good for the greatest numberDisabling a system affecting 100 users to prevent a breach affecting millions of customers
Deontological / Duty-Based EthicsCertain actions are inherently right or wrong regardless of consequences; follow rules/dutiesNever disclose a client's confidential data even if doing so might prevent a hypothetical future harm
Virtue EthicsFocus on character — act as a person of good character would act"What would an ethical, competent, honest security professional do?"; forms basis of professional conduct standards
Social Contract TheoryIndividuals give up some freedom in exchange for social benefits; social norms have moral weightRespecting AUPs and network usage policies as social contracts; not abusing privileged access
CISSP Exam Mindset is Consequentialist + Rule-Based: Exam questions generally reward choices that protect the most people, follow established rules/procedures, and prioritise transparency (reporting issues) over personal interest. "What decision best protects the organisation and public?" is usually the right framing.

8. Ethics in Security Practice

Responsible Disclosure (Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure)

When a security researcher discovers a vulnerability, current ethical consensus (and often legal requirement) is:

  1. Notify the vendor privately with details of the vulnerability
  2. Give the vendor reasonable time to develop a patch (commonly 90 days — Google Project Zero's standard)
  3. Coordinate public disclosure with the vendor
  4. If the vendor fails to act, disclose publicly after the deadline

This differs from full immediate public disclosure (which maximises vendor accountability but may enable exploitation before a patch exists) and non-disclosure (which protects no one).

Ethics of Surveillance and Privacy

  • Monitoring employee communications is legal in many jurisdictions but is ethically complex; must be disclosed in Acceptable Use Policies and employment agreements
  • Security analysts who access data beyond the scope of their investigation are violating the (ISC)² code even if technically authorised by their role
  • The principle of minimum necessary applies not just legally (HIPAA Privacy Rule) but ethically

Whistleblowing

When an employee discovers that an organisation is committing illegal acts or creating serious harm, the ethical response under the (ISC)² Code prioritises Canon I (protection of society) over Canon III (service to principals). However, the CISSP standard is to:

  1. Report internally through appropriate channels first (ethics hotline, legal, board)
  2. Document all attempts to raise the issue internally
  3. If internal reporting fails and the harm is serious enough, escalate to appropriate external authorities
  4. Never destroy evidence (even if instructed by management)

9. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 (ISC)² Four Canons — "SCPS" in Priority Order:
Society (Canon I — HIGHEST)
Client/Principals (Canon II — Honorably + III — Diligent service)
Profession (Canon IV — Advance the profession)
Self (implied — last in all cases)
Memory hook: "Super Cool Professionals Serve" or "Society Comes Practically Simultaneously"
When in conflict: Society ALWAYS wins.
🧠 Which Canon Applies?
Public safety threatened? → Canon I
Need to be honest or legal? → Canon II
Need to serve client competently? → Canon III
Need to support the profession? → Canon IV
Two canons conflict? → Lower number wins (I beats II beats III beats IV)
🧠 Responsible Disclosure — "NVT-P"
Notify the vendor privately
Vendor gets time to patch (typically 90 days)
Then coordinate public disclosure
Publish if vendor fails to act
Memory hook: "Notify, Wait, Then Publish"
⚠️ "Obey your employer" is NOT a CISSP ethic:
The exam will present scenarios where following your employer's instruction conflicts with society's interest. Always choose to protect society first. "My boss told me to do it" is not a valid ethical defence.
⚠️ CEI vs (ISC)² vs RFC 1087:
These are three DIFFERENT ethics documents from three different bodies:
• (ISC)² Code = mandatory for CISSP certification holders; 4 canons
• Computer Ethics Institute = 10 Commandments; academic/general use
• RFC 1087 = IAB internet ethics statement; historical foundation
Both CEI and RFC 1087 appear on the exam as knowledge references, but (ISC)² Code is the primary professional obligation.
