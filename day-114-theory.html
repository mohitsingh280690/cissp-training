2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Many organizations create DR plans but never test them. When disaster strikes, untested plans routinely fail — contact lists are outdated, procedures reference decommissioned systems, staff don't know their roles. Regular DR testing transforms a document into an operational capability. The 2017 British Airways data center outage caused 75,000 passengers to be stranded; their DR procedures were found inadequate after going years without a realistic test.

4. Detailed Comparison of Test Types

Checklist (Desk Check) Review

  • Individual activity — no group meeting required
  • Verifies plan completeness: are all systems listed? contacts current? procedures documented?
  • Does NOT validate that the plan actually works
  • Good starting point for new or significantly updated plans

Structured Walk-Through (Tabletop Exercise)

  • Group discussion — team gathers and talks through scenarios
  • Identifies logical gaps, confusion about roles, and planning assumptions
  • No actual systems are activated
  • Cost-effective way to identify plan weaknesses before more expensive tests
  • Often facilitated by a moderator who presents escalating scenarios
Tabletop vs Simulation: Tabletop = discussion only ("what would you do if..."). Simulation = role-playing with actions ("show me what you would do") but still no actual system activation. Both are paper-based in that production systems are not affected.

Simulation Test

  • Specific scenario is enacted (e.g., "building is on fire at 2 PM Tuesday")
  • Participants perform their roles — make calls, reference procedures, make decisions
  • May include practice notifications, assembly at rally points
  • Does NOT involve actual system recovery or site activation

Parallel Test

  • Alternate site is actually activated and systems recovered there
  • Primary site continues normal operations — no business disruption
  • Validates that recovery procedures actually work technically
  • Can verify RTO achievement (did we recover within target time?)
  • Most realistic test that does NOT risk production
Parallel Test — The Sweet Spot: A parallel test proves that recovery procedures work (systems actually restored at alternate site) without risking production operations (primary stays running). This is the most realistic test that most organizations can justify doing regularly. It's also how you validate RTO — "Can we restore within our target time?"

Full Interruption Test

  • Primary site is actually shut down
  • ALL operations transfer to alternate site
  • Provides most realistic validation — proves the plan works end-to-end
  • Highest risk — if recovery fails, business operations are disrupted for real
  • Requires executive approval due to risk
  • Rarely performed except by organizations with the highest maturity
⚠️ Full Interruption = Most Realistic but Most Dangerous: This is the only test that truly proves your DR plan works under real conditions. However, failure means actual business disruption. Most organizations never perform full interruption tests due to risk. The exam will test whether you know this is the most realistic AND most risky option.

5. Test Planning & Execution

ElementDescription
ScopeWhich systems, teams, and scenarios will be tested
ObjectivesSpecific goals — e.g., recover email within 4-hour RTO; verify backup integrity
ScheduleWhen the test occurs; avoid peak business periods for parallel/full interruption
ParticipantsAll relevant DR team members and stakeholders
Success criteriaHow success/failure will be measured (e.g., systems operational within RTO)
Rollback planHow to return to normal if the test causes issues (especially for parallel/full interruption)
Observer/evaluatorIndependent observer documenting what works and what doesn't
Progressive Testing Strategy: Organizations should progress through test types over time: Start with checklist → tabletop → simulation → parallel → (optionally) full interruption. Each level builds on lessons from the previous level. Never jump straight to full interruption without first validating through less disruptive tests.

6. DR Plan Maintenance

Triggers for Plan Update

  • After every DR test — incorporate lessons learned
  • Organizational changes — new systems, staff turnover, facility changes
  • Technology changes — system upgrades, cloud migrations, new applications
  • Regulatory changes — new compliance requirements
  • After any actual incident — real events reveal gaps
  • Scheduled review — at minimum annually, preferably quarterly

Maintenance Activities

  • Update contact lists (people change roles/leave — most common plan failure)
  • Review and update recovery procedures for changed systems
  • Verify alternate site readiness
  • Confirm vendor contracts and SLAs are current
  • Version control the plan — document all changes with dates
  • Distribute updated copies to all plan holders

8. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Five DR Test Types — "Can The Students Pass Finals?"
Checklist (desk check) — individual review
Tabletop (structured walk-through) — group discussion
Simulation — role-play scenario
Parallel — recover at alternate site, primary stays running
Full interruption — shut down primary, operate from alternate
🧠 Risk/Realism Scale
"As you go down the list (C→T→S→P→F), BOTH risk AND realism INCREASE"
Checklist = zero risk, lowest realism
Full interruption = highest risk, highest realism
🧠 Parallel vs Full Interruption — "Parallel = Primary stays UP; Full = Primary goes DOWN"
Parallel: recovery site activated + primary continues = safe
Full: primary shut down + recovery site takes over = dangerous
