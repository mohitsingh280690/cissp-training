2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Organizational Ownership

GDPR responsibility sits at the C-suite level. The Data Protection Officer (DPO) reports to the highest management level. Legal, IT, HR, and marketing all handle personal data — making GDPR a cross-functional governance requirement, not just an IT compliance task.

Implementation Reality

In practice, organizations must maintain a Record of Processing Activities (ROPA), appoint a DPO (if required), establish lawful bases for each processing activity, execute Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with every processor, and implement technical/organizational measures (TOMs) like encryption, access controls, and pseudonymization.

Failure Modes

  • Meta/Facebook (2023): €1.2 billion fine for transferring EU data to the US without adequate safeguards after the Schrems II ruling invalidated Privacy Shield.
  • British Airways (2020): £20 million fine for failing to protect customer data — the controller was held accountable even though technical implementation was delegated.
  • Organizations that treat GDPR as a checkbox exercise (one-time compliance) rather than ongoing accountability repeatedly fail audits when processing activities change without updated DPIAs or DPAs.

Business Impact

Maximum fines: €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher) for the most serious violations. Beyond fines, GDPR enforcement creates reputational damage, customer trust erosion, and operational disruption from enforcement orders that can halt data processing entirely.

3. GDPR Overview & Core Principles

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation): EU Regulation 2016/679, effective 25 May 2018. It governs the processing of personal data of individuals within the EU/EEA, regardless of where the processor or controller is located. It has extraterritorial scope — if you process EU residents' data, GDPR applies to you.

The Seven GDPR Principles (Article 5)

#PrincipleMeaning
1Lawfulness, Fairness, TransparencyProcessing must have a lawful basis, be fair to the data subject, and be transparent about what is done with data
2Purpose LimitationData collected for specified, explicit, legitimate purposes must not be further processed incompatibly
3Data MinimisationOnly collect data that is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary
4AccuracyPersonal data must be accurate and kept up to date; inaccurate data must be erased or rectified without delay
5Storage LimitationData kept in identifiable form only as long as necessary for the processing purpose
6Integrity & ConfidentialityAppropriate security measures to protect against unauthorised processing, loss, destruction, or damage
7AccountabilityThe controller must be able to demonstrate compliance with all the above principles
Key Concept — Six Lawful Bases for Processing (Article 6): Consent, Contract, Legal obligation, Vital interests, Public task, Legitimate interests. Consent is NOT the only lawful basis — the exam tests this distinction frequently. A hospital processing emergency medical data uses vital interests, not consent.

4. Data Subject

Data Subject: An identified or identifiable natural person whose personal data is being processed. The data subject is the individual the data is about — always a living human being, never a company or deceased person under GDPR.

Data Subject Rights (Articles 12–22)

RightArticleDescription
Right to be Informed13–14Know what data is collected, why, and by whom — privacy notices
Right of Access (SAR)15Obtain a copy of all personal data being processed, free of charge
Right to Rectification16Have inaccurate data corrected or incomplete data completed
Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten")17Have personal data deleted when no longer necessary, consent withdrawn, or unlawfully processed
Right to Restrict Processing18Have processing paused while accuracy or lawfulness is contested
Right to Data Portability20Receive personal data in a structured, machine-readable format and transmit to another controller
Right to Object21Object to processing based on legitimate interests or direct marketing — controller must stop unless compelling grounds exist
Rights Related to Automated Decision-Making22Not be subject to solely automated decisions (including profiling) that produce legal or significant effects
⚠️ Exam Trap: The Right to Erasure is not absolute. It does not apply when processing is necessary for exercising freedom of expression, compliance with a legal obligation, public health, archiving in the public interest, or establishment/exercise/defence of legal claims.

5. Data Controller

Data Controller (Article 4(7)): The natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body which, alone or jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of the processing of personal data. The controller is the decision-maker — "why" and "how" data is processed.

Controller Obligations

  • Accountability & compliance: Implement appropriate technical and organizational measures; maintain Records of Processing Activities (ROPA)
  • Data Protection by Design and by Default (Article 25): Build privacy into systems from the start; default settings must be the most privacy-friendly
  • Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA — Article 35): Mandatory before high-risk processing (profiling, large-scale special category data, systematic monitoring of public areas)
  • Breach notification (Article 33): Notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach likely to result in risk to data subjects
  • Data subject notification (Article 34): Notify affected data subjects without undue delay if the breach is likely to result in high risk to their rights and freedoms
  • Appoint a DPO where required (public bodies, large-scale systematic monitoring, large-scale special category processing)
  • Select processors carefully: Use only processors providing sufficient guarantees; execute a Data Processing Agreement
Key Concept — Joint Controllers (Article 26): When two or more controllers jointly determine purposes and means, they are joint controllers and must arrange (in a transparent manner) their respective responsibilities. Data subjects can exercise rights against any of them.

6. Data Processor

Data Processor (Article 4(8)): A natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body which processes personal data on behalf of the controller. The processor acts only on the controller's documented instructions — it does not decide why or how to process data.

Controller vs. Processor — Comparison

AttributeControllerProcessor
Determines purpose?Yes — decides whyNo — follows instructions
Determines means?Yes — decides howMay choose technical means only (e.g., which cloud platform), not essential means
GDPR liabilityPrimary — accountable for everythingLiable for breaching processor-specific obligations, acting outside instructions, or becoming a de facto controller

💡 Exam Tip: A processor that starts using data for its own purposes (e.g., analytics for product improvement beyond the contracted service) becomes a controller for that new purpose — and assumes full controller liability. This is a favourite exam scenario.

Sub-Processors

A processor may not engage another processor (sub-processor) without prior specific or general written authorisation from the controller. If general authorisation is given, the processor must inform the controller of any intended changes (additions or replacements) to sub-processors, giving the controller the opportunity to object.

7. Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Data Protection Officer (DPO — Articles 37–39): An independent, expert role designated by the controller or processor to oversee GDPR compliance. The DPO is not personally liable for non-compliance — the controller/processor retains liability.

When Is a DPO Mandatory?

  • Processing carried out by a public authority or body (except courts acting in judicial capacity)
  • Core activities require regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale
  • Core activities involve large-scale processing of special categories (health, biometric, genetic, racial/ethnic, political, religious, trade union, sexual orientation, criminal convictions)

DPO Requirements & Protections

RequirementDetail
ExpertiseExpert knowledge of data protection law and practices — GDPR does not mandate a specific certification
IndependenceMust not receive instructions on how to perform tasks; reports to highest management level
No conflicts of interestCannot hold a position that determines purposes/means of processing (e.g., cannot be CISO, IT director, or head of marketing simultaneously)
Protection from dismissalCannot be dismissed or penalised for performing DPO tasks
ResourcesController/processor must provide necessary resources (budget, staff, access to operations)
Direct reporting lineReports directly to the highest management level of the controller/processor
Contact pointServes as contact for data subjects and supervisory authorities
⚠️ Exam Trap — DPO Conflict of Interest: The DPO cannot simultaneously be the CISO, IT Director, CEO, CFO, Head of HR, or Head of Marketing — because these roles determine purposes/means of processing, creating a conflict of interest. A common exam distractor claims the CISO should serve as DPO.

8. Supervisory Authority & Enforcement

Supervisory Authority (SA — Articles 51–59): An independent public body established by each EU/EEA member state to monitor GDPR application, handle complaints, and impose sanctions. Each member state has at least one SA (e.g., ICO in the UK, CNIL in France, BfDI in Germany).

SA Powers

Power TypeExamples
InvestigativeOrder controllers/processors to provide information; carry out audits; access premises; obtain access to data
CorrectiveIssue warnings; reprimands; order compliance; order communication of breach to data subject; impose temporary or permanent processing bans; order suspension of data flows; impose fines
AdvisoryIssue opinions; approve codes of conduct; accredit certification bodies

Fine Structure

TierMaximum FineViolations
Lower Tier€10 million or 2% of global annual turnoverViolations of controller/processor obligations, DPO provisions, certification body requirements
Upper Tier€20 million or 4% of global annual turnoverViolations of processing principles, data subject rights, international transfer rules, non-compliance with SA orders

Lead Supervisory Authority & One-Stop-Shop

For cross-border processing, the lead SA is determined by the location of the controller's "main establishment" (where decisions about purposes and means are made). This is the one-stop-shop mechanism — the controller deals primarily with one SA, which coordinates with other "concerned" SAs through the consistency mechanism (Article 63).

9. Data Processing Agreements & Cross-Border Transfers

Data Processing Agreements (DPA — Article 28)

A DPA is a legally binding contract between controller and processor that must include:

  • Subject matter, duration, nature, and purpose of processing
  • Type of personal data and categories of data subjects
  • Controller's obligations and rights
  • Processor must: process only on documented instructions; ensure confidentiality; implement appropriate security measures; assist with data subject requests and DPIAs; delete or return data at end of service; make available all information to demonstrate compliance and allow audits

Cross-Border Data Transfer Mechanisms

GDPR prohibits transferring personal data outside the EU/EEA unless an adequate level of protection is ensured. Current mechanisms:

MechanismDescription
Adequacy Decision (Article 45)European Commission determines a third country provides adequate protection — transfers permitted freely (e.g., Japan, UK, Canada, South Korea, Israel, Switzerland, New Zealand)
Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs — Article 46(2)(c))Pre-approved contractual terms between exporter and importer — most widely used mechanism post-Schrems II
Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs — Article 47)Internal rules within a multinational corporate group approved by an SA — complex and costly but covers intra-group transfers
EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)Adequacy decision adopted July 2023 replacing invalidated Privacy Shield — US companies self-certify; subject to future legal challenges
Derogations (Article 49)Explicit consent, contractual necessity, important public interest, legal claims, vital interests — limited to non-repetitive, occasional transfers
⚠️ Exam Trap — Schrems II: The 2020 Schrems II ruling (CJEU) invalidated Privacy Shield and required organizations using SCCs to conduct Transfer Impact Assessments (TIAs) to evaluate whether the recipient country's laws undermine the protections offered by SCCs. Simply having SCCs is no longer sufficient — supplementary measures may be needed.

10. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 GDPR Roles — "C-P-S-D-S":
Controller decides purpose → Processor follows instructions → Subject owns the data → DPO oversees compliance → Supervisory Authority enforces

Memory hook: "Controller Commands; Processor Processes; Subject owns; DPO watches; SA enforces"
🧠 7 GDPR Principles — "L-P-D-A-S-I-A":
Lawfulness, Purpose limitation, Data minimisation, Accuracy, Storage limitation, Integrity & Confidentiality, Accountability

Memory hook: "Lawyers Prefer Data Accuracy — Securely, Isn't that Accountable?"
🧠 Data Subject Rights — "I ACCESS DP":
Informed, Access, Correction (rectification), Curtail (restrict), Erasure, Switching (portability), Stop (object), Decision-making (automated), Profiling

Memory hook: "I ACCESS My Data Protection"
🧠 Breach Notification — "72 → SA; High Risk → Subject":
Controller notifies Supervisory Authority within 72 hours of awareness
Processor notifies Controller without undue delay
Controller notifies Data Subjects without undue delay if high risk to rights/freedoms

Memory hook: "72 to the SA, ASAP to the Subject (if high risk)"
🧠 Controller vs. Processor — "Purpose Test":
Ask: "Who decides WHY this data is processed?" → That's the controller
Ask: "Who only does WHAT they're told with the data?" → That's the processor
If the processor starts deciding WHY → they become a controller
🧠 Transfer Mechanisms — "A-S-B-D":
Adequacy decisions, SCCs, BCRs, Derogations

Memory hook: "A Safe Bridge for Data"
