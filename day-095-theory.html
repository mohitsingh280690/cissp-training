2. Real-World Relevance

SOC reports have become the de facto standard for third-party assurance in cloud and SaaS environments. Before signing a contract with AWS, Salesforce, or any SaaS vendor, procurement and security teams request the SOC 2 Type II report. Without it, enterprise customers often refuse to proceed.

When Organizations Encounter SOC Reports

  • Vendor risk assessment: Reviewing a vendor's SOC 2 before onboarding
  • Customer requests: Your customers demand to see YOUR SOC 2 report
  • Financial audits: External auditors request SOC 1 reports from payroll/ERP service providers
  • Regulatory compliance: Demonstrating due diligence for HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR via SOC reports
  • Insurance applications: Cyber insurers may request SOC 2 reports as part of underwriting

3. SOC Report Framework & SSAE 18

SOC (System and Organization Controls): A suite of audit reports developed by the AICPA (American Institute of Certified Public Accountants). SOC reports are prepared by independent CPA firms using the SSAE 18 (Statement on Standards for Attestation Engagements No. 18) framework.

Key Terminology

TermMeaning
AICPAAmerican Institute of Certified Public Accountants — develops SOC standards
SSAE 18The attestation standard under which SOC 1 and SOC 2 reports are issued (replaced SSAE 16 in 2017, which replaced SAS 70)
Service OrganizationThe entity providing services (e.g., cloud provider, payroll processor, data center operator)
User EntityThe customer using the service organization's services
Subservice OrganizationA vendor used by the service organization (e.g., AWS hosting the SaaS provider's infrastructure)
CUECsComplementary User Entity Controls — controls that must be implemented by the customer for the service organization's controls to be effective
⚠️ SAS 70 is Obsolete: SAS 70 was replaced by SSAE 16 (2011), which was then replaced by SSAE 18 (2017). If you see "SAS 70 audit" referenced, it is outdated. The current standard is SSAE 18. However, the exam may reference the historical evolution.

4. SOC 1 Reports (Financial Controls)

SOC 1: Reports on controls at a service organization relevant to user entities' internal control over financial reporting (ICFR). SOC 1 focuses exclusively on financial controls — NOT security or privacy.

When SOC 1 is Used

  • Payroll processing services (ADP, Paychex)
  • Financial transaction processing (payment processors)
  • Hosting services that process financial data (ERP hosting)
  • Loan servicing and claims processing

Audience

SOC 1 reports are restricted-use — intended only for the service organization, user entities, and their auditors. They are NOT published publicly.

Exam Trap: SOC 1 is about FINANCIAL controls, not security controls. If a question asks about security, availability, or privacy assurance — that's SOC 2, not SOC 1.

5. SOC 2 Reports (Trust Services Criteria)

SOC 2: Reports on controls relevant to security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and/or privacy — known as the Trust Services Criteria (TSC). This is the most relevant SOC report for CISSP and cybersecurity.

Trust Services Criteria (TSC)

CriterionFocus AreaKey Questions Addressed
Security (Common Criteria)Protection against unauthorized accessAre systems protected against unauthorized access? (REQUIRED — always included in every SOC 2)
AvailabilitySystem uptime and accessibilityAre systems available for operation as agreed? SLAs, DR, capacity planning
Processing IntegrityAccurate, complete, timely processingIs data processed completely, accurately, and in a timely manner?
ConfidentialityProtection of confidential informationIs information designated as confidential protected throughout its lifecycle?
PrivacyPersonal information handlingIs personal information collected, used, retained, disclosed, and disposed of properly? (Aligned with GAPP — Generally Accepted Privacy Principles)
Security is ALWAYS Included: Security (Common Criteria) is mandatory in every SOC 2 report. The other four criteria (Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, Privacy) are optional — selected based on relevance to the services provided. A SOC 2 may cover only Security, or Security + Availability, etc.

Audience

SOC 2 reports are restricted-use — provided only to the service organization, user entities, user entity auditors, and prospective customers under NDA. They contain detailed control descriptions and test results, making them sensitive documents.

6. SOC 3 Reports (General Purpose)

SOC 3: A general-purpose report that uses the same Trust Services Criteria as SOC 2 but provides a summary-level opinion without detailed control descriptions or test results. Designed for public distribution.

SOC 3 vs SOC 2

AttributeSOC 2SOC 3
Detail LevelComprehensive — detailed controls, tests, resultsSummary — opinion only with no control details
AudienceRestricted (NDA required)General public (can post on website)
Use CaseDue diligence, vendor risk assessmentMarketing, trust badge on website
Trust Services CriteriaSame criteriaSame criteria
CPA FirmYes — independent CPAYes — independent CPA (same audit, different report format)
Exam Tip: SOC 3 is often the result of the SAME audit as SOC 2 — just formatted differently. Think of SOC 3 as the "public summary" version of SOC 2. It's useful for marketing but insufficient for detailed due diligence.

7. SOC for Cybersecurity & Supply Chain

SOC for Cybersecurity

A newer AICPA framework allowing ANY organization (not just service organizations) to report on its cybersecurity risk management program. Unlike SOC 2, which focuses on specific services, SOC for Cybersecurity covers the entity-wide cybersecurity program.

AttributeSOC 2SOC for Cybersecurity
Who Can UseService organizations onlyAny organization
ScopeSpecific system/serviceEnterprise-wide cybersecurity program
CriteriaTrust Services CriteriaAICPA cybersecurity criteria (flexible framework mapping)
AudienceRestricted useGeneral use (board, investors, public)

SOC for Supply Chain

Addresses supply chain risks by reporting on controls relevant to production and distribution. Useful for manufacturers and distributors who need to assure customers that their supply chain processes are secure and reliable.

  • Covers production and distribution controls
  • Addresses integrity and security of the supply chain
  • Uses Trust Services Criteria adapted for supply chain context
  • Relevant for industries with complex supply chains (manufacturing, pharmaceuticals, food)

8. Type I vs Type II — The Critical Difference

TYPE I vs TYPE II — The Fundamental Distinction TYPE I (Point-in-Time) TYPE II (Period of Time) ───────────────────────── ──────────────────────────── "Are controls DESIGNED "Are controls OPERATING properly?" EFFECTIVELY over time?" Examines: Control design Examines: Control design AND at a specific date operating effectiveness Test period: Usually 6-12 months ────── X ────── ═══════════════════════════════ Single date snapshot Continuous period of testing Lower assurance Higher assurance (intention ≠ action) (proves controls actually work) Less expensive / faster More expensive / longer Often used as first step Gold standard for assurance
AttributeType IType II
What Is TestedDesign suitability of controls AT a specific dateDesign AND operating effectiveness OVER a period (typically 6-12 months)
Assurance LevelLower — confirms controls exist and are suitably designed, but not that they work consistentlyHigher — confirms controls exist, are designed properly, AND operate effectively over time
PeriodPoint in time (e.g., "as of March 31, 2024")Period of time (e.g., "April 1, 2023 through March 31, 2024")
TestingAuditor inspects control design; minimal testingAuditor performs extensive testing of control operation over the entire period
Customer PreferenceAcceptable for new service organizations with no Type II historyStrongly preferred; many enterprises require Type II
⚠️ Most Common Exam Trap: Type I only proves controls are DESIGNED — it does NOT prove they actually WORK over time. Many exam questions test whether you understand this distinction. Type II is always the higher-assurance answer.
