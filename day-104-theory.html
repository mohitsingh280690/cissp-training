2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

Why Incident Management Matters

Without a structured incident management framework, organizations discover that during a crisis: nobody knows who is in charge, communications are ad-hoc and conflicting, evidence is accidentally destroyed, regulatory notification deadlines are missed, and recovery takes far longer than necessary. Incident management is the organizational scaffolding that makes technical incident response possible.

Regulatory Drivers

  • GDPR Art. 33: 72-hour notification to supervisory authority after discovery of personal data breach
  • HIPAA Breach Notification Rule: 60-day notification requirement for breaches affecting 500+ individuals
  • SEC Cybersecurity Rules (2023): Material cybersecurity incidents must be disclosed in 8-K filing within 4 business days
  • PCI DSS Req. 12.10: Establish, document, and distribute a security incident response plan

3. Event vs Incident — The Critical Distinction

TermDefinitionExamples
EventAny observable occurrence in a system or network. Events are neutral — they may or may not indicate a problemUser login; file access; firewall allow/deny log entry; scheduled backup completion; port scan detected
AlertAn event that has been flagged by a monitoring system as potentially significantIDS alert for suspicious traffic; failed login threshold exceeded; antimalware detection
IncidentAn event (or series of events) that actually or potentially violates the organization's security policy or threatens the CIA triadConfirmed malware infection; unauthorized data access; ransomware attack; data exfiltration; DDoS attack
EVENT TRIAGE FUNNEL Millions of Events (logs, network traffic, system activity) │ ▼ ┌─────────┐ │ FILTERS │ Automated: SIEM rules, correlation, thresholds └────┬────┘ │ ▼ Thousands of Alerts (flagged events) │ ▼ ┌─────────┐ │ TRIAGE │ Analyst review: true positive? false positive? └────┬────┘ │ ▼ Scored TRUE POSITIVES → Declared INCIDENTS │ ▼ ┌─────────┐ │CLASSIFY │ Severity, category, priority └────┬────┘ │ ▼ INCIDENT RESPONSE LIFECYCLE (Day 105)
Key CISSP Distinction: Every incident is an event, but NOT every event is an incident. The determination of whether an event constitutes an incident requires human judgment (or well-tuned automated rules) measured against the organization's security policy.

4. NIST SP 800-61 Framework

NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 2: "Computer Security Incident Handling Guide" — the primary reference for CISSP incident response. Provides guidelines for establishing incident response capabilities and handling incidents efficiently and effectively.

Four Lifecycle Phases

PhasePurposeKey Activities
1. PreparationEstablish incident response capability BEFORE incidents occurBuild IRT; write policies/procedures; deploy tools; conduct training; establish communication channels
2. Detection & AnalysisIdentify potential incidents and determine their scope/impactMonitoring/alerting; triage; incident classification; documentation; notification
3. Containment, Eradication & RecoveryStop the incident, remove the cause, and restore operationsShort/long-term containment; evidence preservation; root cause removal; system restoration; verification
4. Post-Incident ActivityLearn from the incident to improve future responseLessons learned meeting; report writing; process improvements; evidence retention
Preparation is Key: The exam frequently emphasizes that preparation is the MOST IMPORTANT phase. Without preparation, the other three phases cannot function effectively. The single most critical preparation element is management commitment and support.

6. Incident Categories & Severity Classification

Common Incident Categories

CategoryDescriptionExamples
Unauthorized accessGaining access to systems, networks, or data without permissionBrute force; stolen credentials; privilege escalation
Malicious codeMalware infection of systemsRansomware; trojans; worms; rootkits; crypto-miners
Denial of serviceDisruption of service availabilityDDoS; resource exhaustion; application-layer attacks
Improper usageViolation of acceptable use policyPolicy violations; unauthorized software; prohibited activity
Data breachUnauthorized disclosure or exfiltration of dataPII/PHI exposure; trade secret theft; database dumps
Social engineeringManipulation of individuals to gain access or informationPhishing; pretexting; business email compromise (BEC)

Severity/Priority Matrix

SeverityImpactResponse Requirement
Critical (P1)Multi-system outage; active data exfiltration; ransomware spreadingImmediate response; all available resources; executive notification; potential regulatory notification
High (P2)Single critical system compromised; confirmed targeted attackRapid response within hours; IRT activation; management notification
Medium (P3)Malware on non-critical system; policy violation with limited impactRespond within business day; standard IRT procedures
Low (P4)Port scan; failed attack attempt; minor policy deviationMonitor, log, and address in normal operational cycle
Priority = Impact × Urgency: When the exam asks about prioritizing incidents, consider BOTH the current/potential impact to the business AND how quickly the situation is evolving. A slow-moving compromise of a critical system may be higher priority than an active but contained attack on a non-critical system.

7. Incident Response Policy & Plan

Hierarchy of IR Documentation

LevelDocumentContentAudience
1IR PolicyManagement commitment; authority; scope; incident definition; roles; reporting requirements; compliance obligationsAll employees; management; auditors
2IR PlanTeam structure; communication procedures; escalation paths; severity definitions; tool inventory; external contacts (law enforcement, legal, PR)IR team; management; stakeholders
3IR ProceduresStep-by-step technical procedures for specific incident types (malware, DDoS, data breach, insider threat)IR technical staff
4Playbooks / RunbooksHighly detailed, scenario-specific response flows with decision trees; may be automated via SOARSOC analysts; SOAR platforms
Policy vs Plan vs Procedure: Policy provides the authority and mandate ("we WILL respond to incidents"). Plan provides the framework ("HERE IS HOW we organize our response"). Procedures provide the technical details ("these are the EXACT STEPS for handling a ransomware incident"). Playbooks are the most granular — specific scenario-by-scenario guides.

⚠️ Attorney-Client Privilege: Having legal counsel direct the incident response investigation can establish attorney-client privilege over investigation findings. This means the investigation report may NOT be discoverable in subsequent litigation. Many organizations engage external counsel to lead investigations for this reason. However, privilege does NOT apply to regulatory notification obligations.

Secure Communications

During an active incident, assume the attacker may have access to internal email, messaging, and phone systems. Use out-of-band communication channels: personal phones, encrypted messaging apps, separate communication systems not connected to the compromised network.

"EAI — Events Are Investigated (down to incidents)"
🧠 IR Team Acronyms — size order
SOC detects → IRT responds → CSIRT coordinates → CERT advises (sector-wide)
"SOC feeds IRT; CSIRT coordinates; CERT is national"
🧠 IR Documentation Hierarchy — "PPPP"
Policy (authority) → Plan (framework) → Procedure (steps) → Playbook (scenario)
"From broadest authority to narrowest detail"
