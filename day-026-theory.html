2. Real-World Relevance

Privacy regulations (GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA) impose significant obligations and penalties. After GDPR went into effect, organisations that couldn't demonstrate proper PII protection faced fines up to 4% of global annual revenue. Privacy engineering — building privacy protections into systems from the ground up — is now a core security competency, not a legal afterthought.

3. PII, PHI, and SPI

PII — Personally Identifiable Information

PII: Any information that can be used to identify, contact, or locate a specific individual — either alone or in combination with other data.

PII examples: Full name, home address, email, phone number, date of birth, SSN, passport number, driver's licence number, IP address (in some jurisdictions), biometric data, photographs, geolocation data.

Per NIST SP 800-122: PII is further categorised by sensitivity. Standalone identifiers (SSN, passport) are highly sensitive; contextual PII (name alone may be low risk; name + employer + medical condition is high risk) requires combination risk assessment.

PHI — Protected Health Information

PHI: Any health information that is linked to an identifiable individual and is covered under HIPAA. PHI includes the 18 HIPAA identifiers (names, geographic data, dates, phone numbers, SSNs, medical record numbers, health plan numbers, and others).

PHI is subject to HIPAA's Privacy Rule and Security Rule. Unauthorised disclosure of PHI carries civil and criminal penalties. De-identified PHI (where all 18 identifiers are removed or statistical expert certification is obtained) is no longer PHI under HIPAA.

SPI — Sensitive Personal Information

SPI: A subset of PII that warrants heightened protection due to its potential for significant harm if disclosed. GDPR Category Article 9 special categories include: racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data (for identification), health data, sex life / sexual orientation.
✅ Exam Tip: Know the GDPR "special categories" (Article 9 data). Processing these categories is generally PROHIBITED unless a specific legal basis applies (explicit consent, vital interests, etc.). These get higher protection than standard PII.

4. Data Anonymisation

Anonymisation: The irreversible process of altering data so that individuals cannot be identified, directly or indirectly, with reasonable effort. After true anonymisation, the data is no longer personal data under GDPR.

Anonymisation Techniques

  • Generalisation: Replace specific values with ranges (age 34 → age range 30-40; postal code 12345 → region: North-East)
  • Suppression: Remove data fields entirely (delete the Name column from a dataset)
  • Noise addition: Add random variation to numerical values to prevent identification while preserving statistical properties
  • Data aggregation: Report only group-level statistics (average salary by department, not individual salaries)
  • k-Anonymity: A formal model ensuring that each record in the dataset is indistinguishable from at least k-1 other records on quasi-identifier fields — limits re-identification risk
⚠️ Re-identification Risk: What is anonymised today may be re-identifiable tomorrow with new techniques or datasets. The Netflix Prize dataset (anonymised by removing names) was partially re-identified by researchers who combined it with public IMDB reviews. True anonymisation requires ongoing assessment.

5. Pseudonymisation

Pseudonymisation: Replacing direct identifiers with pseudonyms (artificial identifiers) while maintaining a separate mapping table that can reverse the process. The data can be re-linked to individuals if the pseudonymisation key is available.

Key Characteristics

  • Pseudonymised data is STILL considered personal data under GDPR — because identity can be restored with the mapping table
  • GDPR Article 4(5) defines pseudonymisation and Article 25 recommends it as a Privacy by Design technique
  • GDPR provides reduced obligations for pseudonymised data (e.g., breach notification thresholds may be different) but does NOT exempt it from full compliance
  • The pseudonymisation key must be stored separately from the pseudonymised data and protected as if it were the original personal data

Example

Patient name "John Smith" → replaced with PatientID "P-982341" in the clinical database. A separate secure table maps P-982341 → John Smith. If a researcher accesses the clinical data with PatientIDs only, their risk of re-identification is reduced — but the mapping still exists, so it's still personal data.

✅ Exam Tip: Pseudonymisation REDUCES risk; anonymisation ELIMINATES the data's personal data status. They are not interchangeable. GDPR explicitly distinguishes them.

6. Data Masking

Data Masking: Obscuring specific data values within a dataset to hide the real data while maintaining the format and appearance of the original data. Often used in non-production environments (development, testing, QA) and in user interfaces.

Types of Data Masking

  • Static data masking: A copy of the production database is made and the sensitive values are permanently replaced with fictitious data. Used for test and development environments.
  • Dynamic data masking: Data is masked in real-time at the query/presentation layer — the underlying stored data is unchanged but some users see masked values based on their role/permissions. Example: call centre agents see "****-****-****-1234" for credit card numbers.
  • On-the-fly masking: Data is masked as it moves between environments (ETL processes).

Common masking patterns: Credit card number showing only last 4 digits; SSN showing only last 4 digits (XXX-XX-1234); email showing first letter and domain (j***@gmail.com).

7. Tokenisation

Tokenisation: Replacing sensitive data with a non-sensitive substitute (token) that has no mathematical relationship to the original value. The mapping between token and original value is stored in a secure, isolated token vault.

🏥 Analogy: Hospital Patient ID

When you check into a hospital, you are given a patient ID number (e.g., PT-00482917). From that point on, every nurse, doctor, and lab technician works with your patient ID — it appears on your wristband, your test results, your medication chart, and the billing system.

Your real sensitive details — Social Security Number, insurance policy number, home address — live in a separate, locked administrative system. The patient ID is just a pointer. The ID number alone tells an attacker nothing. It cannot be used to file a tax return, drain a bank account, or impersonate you outside that hospital.

Payment tokenisation works the same way: your real card number sits in one secure vault; everywhere else in the payment chain, only the meaningless token circulates. The token is useless outside the system that issued it.

🔄 How Payment Tokenisation Works — Step by Step

Here is what happens when you tap your card at a coffee shop that uses tokenisation:

1
You pay. You tap your card. Your real card number (e.g., 4532 8812 3456 7890) is sent — once, securely — to the payment processor's tokenisation service.
2
Token generated. The processor generates a random, meaningless token (e.g., 9271 0043 8812 5501) and stores the mapping token ↔ real card number inside a heavily secured token vault.
3
Token sent back. The coffee shop's point-of-sale system receives and stores only the token. Your real card number never touches their servers or their database.
4
Future purchases. Next time you buy coffee, the shop sends your token to the payment processor. The processor looks it up in the vault, retrieves your real card number, and processes the charge — all behind the scenes.
✓
If the coffee shop is hacked, attackers steal tokens — random numbers that are useless without the vault. The vault is held by a specialist processor (e.g., Stripe, Visa, Mastercard), not the coffee shop.

Why This Matters for PCI-DSS Compliance

PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) requires any system that stores, processes, or transmits real card numbers to meet strict security controls. Tokenisation is a game-changer:

  • The merchant's systems only ever see tokens — they never store real card numbers
  • Because tokens have no value, the merchant's environment is considered out of PCI scope (or dramatically reduced scope) — far fewer compliance requirements apply
  • The payment processor who holds the token vault carries the full PCI-DSS burden, and they specialise in it
💡 Real-World Example: Apple Pay and Google Pay both use tokenisation. When you add your Visa card to Apple Pay, Apple receives your card number once, sends it to Visa's tokenisation service, and stores a device-specific token. Apple never stores your real card number. If Apple's servers are breached, attackers get tokens — worthless without Visa's vault.

⚠️ Critical Nuance: "Out of Scope" Only Works If the Card Number Never Touches the Merchant

A common misconception: "We tokenise card numbers, so we are out of PCI scope." This is only true if the real card number never passes through the merchant's infrastructure at all. If the card number hits the merchant's server first — even for a millisecond before being sent to the processor for tokenisation — that server touched real card data and is fully in scope. Tokenisation after the fact does not remove the scope.

How merchants genuinely keep themselves out of scope depends on the payment channel:

🌐 Online Payments — Hosted Fields (Client-Side Tokenisation)

When you type your card number into a website using Stripe, Braintree, or Adyen, those card input fields are not part of the merchant's website. They are <iframe> elements served directly from the processor's domain.

Wrong way (merchant IN scope)Right way (merchant OUT of scope)
Browser → Merchant server → Processor → token returnedBrowser → Processor directly → token returned → Merchant server
Merchant server saw the real card numberMerchant server only ever saw the token
Merchant is fully in PCI scopeMerchant's PCI scope is dramatically reduced

The browser's same-origin policy enforces this isolation — the merchant's own JavaScript cannot read what is typed inside an iframe served from a different domain. The card number travels directly from the customer's browser to the processor's servers. Only the token comes back to the merchant.

🏪 Physical POS — Point-to-Point Encryption (P2PE)

At a physical checkout, you physically insert or tap your card. The terminal does read the raw card number for a split second — but here is why the merchant still never sees it:

  • The terminal is a PCI-PTS certified tamper-resistant hardware device. The moment the card is read, the hardware encrypts the number inside its own secure element — before it ever reaches the merchant's POS software running on the register.
  • The encryption key was injected by the payment processor during device manufacturing, not by the merchant. The merchant cannot read, access, or change this key. If someone physically opens the terminal to try to extract the key, the hardware detects tampering and wipes the key automatically.
  • What leaves the terminal to the merchant's system is an encrypted blob — not a card number. Only the processor can decrypt it.
  • The processor decrypts, processes the payment, and returns a token. The merchant stores only the token.

This combination — hardware encryption inside the terminal plus tokenisation at the processor — is called a P2PE (Point-to-Point Encryption) validated solution under PCI-DSS, and it grants the maximum possible scope reduction for merchants.

For tap-to-pay (NFC) it is even cleaner: Your card or phone never transmits the real card number at all. The secure element inside your device transmits a token + one-time cryptogram directly. The raw card number never even leaves your card — the terminal only ever receives a token.

Format-Preserving Tokenisation

Sometimes systems are built around a specific data format (e.g., a 16-digit card number field). Ripping out that format would require rewriting every legacy system. Format-preserving tokenisation solves this: the token looks exactly like the original (16 digits, same structure) but is still a random, meaningless substitute. Legacy systems don't notice anything changed — but real data never touches them.

Key Characteristics

  • Tokens are meaningless without access to the token vault — unlike encryption, there is no algorithm to reverse-engineer the original value
  • Widely used in payment processing (PCI-DSS compliance): card numbers are replaced with tokens; merchants store tokens, token vault is held by payment processor
  • Reduces PCI-DSS scope: systems that only see tokens are generally outside PCI scope
  • Format-preserving tokenisation: tokens can mimic the format of the original data (16-digit token for 16-digit card number) to avoid breaking legacy system integrations

Tokenisation vs. Pseudonymisation

Both replace real identifiers with substitutes — the difference is how the substitute is generated and what holds the mapping back.

🪤 Exam Trap — Tokenisation vs. Pseudonymisation: Both replace real values with substitutes and both are reversible — so they look identical on the surface. The distinction: tokenisation uses a random, mathematically unrelated token stored in a vault (no algorithm can reverse it without the vault). Pseudonymisation uses a keyed mapping or algorithm — the key holder can re-derive the original. Tokenisation is the stronger de-identification technique for payment data; pseudonymisation is GDPR's named Privacy by Design method for analytics/research.

8. Privacy by Design (PbD)

Privacy by Design (PbD): A framework developed by Ann Cavoukian, former Ontario Privacy Commissioner, establishing that privacy should be proactively embedded into systems and processes from the design phase — not bolted on after deployment. GDPR Article 25 codifies this as a legal requirement.

7 Foundational Principles of PbD

  1. Proactive, not reactive: Prevent privacy risks before they occur; don't wait for a breach to address privacy
  2. Privacy as the default: Maximum privacy protection is the default — users must actively opt-in to less privacy, not opt-out of it
  3. Privacy embedded into design: Privacy is an integral part of system design, not an add-on feature
  4. Full functionality — positive-sum: Privacy and security are not trade-offs; both can be achieved ("not privacy vs. security, but privacy AND security")
  5. End-to-end security: Privacy protection throughout the entire data lifecycle, from collection to destruction
  6. Visibility and transparency: Operations are open to verification; policies are published; users know how their data is used
  7. Respect for user privacy: Keep systems user-centric; enable data access rights, correction, and deletion for individuals
