2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

IssueReal-World Impact
Joiners – delayed provisioningNew employee starts Day 1 without necessary access — lost productivity, frustration. Provisioning SLA (e.g., accounts active within 1 business day of start) requires automated joiner workflows connected to HR system.
Movers – access accumulationEmployee moves from Finance to HR — gains new HR access but old Finance access never removed. By Year 3 of lateral moves, the employee has accumulated access across 6 different departments, violating least privilege.
Leavers – delayed de-provisioningTerminated employee account not disabled within hours — a common breach vector for insider threats and disgruntled employee retaliation. High-profile breach cases often involve accounts active for weeks post-termination.
Orphan account exploitationA shared test account created during a project and never deleted — attackers find it via enumeration, no current owner to notice unusual login activity, default/weak credentials. Easy lateral movement entry point.
Service account sprawlApplication accounts with excessive privileges, shared credentials, no expiry, not tied to any owner — when the responsible developer leaves, no one knows the password and the account can't be changed without breaking integrations.

3. Account Provisioning

📖 Account Provisioning: The process of creating, configuring, and granting access rights to a new user account in response to an authorized identity request (typically triggered by HR onboarding). Encompasses creating credentials, assigning roles/groups, configuring entitlements, and notifying the user.
Provisioning PhaseActionsSecurity Control
Identity establishmentConfirm requestor's identity (new hire documentation, contractor agreement); establish authoritative source of identity record in IAM/HR systemIdentity proofing — ensure the person is who they claim to be; prevent duplicate or ghost identities
Access request & approvalManager submits provisioning request specifying required access; request routed for approval (manager + resource owner + security team for privileged access)Formal approval chain prevents unauthorized access grants; least-privilege principle guides which roles are requested
Account creationCreate unique account ID in directory (AD, LDAP, IAM); set initial credentials per password policy; assign to role/group(s)No shared accounts; unique ID for accountability; initial credential delivered securely (OTP, email link)
Entitlement assignmentAssign minimum required access based on role profile for job function; do NOT over-grant speculativelyPrinciple of least privilege — grant only what is required for the approved job function, nothing more
Documentation & loggingRecord all provisioning actions in audit log: who requested, who approved, what was granted, whenAudit trail for SOX/PCI/HIPAA compliance; supports forensic investigation if access is later misused

Provisioning Triggers in Practice

HR System (Source of Truth) │ │ "New Employee Record Created" │ (name, start date, department, job title, manager) ▼ IGA / Provisioning Engine (e.g., SailPoint, Saviynt, Azure AD Lifecycle workflows) │ │ Maps: "HR Analyst in Finance Dept" → "Finance-Analyst RBAC role" │ Creates: AD account, Azure AD account, email, VPN profile │ Assigns: Finance-Analyst group, Finance-Intranet access, JIRA access │ Notifies: user with initial login link (secure OTP), manager with summary ▼ All Systems Provisioned Automatically — Consistent, Audited, Least-Privilege

4. Account Review & Recertification

📖 Access Recertification (Re-validation): A periodic process where account owners, managers, or resource administrators formally review and confirm (or revoke) all access entitlements for users in their purview. Designed to catch privilege creep — access that was legitimately granted but is no longer needed.
AspectDetail
FrequencyQuarterly, semi-annual, or annual depending on sensitivity. Privileged/admin access: quarterly. Regular business access: annual. Compliance frameworks may mandate specific periods (e.g., SOX quarterly for key financial controls).
Who certifiesBusiness manager (reviews direct reports' access); resource/application owner (reviews who has access to their system); data owner (reviews who has access to their data classification).
Review workflowIGA system generates certification campaign → emails access owners/managers → reviewer approves (keep) or revokes (remove) each entitlement → automated revocation of denied access → report generated for compliance.
Privilege creepOccurs when users accumulate permissions over time through job changes, project assignments, temp access never revoked. Recertification's primary purpose is detecting and correcting privilege creep.
Certification actionsCertify (keep access as-is); Revoke (remove access immediately); Modify (adjust scope/level); Escalate (refer to security team for review).

6. Account Suspension & Deletion

ActionDefinitionWhen UsedBest Practice
Suspension (Disable)Account is preserved in the directory but authentication is blocked. The account, its group memberships, and history are retained but login is prevented.Employee on extended leave (FMLA, parental leave, sabbatical); temporary lock pending investigation; immediate response to suspected compromise; beginning of offboarding.Immediate action on notice of termination — disable within hours, not days. Preserve account for forensic purposes or reactivation. Easier to re-enable than to reprovisioning.
Deletion (Removal)Account is permanently removed from the directory along with its group memberships and entitlements. Historical audit logs should be retained even after account deletion.Confirmed permanent separation (resigned, terminated, contractor engagement ended); after retention period following suspension; when reprovisioned identity is confirmed not needed.Do not delete immediately on termination — suspend first, delete after retention period (e.g., 90 days). Audit logs must outlive the account itself for compliance.
Offboarding sequence1. Receive termination notification from HR (ideally automated). 2. Immediately suspend account + revoke active sessions + revoke tokens. 3. Transfer data/items to manager. 4. Retrieve physical assets. 5. After retention period → delete account.All permanent separationsLeavers are the HIGHEST-RISK IAM event. Speed of account suspension is a key security metric — target: within 1 hour of termination for privileged users; same-day for all users.
⚠️ Insider Threat Risk: Studies show many insider-threat incidents occur during the notice period or within days of departure. Accounts should be suspended AT THE TIME of termination notification, not when IT processes the request. HR-to-IT automated offboarding workflow is critical.

7. Orphan Accounts

📖 Orphan Account: An account that exists in a system but no longer has an associated active user or owner. Created when a user leaves and their account is not properly deprovisioned, or when system/service accounts outlive the team or application that created them. An orphan account has no responsible owner aware of it.
AspectDetail
How orphans occurEmployee terminates but account only deleted in AD, not in downstream applications (HR app, ERP, CRM, cloud apps). Each system provisioned independently without lifecycle sync. Result: user is gone but has active accounts in 5 of 8 systems.
Security risksNo current owner monitoring for anomalous activity. Credentials may be stale/known. Attackers specifically seek orphan accounts for lateral movement — low detection risk, no active owner to raise alert. May retain elevated permissions from the former user's highest-privilege role.
DetectionPeriodic orphan account review: compare accounts in each system against authoritative HR roster. Accounts not matching any active HR record are candidates for review + suspension. Automated reconciliation via IGA tools.
PreventionAutomated deprovisioning triggered by HR offboarding event across ALL connected systems simultaneously via IGA/SCIM integration. No manual process that relies on IT receiving notification months later.

8. Dormant Accounts

📖 Dormant Account: An account that belongs to a real, currently active user but has not been used within a defined period (e.g., 90 days). The user exists but has not logged in — account is idle/inactive. Distinct from orphan accounts (which have no owner at all).
AspectDetail
ExamplesContractor account created for project that ended but contractor is still employed; system admin account for a production server only accessed during incidents; guest/vendor accounts; training environment accounts; old API service accounts no longer called.
Security risksDormant accounts may have stale credentials (never changed because user doesn't log in). If credentials are compromised (leaked via third-party breach), attack may occur months later when no one is watching the account's first login. Low-activity baseline means anomaly detection doesn't trigger.
Policy standardDefine a dormancy threshold (e.g., no login in 90 days). At threshold: notify account owner + manager. If no response within 7 days: automatically suspend. Requires periodic automated scanning against last-login timestamps in directory.
Dormant vs. Orphan distinctionDormant: has an active owner who just hasn't used it recently. Orphan: has NO active owner. A dormant account can be reactivated by the existing owner. An orphan account needs a new ownership assignment or deletion.
💡 Exam Tip — Orphan vs. Dormant:
Orphan account = no active owner. User left, account left behind. No one knows it's there.
Dormant account = active owner, but account not used recently. Owner still employed; just hasn't logged in.
Both represent risk. Both require detection and action. Different remediation: orphan → delete/disable; dormant → notify owner → disable if no response.

9. Service Account Management

📖 Service Account: A non-human account (also called a machine identity, system account, or application account) used by an application, script, service, or automated process to authenticate and access resources. Not associated with a specific human user but requires its own identity lifecycle governance.
Risk AreaProblemControl
Excessive privilegeService accounts often provisioned with broad "Domain Admin" or "DBA" privileges because it was easier at the time. Never scoped to minimum necessary.Apply least-privilege principle: service account should only have exactly the permissions needed for its specific function. Separate accounts per application/service — never shared.
No expiry / non-rotating credentialsService account passwords never expire because rotation breaks applications. Credentials may be decades old. Historical credential dumps (e.g., from a breach years ago) may still be valid.Implement PAM solution with automated credential vaulting and rotation (see Day 79). Modern systems use short-lived credentials (AWS IAM Roles, Azure Managed Identities) that eliminate long-lived static passwords.
Ownership/accountability gapCreated by a developer who left — no current owner knows what the account does, how to rotate its password, or what breaks if it's disabled. Account is invisible to IAM governance.Every service account must have a designated human owner (typically the application owner or team lead). Owner attested annually. Documented in CMDB/service catalog alongside the application it serves.
Interactive login enabledService accounts with ability to interactively log into systems (desktop, SSH). An attacker who compromises the credential can use it for lateral movement, not just service calls.Disable interactive login on service accounts. Restrict to specific service/process invocation only. Log any interactive login attempts as an alert.
Shared service accountsMultiple applications or developers using the same service account — loss of accountability, credential changes affect all consumers, impossible to audit which app caused an action.Unique service account per application per environment (dev/test/prod). Never share credentials between applications.

10. Identity Lifecycle Automation – SCIM & IGA

TechnologyPurposeKey Features
SCIM (System for Cross-domain Identity Management)Open standard (RFC 7644) for automating user provisioning and deprovisioning between identity provider (IDP) and applications. REST API-based protocol for creating, reading, updating, and deleting (CRUD) identity records across systems.HR fires "create user" → SCIM propagates to connected SaaS apps (Salesforce, Slack, Box, ServiceNow) simultaneously. Termination triggers delete/suspend SCIM call cascaded to all apps. Eliminates manual provisioning and orphan accounts from missed deprovisioning.
IGA (Identity Governance and Administration)Platform (e.g., SailPoint IdentityNow, Saviynt, Microsoft Entra Identity Governance) for holistic identity lifecycle management: provisioning, recertification campaigns, SoD policy enforcement, role management, audit reporting.Connects to HR, Active Directory, LDAP, hundreds of SaaS apps. Automates joiner/mover/leaver workflows. Runs automated access reviews. Generates compliance-ready reports for SOX/PCI/HIPAA. Detects SoD violations automatically.
Joiner-Mover-Leaver (JML) WorkflowStructured automated process triggered by HR events. Joiner: new employee start → provision required roles. Mover: transfer/promotion → remove old role + add new role. Leaver: termination → suspend + revoke + offboard within hours.HR system is the trigger. IGA is the orchestrator. AD/LDAP and downstream apps are the targets. The entire lifecycle is governed by policy without manual IT tickets.

11. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 Identity Lifecycle — JML Model:
Joiner → Provision (new hire, grant minimum access)
Mover → Modify (change job/dept: REMOVE old + GRANT new, don't just add)
Leaver → Deprovision (terminate: SUSPEND immediately, DELETE after retention)
The CISCO security model: Join → Manage → Leave. Every account passes through these phases.
🧠 Orphan vs. Dormant:
Orphan = Ownerless (user is gone, account was left behind)
Dormant = Disused (user still exists, just hasn't logged in recently)
Orphan: like an abandoned house — no one lives there anymore; break-in goes unnoticed.
Dormant: like a vacation home — owner is real but away; still needs periodic security checks.
🧠 Privilege Creep = Access Accumulation Over Time:
Movers gain new access but old access is never removed.
Annual recertification is the prevention mechanism — certify or revoke all entitlements.
"Use it or lose it": access not used within recertification window should be flagged for revocation.
🧠 Service Account Risk Keywords:
No expiry + Excessive privilege + Shared + No owner + Interactive login = HIGH RISK
Solution: PAM (Day 79) + Least privilege + Unique accounts + Human owner + No interactive login enforced
