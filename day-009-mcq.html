1The FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk) model defines risk as a function of two primary components. Which combination correctly represents the FAIR risk formula?
✅ Correct: B. FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk) defines Risk = Loss Event Frequency (LEF) × Loss Magnitude (LM). LEF is further decomposed into Threat Event Frequency × Vulnerability (probability of action succeeding). LM is decomposed into Primary Loss and Secondary Loss (reputational, regulatory, competitive). FAIR is notable for providing a rigorous, quantitative, probabilistic approach to risk that avoids the "red/yellow/green" imprecision of qualitative matrices. Option D describes a generic risk formula, not specifically FAIR.
2A security manager calculates that a critical server has an ALE of $15,000 (ARO of 0.01 per year; SLE of $1.5M). A vendor proposes a $2M control that would reduce the ARO to near zero. What does sound risk management analysis suggest?
✅ Correct: B. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) requires that the annualised cost of a control not exceed the ALE it mitigates. A $2M control (or even its annualised amortisation) vastly exceeds a $15,000 ALE — the investment is economically unjustified. The correct response is to formally accept the risk (document it, notify risk owners) or find a proportionate control. This is a classic CISSP scenario testing whether candidates understand that risk elimination is not always the goal — cost-efficiency is. Residual risk after acceptance must still be tracked.
3A company's web application is running on an unpatched version of a web server software known to have a publicly documented buffer overflow flaw. No attack has been detected yet. In risk terminology, the unpatched software MOST accurately represents:
✅ Correct: C. A Vulnerability is a weakness or flaw in a system — unpatched software with a known flaw is the textbook definition. A Threat is the potential source of harm (e.g., a threat actor who could exploit it). A Threat Event (B) is the actual occurrence of an attack. Exposure (D) is a condition where vulnerability is exposed to threats but it's often used interchangeably with vulnerability in common usage — however, CISSP specifically distinguishes vulnerability as the weakness. Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Asset Value.
4During a risk assessment, an analyst calculates that a particular risk scores a 9 out of 10 — well above the organisation's defined risk appetite threshold of 6. The risk owner is a business unit manager. What is the MOST appropriate immediate action?
✅ Correct: B. Risk appetite defines the level of risk an organisation is willing to accept without additional treatment. When risk exceeds appetite, it must be escalated for formal senior management decision — not unilaterally handled by the analyst (no budget authority) or the business unit manager (whose authority to accept risk may be limited). Senior management is responsible for setting risk appetite and authorising exceptions. Only they can formally raise the risk tolerance threshold or commit organisational resources to treatment. This is the CISSP "manager mindset" — escalate rather than act unilaterally.
5The NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF) consists of seven steps. In the "Assess" step, what is the primary objective?
✅ Correct: C. The NIST RMF steps are: Prepare → Categorise → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorise → Monitor. The Assess step involves security control assessments (SCA) — an independent party tests whether the controls selected and implemented are in place, operating as intended, and producing the desired security outcomes. This produces a Security Assessment Report (SAR). Categorise (B) is Step 2; Select (A) is Step 3; Authorise (D) is Step 6. Confusing Assess with Authorise is a common exam mistake.
6An organisation lacks historical loss data and has limited security staff. It wants to perform a risk assessment that is primarily self-directed, driven by the organisation's own asset owners identifying risks, without requiring external security expertise or financial data inputs. Which risk assessment methodology MOST closely fits this profile?
✅ Correct: B. OCTAVE Allegro (Operationally Critical Threat, Asset, and Vulnerability Evaluation) is Carnegie Mellon's streamlined self-assessment methodology. It is designed to be performed by internal staff without deep security expertise, focusing on information assets and operational risk profiles. It uses workshops and worksheets — no financial modelling is required. FAIR (A) requires quantitative data. NIST 800-30 (C) is more comprehensive and typically uses independent assessors. ISO 31000 (D) is a principles framework, not a specific assessment method.
7An organisation wants a risk management framework applicable across all types of risk — not just information security — including financial, operational, strategic, and reputational risk. Which framework provides the MOST generic, universally applicable risk management principles and guidelines?
✅ Correct: B. ISO 31000:2018 is deliberately generic — it provides universal risk management principles, a framework, and a process applicable to any organisation (regardless of size, sector, or type) for any category of risk. It is not prescriptive about specific controls or assessment methods. NIST 800-37 (A) is specific to US federal information systems. COBIT (C) addresses IT governance. OCTAVE (D) is specific to information security operational risk. When the question says "all types of risk" or "universal applicability," ISO 31000 is the answer.
8An asset is valued at $1,000,000. A risk analyst implements a control that reduces the expected annual loss by 75%. What is the residual risk (remaining ALE) after control implementation, assuming original ALE was $1,000,000?
✅ Correct: B. Residual Risk = Total Risk × (1 − Control Effectiveness). With 75% reduction: $1,000,000 × (1 − 0.75) = $1,000,000 × 0.25 = $250,000. Option A ($750,000) is the risk mitigated (the "savings"), not the residual. Option C is incorrect because controls reduce but virtually never eliminate risk entirely — some residual risk always remains. This distinction is fundamental: risk management reduces risk to an acceptable residual level, it does not eliminate it.
9A company deploys a new intrusion detection system (IDS) to monitor its network. The IDS itself has a known web management interface vulnerability. The risk introduced by adding this security control is BEST described as:
✅ Correct: C. Secondary Risk (also called "risk of the risk response") is new risk that arises as a direct consequence of implementing a risk treatment. Deploying an IDS with its own vulnerability creates a new attack surface — that's secondary risk. This concept appears explicitly in PMBOK and risk management standards. It reinforces a key principle: risk responses must themselves be risk-assessed. Inherent risk (A) is pre-control baseline risk. Residual risk (B) is post-control remaining risk. Transferred risk (D) involves shifting risk to another party (e.g., insurance).
10After implementing all planned security controls, a CISO presents the board with a residual risk dashboard showing $200,000 in annualised risk exposure remains across several accepted risks. A board member asks whether they can simply implement more controls to bring this to zero. What is the MOST accurate response?
✅ Correct: B. A foundational principle of risk management is that risk cannot be fully eliminated — only reduced to an acceptable residual level. Zero-risk is neither achievable nor economically sensible. Additional controls have diminishing returns and introduce secondary risks of their own. Risk transfer (C) via insurance also leaves residual risk — insurers don't cover all scenarios, have deductibles, and don't cover reputational damage. The board's role is to accept the remaining residual risk after controls are deemed cost-effective — this is a governance decision, not a technical one.
