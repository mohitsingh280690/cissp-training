1. Legal Systems of the World

One of the most important (and often overlooked) realities for a security professional is that not everyone operates under the same legal system. When you work in a global organization or deal with international incidents, you need to understand which legal framework applies. There are four major legal systems in the world:

Legal System How Law is Made Where Used Example Relevance
Common Law Based on judicial precedent (court decisions over time). Judges interpret laws and their decisions become part of the law itself (case law / stare decisis). USA, UK, Canada, Australia, India Most US cybersecurity legal interpretations are rooted in common law. Prior court cases set the standard for data breach liability and negligence.
Civil Law (not the same as "civil" liability) Based on comprehensive written codes and statutes. Judges apply the code; they do not set precedent. The code is the ultimate authority. France, Germany, Spain, Japan, most of Latin America and Europe GDPR is enforced under civil law jurisdictions. Fines are applied based on the written regulation, not on prior judgments.
Religious Law Based on religious texts and doctrines. Applies to personal and sometimes commercial matters. Saudi Arabia, Iran (Sharia Law); Israel (aspects of Jewish law) Can affect how contracts and certain business practices are structured in those jurisdictions.
Customary Law Based on long-standing traditions and customs of a community. Often unwritten. Certain regions of Africa, Asia, and indigenous communities Rarely directly relevant to CISSP scenarios, but important to understand that these systems exist.
🔑 Key Concept — Common vs. Civil: Don't confuse Civil Law (the legal system) with Civil Law (the type of lawsuit). In the legal system context, Civil Law = a system based on written codes (used in France, Germany, etc.). In the lawsuit context, "civil" = a non-criminal lawsuit between private parties. Both terms appear on the CISSP exam. Context determines meaning.

2. Types of Law: Criminal, Civil, and Administrative

Within any legal system (particularly common law), there are distinct types of law. These govern different kinds of disputes and have different consequences. As a CISSP, you must know which type applies to a given scenario.

Criminal Law

Criminal Law: Governs actions that are harmful to society as a whole. The government (as the representative of society) prosecutes the accused. Conviction results in punishment: fines, imprisonment, or both.
  • Cases are brought by the government (e.g., United States v. John Doe)
  • Burden of proof: Beyond a reasonable doubt (highest standard)
  • Outcomes: Jail, prison, fines, probation
  • Cybersecurity examples: Hacking under the CFAA, ransomware attacks, identity theft, wiretapping

Civil Law (Tort Law)

Civil Law / Tort Law: Governs disputes between private parties (individuals or organizations). One party sues another for damages. No imprisonment — only financial compensation or injunctions.
  • Cases are brought by the harmed party (the plaintiff) against the defendant
  • Burden of proof: Preponderance of evidence — more likely than not (51%+)
  • Outcomes: Monetary damages, injunctions (court orders to stop/start an action)
  • Cybersecurity examples: Data breach lawsuits, negligence claims, breach of contract
  • Tort = a wrongful act (not a breach of contract) that causes harm to another party

Administrative / Regulatory Law

Administrative Law: Governs the activities of government agencies. Agencies like the FTC, SEC, or HHS create rules and can impose fines or sanctions for violations — without going through the full criminal/civil court process.
  • Cases are brought by a government agency against an organization or individual
  • Burden of proof: Varies, typically substantial evidence
  • Outcomes: Fines, license revocation, consent decrees, mandatory corrective actions
  • Cybersecurity examples: FTC enforcement actions for unfair data practices; HHS fines for HIPAA violations; SEC sanctions for failing to disclose breaches
💡 Exam Tip: A single cybersecurity incident can trigger all three types simultaneously. Example: A rogue employee who hacks a competitor's database may face:
  • Criminal prosecution by the government (CFAA violation)
  • Civil lawsuit by the competitor for damages
  • Administrative sanctions against both companies if data regulations were violated (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR)

3. Key Differences: Criminal vs. Civil Law

This comparison is a frequent exam topic. Know it cold.

Aspect Criminal Law Civil Law (Tort)
Who brings the case? The government (state or federal prosecutor) The harmed private party (plaintiff)
Burden of proof Beyond a reasonable doubt (~95%+ certainty) Preponderance of evidence (~51%+ certainty)
Possible outcomes Imprisonment, fines paid to government, death penalty Monetary damages paid to plaintiff, injunctions
Can defendant go to jail? Yes No
Standard example USA v. Kevin Mitnick (famous hacker prosecution) Company sues former employee for stealing trade secrets
Double jeopardy applies? Yes — cannot be tried twice for the same crime No — civil and criminal cases can both proceed (O.J. Simpson was acquitted criminally but found liable civilly)
🔑 Key Concept — OJ Simpson Rule: A person can be acquitted (found not guilty) of a criminal charge and still be found liable in a civil lawsuit for the same act. This is because the burden of proof is much lower in civil cases. This is directly relevant when a hacker is not convicted criminally but the victim organization sues for damages.

4. Liability, Due Care & Due Diligence (Legal View)

You learned about Due Care and Due Diligence as governance concepts on Day 2. Now let's look at them through a legal liability lens, which is how CISSP exam questions often frame them.

Legal Liability in Cybersecurity

Liability is legal responsibility for harm caused to another. An organization can be held liable if it:

  • Failed to implement reasonable security controls (failure of due care)
  • Failed to investigate known risks (failure of due diligence)
  • Knew about a vulnerability and failed to patch it
  • Allowed a foreseeable harm to occur through negligence

Negligence

Negligence: The failure to use reasonable care, resulting in harm to another. To prove negligence in court, four elements must be shown:
  1. Duty: The organization had a legal duty to protect (e.g., safeguard customer data)
  2. Breach: The organization breached that duty (e.g., did not encrypt data)
  3. Causation: The breach caused the harm (e.g., the breach directly led to data exposure)
  4. Damages: Actual harm occurred (e.g., financial loss, reputational damage)

Downstream Liability

An important concept for CISSP: Downstream liability occurs when your organization's compromised systems are used to attack others. Even if you were the victim, if your negligence allowed your systems to become an attack platform, you could be held liable for damage inflicted on third parties.

⚠️ Warning: Ignorance is not a defense. Courts do not accept "we didn't know" as an excuse if the risk was foreseeable and a reasonable organization would have taken precautions. This is why the "prudent person" rule and industry standards like NIST and ISO 27001 matter—they define what is "reasonable."

5. Jurisdiction Issues in Cybersecurity

Jurisdiction refers to the authority of a court or government to hear a case and enforce laws. In physical crimes, jurisdiction is usually clear (the crime happened in City X, therefore City X's courts handle it). Cybercrime breaks this model entirely.

Why Jurisdiction is Complex in Cyberspace

  • An attacker in Russia can compromise a US company's server hosted in Ireland, stealing data of EU citizens
  • Multiple countries may claim jurisdiction over the same event
  • Laws vary widely: What is illegal in one country may be legal or simply not prosecuted in another
  • Extradition treaties are inconsistent — many countries do not extradite their own citizens

Long-Arm Jurisdiction

Some jurisdictions use long-arm statutes to claim authority over foreign entities if their actions had effects within that jurisdiction. For example, a company based outside the EU may still be subject to GDPR if they process EU residents' data — this is GDPR's extraterritorial reach.

Practical Implications for Security Professionals

  • Understand which laws apply to your organization based on where you operate and where your customers are located
  • Data residency requirements: Some countries require that certain data stays within their borders
  • Incident response must consider cross-border legal requirements before accessing, copying, or sharing evidence
  • Law enforcement requests (e.g., FBI subpoenas, MLAT — Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties) are the formal mechanism for cross-border evidence sharing

💡 Exam Tip: The CISSP is US-centric and globally aware. When a question involves international incidents, the key considerations are: Where did the attack originate? Where was the victim? Where was the data? Which laws have extraterritorial reach? GDPR is the biggest example of extraterritorial law you must know.

6. Key Computer Crime Laws

The CISSP exam tests knowledge of landmark US federal computer crime laws. Know these by name, what they cover, and what makes them significant.

Law Year What It Covers
Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) 1986, amended multiple times The primary US federal anti-hacking law. Prohibits unauthorized access to "protected computers" (any computer connected to the internet). Covers hacking, DoS attacks, trafficking passwords, and fraud using computers. Both criminal and civil remedies.
Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) 1986 Extends government restrictions on wiretaps to include electronic data transmissions. Includes the Wiretap Act, Stored Communications Act (SCA), and Pen Register Act. Regulates government interception and access to stored electronic communications.
Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) 1998 Makes it illegal to circumvent digital rights management (DRM) technology. Protects online service providers from liability for users' copyright infringement (safe harbor provisions). Relevant to security research involving DRM-protected systems.
Cyber Intelligence Sharing and Protection Act (CISPA) / Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA) 2015 (CISA) Encourages sharing of cybersecurity threat information between private companies and the government. Provides liability protections for companies that share threat intelligence.
Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act 1998 Makes identity theft a federal crime. Covers using another person's identifying information to commit unlawful activities.
🔑 Key Concept — CFAA "Protected Computer": Under the CFAA, a "protected computer" broadly means any computer used in interstate or foreign commerce or communications—essentially any internet-connected device. This makes the CFAA extremely broad in scope. The debate over "unauthorized access" (e.g., exceeding authorized access by employees) has been a major legal controversy.

7. Key Takeaways

  • The four major legal systems: Common Law (USA/UK), Civil Law (France/Germany), Religious Law, Customary Law
  • Civil Law (the legal system) ≠ Civil Law (the lawsuit type) — context determines meaning
  • Criminal Law: Government prosecutes; burden = beyond a reasonable doubt; outcomes include jail
  • Civil/Tort Law: Private party sues; burden = preponderance of evidence; outcomes are monetary damages
  • Administrative Law: Agencies enforce regulations; outcomes include fines and sanctions
  • A single incident can trigger criminal, civil, and administrative proceedings simultaneously
  • Negligence requires: Duty + Breach + Causation + Damages
  • Jurisdiction in cybersecurity is complex — attacks cross borders; GDPR has extraterritorial reach
  • Key US laws: CFAA (anti-hacking), ECPA (wiretapping/stored comms), DMCA (copyright/DRM)
