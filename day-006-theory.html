⚠️ Exam Traps:
  • GDPR breach notification = 72 hours to supervisory authority; HIPAA = 60 days to HHS. Do NOT swap these.
  • GDPR applies to ANY organisation processing EU resident data — regardless of where the organisation is located.
  • Privacy ≠ Security. Privacy is about appropriate data use and individual rights; security is about protecting data from unauthorised access. Privacy requires security but extends well beyond it.

2. Real-World Organizational Relevance

The Privacy Explosion

Privacy regulation has exploded since GDPR took effect in May 2018. Today, 137+ countries have data privacy laws. Organisations face a patchwork of overlapping requirements depending on where they operate, where their customers are located, and what type of data they process.

The Business Case for Privacy

  • Fines: GDPR fines up to €20M or 4% of global annual revenue (whichever is higher). Amazon fined €746M in 2021. Meta fined €1.2B in 2023.
  • Reputation: Trust is a competitive differentiator. Privacy breaches destroy customer relationships even without regulatory fines.
  • M&A: Privacy compliance status is now a standard due diligence item in acquisitions. The Yahoo! breach (later attributed to 3 billion accounts) reduced its acquisition price by $350M.

Organizational Ownership

Privacy is governed by the Chief Privacy Officer (CPO) or Data Protection Officer (DPO) in collaboration with Legal, IT, and Security. GDPR requires a DPO for certain classes of organisations (e.g., public authorities, those doing large-scale profiling). The DPO must report to the highest management level and cannot be penalised for performing their duties.

4. GDPR – EU General Data Protection Regulation

The GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) is the gold standard of global privacy law. Effective May 25, 2018, it replaced the EU Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC). Its extraterritorial reach and severe penalties have made it the benchmark against which all other privacy laws are measured.

Who GDPR Applies To

  • Organisations established in the EU (regardless of where they process data)
  • Organisations outside the EU that offer goods/services to EU residents
  • Organisations outside the EU that monitor the behaviour of EU residents (e.g., tracking cookies, profiling)

Seven GDPR Privacy Principles (Article 5)

PrincipleMeaning
1. Lawfulness, Fairness & TransparencyProcessing must have a legal basis; individuals must be informed
2. Purpose LimitationData collected for specific purposes; cannot be reused for incompatible purposes
3. Data MinimisationCollect only what is necessary (adequate, relevant, limited)
4. AccuracyData must be accurate and kept up to date; erasure of inaccurate data
5. Storage LimitationRetain no longer than necessary for the stated purpose
6. Integrity & ConfidentialityAppropriate security measures (technical and organisational)
7. AccountabilityController is responsible and must be able to demonstrate compliance
🧠 GDPR 7 Principles: "Laughter Per Day Achieves Solid Integrating Accountability"
Lawful → Purpose limited → Data minimised → Accurate → Storage limited → Integrity/Confidentiality → Accountable

Lawful Bases for Processing (Article 6)

  1. Consent — freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous indication of agreement
  2. Contract — necessary for performance of or entering a contract with the data subject
  3. Legal obligation — necessary to comply with a legal obligation
  4. Vital interests — necessary to protect someone's life
  5. Public task — necessary for official/public interest functions
  6. Legitimate interests — necessary for legitimate interests of the controller/third party unless overridden by the data subject's rights

Data Subject Rights (Articles 12–22)

RightDescription
Right to be informedKnow that data is being collected and how it will be used (privacy notice)
Right of accessObtain confirmation of processing and a copy of data (Subject Access Request)
Right to rectificationCorrect inaccurate data
Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")Have data deleted under certain conditions
Right to restrict processingLimit processing in certain situations while dispute is resolved
Right to data portabilityReceive data in structured, machine-readable format; transmit to another controller
Right to objectObject to processing for direct marketing, research, or legitimate interests grounds
Rights re: automated decisionsNot be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects; request human review

Breach Notification (Articles 33–34)

  • To supervisory authority: Within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach that poses a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms
  • To data subjects: Without undue delay when breach is likely to result in high risk to the individual
  • Notification not required if data was encrypted and the key was not compromised (risk mitigated)

GDPR Penalties

  • Tier 1 (lower limit): Up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover — for breaches of processor obligations, technical measures, child protection
  • Tier 2 (upper limit): Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover — for breaches of core principles, data subject rights, international transfers

Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Required when: (1) a public authority, (2) large-scale systematic monitoring of individuals, or (3) large-scale special category data processing. The DPO must: advise on GDPR obligations, monitor compliance, cooperate with the supervisory authority, and be a point of contact. Cannot be dismissed or penalised for performing their role.

5. HIPAA – Health Privacy in the US

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996) governs the protection of health information in the United States. While covered in more detail on Day 3 (Compliance), the privacy aspects warrant deeper treatment here.

What Is PHI?

Protected Health Information (PHI) = any information that relates to:

  • The individual's past, present, or future physical or mental health condition
  • The provision of healthcare to the individual
  • The past, present, or future payment for healthcare

... AND can be used to identify the individual. The 18 HIPAA identifiers include: name, geographic data, dates, phone/fax numbers, email, SSN, medical record numbers, health plan numbers, account numbers, certificate/licence numbers, device IDs, URLs, IP addresses, biometric identifiers, photographs, and any unique identifying number or code.

HIPAA Key Rules

RuleWhat It Requires
Privacy RuleStandards for when PHI may be used or disclosed; patients' rights to their health information; limited to minimum necessary disclosure; requires Notice of Privacy Practices
Security RuleAdministrative, physical, and technical safeguards specifically for electronic PHI (ePHI); covers confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ePHI
Breach Notification RuleCovered entities must notify: affected individuals (within 60 days of discovery), HHS (within 60 days for breaches >500 individuals), and media if breach affects >500 people in a state
⚠️ HIPAA vs. GDPR Breach Notification:
HIPAA breach notification to HHS = 60 days
GDPR breach notification to supervisory authority = 72 hours
This is a very common exam trap. GDPR is much stricter.

Business Associates

HIPAA applies directly to Covered Entities (healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses) AND to Business Associates — any third party that creates, receives, maintains, or transmits PHI on behalf of a covered entity. Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) are mandatory contracts specifying how PHI must be protected. Failure to have a BAA is itself a HIPAA violation.

6. CCPA & CPRA – California Privacy Laws

California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) – 2020

California's CCPA is the most comprehensive state privacy law in the US, often called "the US version of GDPR" though it operates differently:

CCPA Consumer RightsDescription
Right to KnowWhat personal information is collected, how it's used, and who it's shared with
Right to DeleteRequest deletion of personal information (with exceptions)
Right to Opt-OutOpt out of sale of personal information (the famous "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" link)
Right to Non-DiscriminationCannot be denied service or charged different prices for exercising CCPA rights

California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) – 2023

CPRA amended and strengthened CCPA. Key additions:

  • New right to correct inaccurate personal information
  • New right to limit use of sensitive personal information
  • Expanded opt-out right to include sharing (not just selling) of data
  • Created California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) — dedicated enforcement authority
  • Additional requirements for automated decision-making and profiling
💡 CCPA Thresholds: CCPA applies to for-profit businesses that do business in California AND meet ANY ONE of: (1) $25M+ annual gross revenue, (2) buy/sell/share 100,000+ consumers' or households' personal data annually, or (3) derive 50%+ of revenue from selling personal information.

8. OECD Privacy Guidelines

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) Guidelines on the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data (1980, revised 2013) established eight foundational privacy principles that have influenced virtually every national privacy law. Understanding these is key to CISSP's systems-level view of privacy.

OECD PrincipleDescription
1. Collection LimitationData collection should be limited and obtained lawfully with knowledge/consent of the individual
2. Data QualityData should be relevant, accurate, complete, and current
3. Purpose SpecificationPurposes must be specified at time of collection
4. Use LimitationData should not be used for purposes other than specified except with consent or by authority of law
5. Security SafeguardsReasonable security measures against loss, unauthorised access, destruction, use, modification, or disclosure
6. OpennessGeneral policy of openness about practices and policies regarding personal data
7. Individual ParticipationIndividuals can inquire about their data, obtain access, and challenge accuracy
8. AccountabilityData controllers are accountable for complying with these principles
🧠 OECD 8 Principles: "Collection Quality, Purpose Use, Security Open, Individual Accountability"
Pairs: Collection + Quality | Purpose + Use | Security + Open | Individual + Accountability
Or: "CQ-PU-SO-IA" — think: "See Queue — Push — Show — IA (intelligent agent)"

9. Cross-Border Data Transfers

Moving personal data across borders (e.g., from EU to US) requires legal mechanisms because different countries have different protections. GDPR Chapter V governs transfers of personal data to third countries.

Transfer Mechanisms (GDPR)

MechanismStatusDescription
Adequacy DecisionAvailable for ~15 countriesEU Commission determines a country has "adequate" protection; data can flow freely. Current: UK, Japan, Canada, New Zealand, South Korea, etc.
Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)Active and most widely usedPre-approved contract clauses providing appropriate safeguards; must be supplemented by Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) after Schrems II
Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)For multinational groupsCompany-wide privacy policies approved by a supervisory authority; allows intra-group transfers
EU-US Data Privacy FrameworkActive since July 2023Replaced Privacy Shield (invalidated by Schrems II in 2020); provides adequacy for certified US organisations; includes US executive order on signals intelligence reform
⚠️ Privacy Shield History: The EU-US Privacy Shield was invalidated by the Court of Justice of the EU in Data Protection Commissioner v. Facebook Ireland (Schrems II) in July 2020, due to insufficient protection against US government surveillance. It was replaced by the EU-US Data Privacy Framework in 2023, though this too faces potential legal challenges.
Data Localisation: Some countries require certain data to remain within their borders (e.g., Russia, China, India's financial data). This creates architectural complexity for cloud deployments and must be factored into data residency decisions.

10. Privacy as a Program: Roles & Controls

Privacy by Design (PbD)

Developed by Ann Cavoukian, Privacy by Design incorporates privacy as a default component of system design rather than a bolt-on afterthought. GDPR Article 25 mandates Privacy by Design and Privacy by Default.

Seven PbD Foundational Principles:

  1. Proactive, not reactive; preventative, not remedial
  2. Privacy as the default setting
  3. Privacy embedded into design
  4. Full functionality — positive-sum, not zero-sum
  5. End-to-end security — full lifecycle protection
  6. Visibility and transparency
  7. Respect for user privacy — keep it user-centric

Privacy Controls Technical Techniques

TechniqueDescriptionUse Case
EncryptionRenders data unreadable without keyProtecting PII at rest and in transit
TokenisationReplace sensitive data with non-sensitive placeholder (token)Payment card data (PCI-DSS); reducing scope of compliance
Data MaskingObscure specific data within a datasetShow last 4 digits of a credit card; test data generation
PseudonymisationReplace identity with pseudonym; key held separatelyAnalytics and research while reducing direct identification risk
AnonymisationIrreversibly remove all identifiersExcludes data from GDPR scope if truly irreversible
Data MinimisationCollect and retain only required dataReduce attack surface; GDPR compliance

11. Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🧠 GDPR 7 Principles: Acronym "LPDA-SIA"
Lawful, fair, transparent → Purpose limitation → Data minimisation → Accuracy → Storage limitation → Integrity & Confidentiality → Accountability
Memory hook: "Lawyers Prefer Data About Stuff In Archives"
🧠 Breach Notification Timelines:
GDPR = 72 hours (to supervisory authority) — think: "GD-PR = 72 hours = 3 days"
HIPAA = 60 days (to HHS) — think: "HIPAA = Healthcare = 60 days — give hospitals time"
Visual: GDPR is much stricter (72 h vs 1,440 h)
🧠 GDPR Data Subject Rights: "I-A-R-E-R-P-O-A"
Informed → Access → Rectification → Erasure → Restriction → Portability → Object → Automated decision rights
Memory hook: "I Always Request Everything, Rarely Paying Or Appealing"
🧠 OECD 8 Principles in Pairs:
Collect Carefully → Collection Limitation + Data Quality
Purpose Properly → Purpose Specification + Use Limitation
Secure Openly → Security Safeguards + Openness
Individuals Are Accountable → Individual Participation + Accountability
⚠️ Privacy ≠ Security:
Security without privacy = You protect data from hackers but still misuse it yourself (data brokers selling behavioural data).
Privacy without security = You promise to be responsible with data but can't stop breaches.
True compliance requires BOTH. GDPR Article 5(1)(f) — Integrity & Confidentiality — explicitly ties security to privacy.
⚠️ GDPR Extraterritorial Reach:
A US company with no EU offices that runs a website, sells products to EU users, or uses tracking cookies on EU visitors IS subject to GDPR. "We're not in the EU" is not a defence.
