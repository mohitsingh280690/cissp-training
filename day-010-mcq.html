Domain 1 – Security and Risk Management

1A data centre has an asset value (AV) of $800,000. A risk analysis determines that a fire could destroy 30% of the asset value. What is the Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)?
✅ Correct: B. SLE = Asset Value (AV) × Exposure Factor (EF). EF is the percentage of asset value lost in a single occurrence. SLE = $800,000 × 0.30 = $240,000. The SLE represents the expected monetary loss from a single threat event. Option C ($560,000) is the residual asset value after the loss — not the SLE. EF must be expressed as a decimal (0.30, not 30) in the formula.
2Using the data from Question 1 (SLE = $240,000), the risk team estimates fires occur at the data centre an average of 3 times per year. What is the Annualised Loss Expectancy (ALE)?
✅ Correct: C. ALE = SLE × ARO (Annualised Rate of Occurrence). ALE = $240,000 × 3 = $720,000. ARO represents the estimated frequency of a threat event per year. An ARO of 3 means the event is expected to occur three times annually. ALE is the primary metric used for cost-benefit analysis of security controls — if a control costs less per year than the ALE it eliminates, it is economically justified (positive CBA).
3A security control costs $500,000 to implement. It will reduce the ALE from $720,000 to $270,000. Its annual maintenance cost is $400,000. What does the Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) tell you about implementing this control?
✅ Correct: C. CBA = ALE(before) − ALE(after) − Annual Cost of Control. ALE reduction = $720,000 − $270,000 = $450,000/year. The $500,000 implementation cost amortised over 5 years = $100,000/year. Annual maintenance = $400,000. Total annual cost = $500,000/year. Net CBA = $450,000 − $500,000 = −$50,000 — negative. The control costs more annually than the risk reduction it provides. Option B is wrong because it only considers maintenance ($400K < $450K) without amortised implementation. Option D is incorrect because CISSP CBA requires total cost to be less than risk reduction for economic justification.
4A company invested $50,000 in a security control. Before the control, ALE was $300,000. After implementation, ALE dropped to $180,000. What is the Return on Security Investment (ROSI)?
✅ Correct: A. ROSI = (ALE before − ALE after − Annual Cost of Control) / Annual Cost of Control × 100%. = ($300,000 − $180,000 − $50,000) / $50,000 × 100% = $70,000 / $50,000 × 100% = 140%. A ROSI > 0% means the control produces positive return. A ROSI of 140% means for every $1 spent on the control, the organisation gets $2.40 back in avoided losses (net profit of $1.40 per dollar invested).
5A risk analyst knows the ALE for a ransomware risk is $90,000 per year and the ARO is 0.5 (once every two years). What is the Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)?
✅ Correct: B. The formula ALE = SLE × ARO can be rearranged to SLE = ALE / ARO. SLE = $90,000 / 0.5 = $180,000. An ARO of 0.5 means the event occurs once every two years. The SLE of $180,000 represents the cost of a single occurrence — which makes sense because if it happens once every two years and costs $180K each time, the average annual loss is $90K. This reverse-formula calculation tests conceptual understanding beyond simple forward calculation.
6Two controls are being evaluated. Control A costs $40,000/year and reduces ALE by $100,000 (ROSI: 150%). Control B costs $120,000/year and reduces ALE by $160,000 (ROSI: 33%). Budget allows implementing only one. Which should be selected and why?
✅ Correct: C. When comparing controls under budget constraints, ROSI (efficiency of investment) is the superior metric. Control A returns $1.50 for every $1 invested; Control B returns only $0.33 per $1 invested. While Control B has a larger absolute ALE reduction, it requires 3× the budget for only 1.6× the benefit — a poor use of limited resources. ROSI allows normalised comparison of controls regardless of their total costs. This is a core quantitative risk analysis skill: always evaluate efficiency (ROSI), not just absolute benefit.
7An asset is valued at $500,000. The Exposure Factor (EF) for a flood scenario is 40%. Historical data shows flooding impacts this asset approximately once every 4 years. What is the ALE?
✅ Correct: A. Step 1: SLE = AV × EF = $500,000 × 0.40 = $200,000. Step 2: ARO = once every 4 years = 0.25/year. Step 3: ALE = SLE × ARO = $200,000 × 0.25 = $50,000/year. Option B ($200,000) is the SLE — only half the calculation. A multi-step calculation like this is a common CISSP exam format — candidates must apply both formulas in sequence. "Once every 4 years" = ARO of 0.25 (1/4).
8A risk analyst is asked to perform quantitative risk analysis but struggles because historical breach data for the organisation's specific industry sector is not publicly available. This highlights which fundamental limitation of quantitative risk analysis?
✅ Correct: B. The primary limitation of quantitative risk analysis is data dependency — it requires accurate historical frequency data (for ARO) and reliable impact data (for EF/SLE). Cybersecurity incidents are often novel, underreported, and highly variable across organisations. Without credible data, the mathematical precision of quantitative analysis may create a false sense of accuracy ("garbage in, garbage out"). This is why qualitative analysis is often used as a starting point when data is unavailable, and why FAIR requires careful sourcing of input distributions.
9In quantitative risk analysis, an Exposure Factor (EF) of 100% means:
✅ Correct: B. The Exposure Factor (EF) is the percentage of asset value expected to be lost in a single threat event. EF = 100% means a single event results in total loss of the asset (SLE = AV × 1.0 = AV). A fire that completely destroys a building is an example of EF approaching 100%. EF is independent of ARO (frequency) — a catastrophic but rare event (high EF, low ARO) can have a lower ALE than a frequent, minor event (low EF, high ARO). Candidates commonly confuse EF with probability of occurrence.
10A quantitative CBA shows that implementing a DLP solution produces a net positive return of $5,000/year after subtracting all costs. A colleague argues this marginal benefit does not justify the operational burden of DLP. What is the MOST complete and correct assessment approach?
✅ Correct: D. Quantitative CBA provides one important data point but should not be the sole decision criterion. Non-financial factors — regulatory compliance obligations (GDPR, PCI-DSS), reputational exposure from a data breach, competitive intelligence protection, contractual requirements — may independently justify a control even if the pure financial return is marginal. CISSP candidates must understand that security decisions are ultimately business decisions integrating both quantitative data and qualitative judgement. A $5K positive CBA might actually understate true value if intangible benefits aren't captured.
